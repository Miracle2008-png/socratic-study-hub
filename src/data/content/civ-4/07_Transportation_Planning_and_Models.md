# Transportation Planning and Models

## Introduction
Transportation planning is a macro-level engineering and policy discipline focused on forecasting future travel demand and designing multi-modal infrastructure to meet that demand safely, efficiently, and equitably. As urban populations expand, traffic congestion becomes a severe constraint on economic productivity and quality of life. The planner's goal is not merely to build wider roads, but to optimize the entire network, integrating transit, non-motorized infrastructure, and land-use policies.

The cornerstone of modern transportation planning is the **Urban Transportation Modeling System (UTMS)**, universally known as the **Four-Step Travel Demand Model**. This sequential mathematical framework processes demographic, economic, and spatial data to predict how many vehicles or transit riders will utilize a specific transport corridor decades into the future. 

## The Four-Step Model
To run the model, a geographical region is first subdivided into Traffic Analysis Zones (TAZs).
### 1. Trip Generation
This step answers the question: *How many trips originate from or are destined for a specific TAZ?*
It relies heavily on land use and socioeconomics. A residential suburb generates "trip productions" based on household size and vehicle ownership, while a downtown commercial district generates "trip attractions" based on retail square footage and employment numbers.

### 2. Trip Distribution
This step answers: *Where do the trips go?*
It links the productions in one TAZ to the attractions in another, creating a trip table. The most common method is the Gravity Model, which assumes that the number of trips between two zones is directly proportional to their size (attractiveness) and inversely proportional to the spatial impedance (travel time or cost) between them.

### 3. Mode Choice
This step answers: *How will people travel?*
Will the trip be taken by a personal automobile, public transit, or bicycle? This is modeled using discrete choice theory (often Logit Models), evaluating the "utility" of each mode based on factors like out-of-pocket cost, in-vehicle travel time, wait time, and convenience.

### 4. Traffic Assignment
This step answers: *Which route will they take?*
The assigned trips are loaded onto the mathematical representation of the physical road or transit network. As more vehicles are assigned to a route, congestion increases, travel time rises, and the model dynamically shifts subsequent vehicles to alternative paths until "user equilibrium" is reached—a state where no driver can reduce their travel time by unilaterally changing routes.

## Detailed Mathematical Examples

### 1. Trip Generation (Linear Regression)
Linear regression models predict the number of trips produced by a household based on specific characteristics.

**Problem:** A transportation agency uses the following regression model to predict the number of daily home-based work trips ($T$) produced by a household:
$$ T = 0.35 + 1.2 \times (W) + 0.5 \times (V) $$
Where $W$ is the number of workers in the household, and $V$ is the number of vehicles available. Calculate the total daily trips produced by a TAZ containing 800 households, where 60% of households have 1 worker and 1 vehicle, and 40% have 2 workers and 2 vehicles.

**Solution:**
Calculate the trips for Household Type 1 (1 worker, 1 vehicle):
$$ T_1 = 0.35 + 1.2(1) + 0.5(1) = 2.05 \text{ trips/household} $$
Total for Type 1 = $0.60 \times 800 \times 2.05 = 480 \times 2.05 = 984 \text{ trips}$

Calculate the trips for Household Type 2 (2 workers, 2 vehicles):
$$ T_2 = 0.35 + 1.2(2) + 0.5(2) = 0.35 + 2.4 + 1.0 = 3.75 \text{ trips/household} $$
Total for Type 2 = $0.40 \times 800 \times 3.75 = 320 \times 3.75 = 1,200 \text{ trips}$

Total Trips Generated by the TAZ:
$$ T_{total} = 984 + 1,200 = 2,184 \text{ trips/day} $$

### 2. Trip Distribution (Gravity Model)
The Gravity Model distributes trips from an origin zone ($i$) to various destination zones ($j$).

**Problem:** Zone A produces 1,000 trips ($P_i = 1000$). There are two potential destination zones, B and C. Zone B has 500 attractions ($A_B = 500$) with a friction factor ($F_{AB}$) of 0.8. Zone C has 800 attractions ($A_C = 800$) with a friction factor ($F_{AC}$) of 0.4. Assume socioeconomic adjustment factors ($K_{ij}$) are 1.0. Calculate the number of trips distributed from Zone A to Zone B ($T_{AB}$) and from Zone A to Zone C ($T_{AC}$).

**Solution:**
The Gravity Model equation is:
$$
T_{ij} = P_i \frac{A_j F_{ij} K_{ij}}{\sum (A_j F_{ij} K_{ij})}
$$

Calculate the denominator (the sum of attractiveness $\times$ friction for all destinations):
$$
\sum (A_j F_{ij}) = (A_B F_{AB}) + (A_C F_{AC})
$$
$$
\sum = (500 \times 0.8) + (800 \times 0.4) = 400 + 320 = 720
$$

Calculate trips to Zone B:
$$
T_{AB} = 1000 \times \frac{400}{720} = 1000 \times 0.5556 = 555.6 \approx 556 \text{ trips}
$$

Calculate trips to Zone C:
$$
T_{AC} = 1000 \times \frac{320}{720} = 1000 \times 0.4444 = 444.4 \approx 444 \text{ trips}
$$
(Note: $556 + 444 = 1000$ trips distributed perfectly).

### 3. Mode Choice (Multinomial Logit Model)
The logit model determines the probability of choosing a specific mode based on its utility compared to all other modes.

**Problem:** A commuter has two choices for traveling to work: Car ($C$) or Transit ($T$). The utility functions are determined to be $U_C = -1.2$ and $U_T = -1.8$. Calculate the probability that the commuter will choose Transit.

**Solution:**
The multinomial logit model equation is:
$$
P_k = \frac{e^{U_k}}{\sum e^{U_i}}
$$

Calculate the exponential utilities:
$$
e^{U_C} = e^{-1.2} = 0.3012
$$
$$
e^{U_T} = e^{-1.8} = 0.1653
$$

Calculate the denominator (sum of all exponentials):
$$
\sum e^{U_i} = 0.3012 + 0.1653 = 0.4665
$$

Calculate the probability of choosing Transit ($P_T$):
$$
P_T = \frac{0.1653}{0.4665} = 0.3543
$$

There is a $35.43\%$ probability the commuter will choose transit (and a $64.57\%$ probability they will drive).
