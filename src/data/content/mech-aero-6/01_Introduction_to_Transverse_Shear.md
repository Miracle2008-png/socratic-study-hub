# Introduction to Transverse Shear

In our previous discussion of bending, we focused exclusively on the normal stresses ($\sigma$) caused by the internal bending moment $M$. However, equilibrium of a beam subjected to transverse loading also requires an internal transverse shear force $V$.

This internal shear force creates **transverse shear stresses** ($\tau$) on the cross-section of the beam. Understanding and predicting the distribution of these shear stresses is critical, especially for short, deep beams where shear failure can occur before bending failure.

## 1. The Nature of Transverse Shear

When a transverse load pushes down on a beam, it attempts to slice the beam vertically, exactly like a pair of scissors. The material resists this slicing action by developing internal shear stresses directed parallel to the applied load.

However, the distribution of this shear stress across the depth of the beam is highly non-uniform. Unlike normal stress in bending, which is maximum at the outer surfaces, transverse shear stress is typically **zero at the outer surfaces** and reaches its absolute **maximum at the Neutral Axis**.

## 2. Complementary Longitudinal Shear

One of the most profound principles of mechanics is that a shear stress acting on a specific plane *must* be accompanied by an equal shear stress acting on a perpendicular plane to maintain rotational equilibrium of a microscopic element.

Therefore, the transverse vertical shear stresses acting on the cross-section of the beam must be accompanied by equal **longitudinal shear stresses** acting along horizontal planes running the length of the beam.

## 3. The Sliding Planks Analogy

The concept of longitudinal shear is easily visualized. 
Imagine a beam made by simply stacking several loose wooden planks on top of one another, without gluing them together.

If you place supports at the ends and push down in the middle, the beam will sag. As it sags, the loose planks will slide past each other longitudinally. The bottom edge of the top plank will slide outward relative to the top edge of the plank beneath it.

If you now drive large bolts vertically through the stack of planks, securely fastening them together, you prevent them from sliding. The beam becomes massively stiffer and stronger. 
The bolts are now subjected to intense longitudinal shear forces. In a solid, continuous beam, the material itself must provide this resistance. It is precisely this longitudinal shear stress that holds the layers of a beam together during bending.

## 4. The Shear Formula Objective

Our primary objective in this topic is to derive a mathematical relationship that predicts the intensity of this transverse (and complementary longitudinal) shear stress at any specific point within the cross-section of a loaded beam.
