# Shear Flow in Thin-Walled Members

In aerospace and lightweight structural engineering, beams are often constructed from very thin sheets of metal bent into specific shapes (channels, angles, I-sections). These are **thin-walled members**.

Because the walls are so incredibly thin, we make a fundamental assumption: the shear stress acts uniformly across the thickness of the wall, and the "flow" of the shear stress is directed purely parallel to the centerline of the wall itself.

## 1. Visualizing Shear Flow

Imagine the shear stress in a thin-walled member as a fluid flowing through a network of pipes. The fluid enters the top of the cross-section, flows through the flanges, down the web, and exits at the bottom.

The intensity of this fluid flow at any point is given by the shear flow equation:
$$ q = \frac{V Q}{I} $$

As you move from the free outer edge of a flange inward toward the web, the area $A^\prime$ (used to calculate $Q$) continuously increases. Therefore, the shear flow $q$ starts at exactly zero at the free edge and continuously builds up like water accumulating in a gutter.

## 2. Shear Flow in an I-Beam Flange

Let's trace the shear flow in the top flange of an I-beam subjected to a downward shear force $V$.

1.  Start at the far right edge of the top flange. $Q = 0$, so $q = 0$.
2.  Move leftward toward the web. The area $A^\prime$ grows linearly, but the centroidal distance $\bar{y}^\prime$ remains constant. Therefore, $Q$ grows linearly.
3.  The shear flow $q$ in the flange is a straight, linear function. The fluid is flowing horizontally inward from the right edge toward the center web.
4.  Simultaneously, fluid is flowing horizontally inward from the far left edge of the top flange toward the center web.
5.  These two horizontal flows meet at the junction of the web and combine, turning 90 degrees to flow massively downward through the vertical web.

## 3. Direction of Shear Flow

Determining the direction of the shear flow arrows is critical. The rule is simple: **The shear flow in the web must act in the same direction as the applied external shear force $V$.**

If $V$ points downward:
*   The shear flow in the web points straight down.
*   To feed this downward flow, the shear flow in the top flanges must point inward (toward the web).
*   To exhaust this downward flow, the shear flow in the bottom flanges must point outward (away from the web).

If you sum the vertical forces generated by the shear flow in the web ($\int q_{web} dy$), it will exactly equal the applied external shear force $V$.
If you sum the horizontal forces generated by the shear flow in the flanges ($\int q_{flange} dx$), the inward flow on the left perfectly cancels the inward flow on the right. The net horizontal force is zero, satisfying equilibrium.
