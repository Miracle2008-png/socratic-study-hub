# Gas Gathering Systems and Pipelines

Once the natural gas violently exits the wellhead choke, its journey has just begun. 

A single gas well rarely produces enough volume to justify building a massive chemical processing plant or a 500-mile cross-country transmission pipeline. 
Instead, dozens, or even hundreds, of individual gas wells are drilled across a massive geographic area (a gas field). The gas from all these widely scattered wells must be physically collected and routed to a single, central location.

This massive, complex web of surface pipes is the **Gas Gathering System**.

## 1. The Architecture of a Gathering System

A gathering system looks exactly like the root system of a massive oak tree or the capillaries in the human body.

1.  **Flowlines (The Capillaries):** A small, 2-inch to 4-inch diameter steel or plastic pipe connects directly to the wellhead of a single well. It carries the raw, wet, high-pressure gas from that specific well across the desert.
2.  **Manifolds (The Branches):** Five or six flowlines from nearby wells all converge at a central steel manifold. The gas streams merge together into a larger pipe.
3.  **Trunklines (The Roots):** The massive, 12-inch to 24-inch diameter main arteries. All the branches feed into the trunkline. The trunkline carries the combined, massive volume of gas from all 100 wells directly to the Central Gas Processing Plant.

## 2. The Nightmare of Two-Phase Flow

Designing a gas gathering system is exponentially more difficult than designing a water pipeline. Why? Because the gas exiting the well is rarely dry. 

As the hot, high-pressure gas travels through miles of steel pipe laid on the cold ground, the gas cools down and the pressure drops.
This causes two distinct liquids to violently condense out of the gas stream directly inside the pipe:
*   Liquid Water.
*   Liquid Natural Gas Liquids (NGLs / Condensate).

The gathering line is no longer carrying a simple gas. It is carrying a chaotic, high-velocity, two-phase mixture of gas and liquid.

### The Physics of Pipeline Liquid
1.  **Liquid Holdup:** The gas is traveling incredibly fast (e.g., 20 mph). The liquid oil and water are heavy and drag along the bottom of the pipe very slowly (e.g., 2 mph). Because the liquid moves slower, it begins to back up and accumulate inside the pipe. This is called "Liquid Holdup."
2.  **Friction and Choking:** As the liquid pool deepens, it physically chokes off the diameter of the pipe. The gas is forced to squeeze through a smaller opening, which causes massive frictional pressure losses. The pressure drops so severely that the wells upstream might die.
3.  **Terrain Slugging:** Pipelines follow the natural hills and valleys of the earth. Liquid naturally drains into the low valleys. A massive pool of liquid water blocks the bottom of a hill. The high-pressure gas builds up behind the water until it violently blasts the massive slug of water up the hill. This multi-ton "slug" of water races down the pipeline at 30 mph, violently smashing into the valves at the gas plant, causing catastrophic mechanical damage.

## 3. Managing the Liquid (Pigging and Drip Legs)

To survive two-phase flow, the gathering system engineer must constantly remove the liquids from the pipe.

### Pigging
A "Pig" is a massive, bullet-shaped rubber or urethane cylinder. 
*   The engineer inserts the pig into a "Launcher" barrel at the beginning of the trunkline.
*   The high-pressure flowing gas gets trapped behind the pig, violently pushing it down the 20-mile pipeline. 
*   The pig acts like a massive squeegee. It physically pushes the thousands of gallons of condensed water and oil lying on the bottom of the pipe ahead of it.
*   At the gas plant, a massive "Slug Catcher" (a giant set of oversized steel pipes) receives the violent slug of liquid pushed by the pig, safely slowing it down and separating the liquid from the gas.

### Drip Legs
In low spots (valleys) where water naturally accumulates, engineers install Drip Legs. A small pipe is welded to the bottom of the main pipeline. Gravity pulls the liquid down into the drip leg. An automated valve senses when the leg is full and automatically dumps the water into a small storage tank, preventing a massive slug from ever forming in the main pipe.

## 4. Field Compression

As the gas field matures over 20 years, the natural pressure of the underground rock depletes. 
Eventually, the pressure of the gas at the wellhead (e.g., 200 psi) is too weak to push its way into the massive trunkline (which operates at 500 psi). 

To keep the field alive, the company must install massive **Field Compressors**. 
These massive, natural-gas-powered V12 engines are bolted to the dirt near the manifolds. They suck the low-pressure gas out of the dying wells, violently compress it to 500 psi, and blast it into the trunkline, artificially pulling millions of cubic feet of gas out of the ground that would otherwise be permanently trapped.
