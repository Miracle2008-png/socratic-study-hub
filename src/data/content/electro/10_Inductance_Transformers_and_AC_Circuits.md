# Inductance, Transformers, and AC Circuits

Faraday's law tells us that a changing magnetic flux induces a voltage. 
What happens when a coil of wire creates a magnetic field that changes its *own* flux?

## 1. Self-Inductance ($L$)

When you run a current through a solenoid (a coil of wire), it creates a magnetic field down its center. 
If you suddenly increase the current, the magnetic field increases. The coil experiences an increasing magnetic flux passing through its own loops. 
According to Faraday and Lenz's Law, the coil must immediately induce a "back-voltage" to fight this change and stop the current from increasing!

This property is called **Self-Inductance ($L$)**, or simply Inductance. It is electrical inertia. An inductor fights any change in current.

$$ \mathcal{E}_L = -L \frac{dI}{dt} $$

*   **Units:** The Henry (H).
*   If the current is steady (DC), $\frac{dI}{dt} = 0$, the inductor does nothing and acts like a normal piece of wire.
*   If you try to cut the circuit suddenly, the inductor will induce a massive forward voltage spike (thousands of volts) to try and force the current to keep flowing, often resulting in a visible spark across the switch.

Just like a capacitor stores energy in an electric field, an **inductor stores energy in a magnetic field**.
$$ U_B = \frac{1}{2} L I^2 $$

## 2. Mutual Inductance and Transformers

If you place two separate coils of wire very close to each other, the magnetic field generated by Coil 1 will pierce through the loops of Coil 2.

If you run an **Alternating Current (AC)** through Coil 1, it creates a constantly fluctuating magnetic field. This fluctuating field passes through Coil 2, constantly changing its flux, which induces an alternating voltage in Coil 2. Electricity is transferred across empty space! This is **Mutual Inductance**.

This is the principle of the **Transformer**. By wrapping both coils around a shared iron core to channel the magnetic field perfectly without leaks, we can manipulate voltage.

The ratio of the voltages is perfectly proportional to the ratio of the number of turns ($N$) in the coils:
$$ \frac{V_{\text{Secondary}}}{V_{\text{Primary}}} = \frac{N_{\text{Secondary}}}{N_{\text{Primary}}} $$

*   **Step-Up Transformer:** If the secondary coil has more turns, the voltage is amplified. 
*   **Step-Down Transformer:** If the secondary has fewer turns, the voltage drops.

Because energy is conserved (Power $P = IV$), if you step up the voltage by a factor of 10, the current must drop by a factor of 10. Transformers are the sole reason our power grids use AC electricity; it allows us to step voltage up to 500,000 V for efficient cross-country transmission, and step it down to a safe 120 V at your house. This cannot easily be done with DC.

## 3. AC Circuits and Reactance

In an Alternating Current (AC) circuit, the voltage is a sine wave: $V(t) = V_{\text{max}} \sin(\omega t)$.
Because the voltage and current are constantly changing, Capacitors and Inductors behave very differently than they do in DC.

Instead of Resistance ($R$), we use **Reactance ($X$)** to describe how much they resist the AC current.
*   **Inductive Reactance ($X_L = \omega L$):** Inductors hate change. At very high frequencies ($\omega$), the current is changing violently, so the inductor fights back brutally, offering massive resistance. At low frequencies, it does nothing.
*   **Capacitive Reactance ($X_C = \frac{1}{\omega C}$):** Capacitors hate steady states. At high frequencies, the capacitor is constantly charging and discharging rapidly, acting almost like a solid wire (low resistance). At low frequencies, it has time to fill up and block the current entirely.

## 4. RLC Resonance

If you put a Resistor, Inductor, and Capacitor together in a series AC circuit, the total "resistance" is called **Impedance ($Z$)**.
Because inductors and capacitors throw the voltage and current out of phase in opposite directions, their reactances actually cancel each other out:
$$ Z = \sqrt{R^2 + (X_L - X_C)^2} $$

There is exactly one magical frequency where the inductive reactance perfectly equals the capacitive reactance ($X_L = X_C$).
At this frequency, the term $(X_L - X_C)$ becomes zero. The impedance drops to its absolute minimum (just $R$), and a massive surge of current flows through the circuit.

This is called the **Resonant Frequency ($\omega_0$)**:
$$ \omega_0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}} $$

This is how radios work. When you turn the dial on a radio, you are physically changing the capacitance ($C$) of the circuit inside. When you tune it so that the circuit's resonant frequency perfectly matches the frequency of the incoming radio wave, the circuit resonates, amplifying that specific station and ignoring all the other radio waves filling the air!
