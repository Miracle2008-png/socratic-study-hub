# Lifting-Line Theory (Prandtl)

Thin Airfoil Theory solves for the lift of a 2D, infinitely long wing profile. To calculate the lift and induced drag of a real, 3D wing with a finite span (including wingtips), Ludwig Prandtl developed **Lifting-Line Theory** in 1918.

## 1. The Horseshoe Vortex Model

Prandtl modeled the entire 3D wing mathematically as a single "lifting line" spanning from tip to tip. 
According to Helmholtz's theorems, a vortex filament cannot simply end in thin air; it must form a closed loop or extend to infinity. 
Therefore, Prandtl proposed that the bound vortex generating lift on the wing turns 90 degrees at the wingtips and trails backward into infinity. This creates a giant "Horseshoe Vortex."

In reality, lift varies across the wing (maximum at the root, zero at the tips). To model this, Prandtl stacked an infinite number of these horseshoe vortices on top of each other, each with infinitesimal strength. This forms a continuous **trailing vortex sheet** behind the wing.

## 2. Induced Downwash

The infinite sheet of trailing vortices trailing behind the airplane continuously pushes air downward. Using the Biot-Savart law (borrowed from electromagnetism), Prandtl calculated the downward velocity (downwash, $w$) induced precisely at the wing itself by its own wake.

This downwash alters the local relative wind, reducing the effective angle of attack:
$$\alpha_{eff}(y) = \alpha_{geom} - \alpha_{induced}(y)$$

Because the downwash $w(y)$ varies along the span of the wing, the true lift generated by each local section of the wing must be calculated iteratively.

## 3. The Elliptical Lift Distribution

Prandtl asked a profound optimization question: *What shape should the wing be to guarantee the absolute minimum induced drag for a given amount of total lift?*

By solving the massive integral equations, he found that induced drag is minimized when the downwash $w(y)$ is perfectly constant across the entire wingspan. 
A constant downwash requires a specific, mathematically ideal distribution of lift across the span: an **Elliptical Lift Distribution**.

To achieve this ideal elliptical lift loading, the physical chord length of the wing must vary elliptically from root to tip.
The **Supermarine Spitfire** from WWII is famous for utilizing exactly this perfect, highly efficient elliptical wing shape.

## 4. Fundamental Results for a 3D Wing

For an ideal elliptical wing, Prandtl derived exactly how much the 3D geometry hurts aerodynamic performance compared to the 2D ideal.

**1. Reduced Lift Slope:**
Because of the downwash, a 3D wing generates less lift at the same angle of attack than a 2D wing. The lift slope is reduced from $2\pi$:
$$a_{3D} = \frac{a_0}{1 + \frac{a_0}{\pi AR}}$$
*(Where $a_0 = 2\pi$ is the 2D slope, and $AR$ is the aspect ratio).*

**2. Induced Drag:**
The absolute minimum possible induced drag for any wing is given by the elliptical formula:
$$C_{D,i} = \frac{C_L^2}{\pi AR}$$

If a wing is not a perfect ellipse (e.g., a simple, cheap rectangular wing), the induced drag will be higher. We insert an Oswald efficiency factor ($e \le 1$) into the denominator to account for the suboptimal shape: $C_{D,i} = C_L^2 / (\pi e AR)$.

These two simple algebraic equations completely revolutionized aircraft design, allowing engineers to quantitatively trade off wing span against structural weight and aerodynamic drag.
