# Directional and Horizontal Drilling

Historically, oil wells were drilled exactly like water wells: straight down. 

While drilling a perfectly vertical hole is easy, it is highly inefficient for extracting oil. Most sedimentary rock layers (and oil reservoirs) are laid down horizontally, like the filling in a massive sandwich. 
If you drill straight down, your vertical wellbore might only pass through 50 feet of the oil reservoir before it punches out the bottom. You are only exposing 50 feet of rock to the well.

**Directional Drilling** is the technology that allows drillers to intentionally steer the drill bit miles away from the surface location. **Horizontal Drilling** is the extreme application of this, where the well is drilled down to depth, and then turned exactly 90 degrees to drill completely sideways, staying perfectly inside the 50-foot thick oil layer for three miles.

## 1. Why Drill Directionally?

1.  **Massive Reservoir Contact:** A vertical well touches 50 feet of oil rock. A horizontal well can punch horizontally through 10,000 feet of the exact same oil rock. The horizontal well produces 10 times as much oil, turning unprofitable reservoirs into goldmines.
2.  **Inaccessible Surface Locations:** If an oil field sits directly underneath a city, a mountain, or a protected national park, you cannot build a drilling rig on top of it. You build the rig 5 miles away in an empty field, drill down, and steer the bit perfectly sideways until it is underneath the city.
3.  **Offshore Platforms:** An offshore deep-water rig costs \$1 billion. You cannot afford to build one platform for every single well. Instead, you build one massive platform, and drill 40 different directional wells radially outward from it in all directions, draining an area the size of a city from a single point on the ocean surface.

## 2. How Do You Steer a Steel Pipe?

If you have a perfectly straight, rigid steel drill pipe rotating from the surface, how do you make the bit turn a corner 2 miles underground?

### The Mud Motor and the Bent Sub
The foundation of traditional directional drilling is the **Mud Motor** (a Positive Displacement Motor, PDM).
*   Normally, the entire 10,000-foot drill string is rotated from the surface. 
*   A Mud Motor is a specialized turbine placed at the very bottom of the drill string, directly above the drill bit. 
*   As high-pressure drilling mud is pumped down the hollow pipe, the mud forces the internal rotor of the motor to spin rapidly. This spins the drill bit without requiring the entire 10,000 feet of pipe above it to rotate.

**The "Bent Sub":**
Immediately above the mud motor, the steel pipe is manufactured with a permanent, slight physical kink in it (usually a 1.5-degree bend).
*   **Rotary Drilling (Going Straight):** If the rig rotates the *entire* drill string from the surface, the bent sub flops around in a massive circle, and the bit drills a slightly oversized, but perfectly straight hole.
*   **Sliding (Steering a Corner):** When the driller wants to turn right, they stop rotating the drill string from the surface. They turn the pipe until the physical 1.5-degree "kink" is pointing exactly to the right. They lock the pipe in place. Then, they pump mud. The mud motor spins *only* the bit. Because the pipe is kinked to the right, the bit aggressively carves a curved path to the right. The entire non-rotating drill string slides forward behind it.

## 3. Rotary Steerable Systems (RSS)

"Sliding" with a mud motor is incredibly slow and causes massive friction, because pushing 10,000 feet of non-rotating steel pipe through a horizontal hole creates immense drag.

Modern horizontal wells use **Rotary Steerable Systems (RSS)**.
This is a computerized, high-tech robot placed directly behind the drill bit.
*   The entire drill string rotates continuously from the surface (zero friction, extremely fast drilling).
*   Inside the RSS tool, three massive hydraulic pads continuously push outward against the rock wall. 
*   As the tool spins at 150 RPM, a computer precisely pulses the hydraulic pads, pushing against the wall at the exact millisecond needed to forcefully nudge the drill bit in the desired direction. 
*   It allows drillers to steer the wellbore with surgical precision in 3D space while drilling at maximum speed.

## 4. Measurement While Drilling (MWD)

To steer to a target 3 miles away, you must know exactly where the bit is in 3D space at all times.

Because the bit is miles underground, you cannot use GPS (radio waves do not travel through rock).
The driller uses **Measurement While Drilling (MWD)**. 
A highly advanced electronic package is placed behind the drill bit. It contains gyroscopes and accelerometers (identical to those used in ballistic missiles) to measure the exact inclination (tilt) and azimuth (compass direction) of the bit.

**Mud Pulse Telemetry:**
How does the MWD tool transmit this data to the surface computer without a wire?
It uses the drilling mud itself. A mechanical valve inside the tool rapidly opens and closes, creating a series of high-pressure shockwaves (binary morse code) in the column of mud flowing down the pipe. Pressure sensors on the surface detect these subtle pulses and decode them, providing the driller with real-time updates on exactly where the bit is headed.
