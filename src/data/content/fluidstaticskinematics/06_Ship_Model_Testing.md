# Ship Model Testing (Froude Scaling)

Naval architecture relies heavily on scale model testing in massive indoor swimming pools called "towing tanks." Designing ships presents a unique scaling problem: a ship moving at the surface of the ocean generates waves (which depend on gravity) and experiences skin friction (which depends on viscosity).

## 1. The Conflicting Scaling Laws

The total resistance (drag) of a ship is the sum of two primary components:
$$ R_{total} = R_{friction} + R_{wave} $$

1.  **Friction Resistance ($R_f$):** Drag from viscous shear stresses along the wetted hull. Governed by the **Reynolds Number** ($Re = \rho V L / \mu$).
2.  **Wave-Making Resistance ($R_w$):** Drag caused by the energy the ship expends creating a continuous bow and stern wave system. Governed by the **Froude Number** ($Fr = V / \sqrt{gL}$).

To achieve perfect dynamic similarity between a model and a prototype, we must match *both* $Re$ and $Fr$ simultaneously.

*   Match $Fr$: $V_m / \sqrt{g L_m} = V_p / \sqrt{g L_p} \implies V_m = V_p \sqrt{L_m / L_p}$
*   Match $Re$: $V_m L_m / \nu_m = V_p L_p / \nu_p \implies V_m = V_p (L_p / L_m) (\nu_m / \nu_p)$

For these two velocity requirements ($V_m$) to be equal, the kinematic viscosity of the fluid in the model test ($\nu_m$) would have to be:
$$ \nu_m = \nu_p \left( \frac{L_m}{L_p} \right)^{3/2} $$

If testing a 1:100 scale model of a ship that sails in water, the model fluid must have a kinematic viscosity 1,000 times smaller than water. No such fluid exists (liquid mercury is closest, but highly toxic). Therefore, **perfect similitude for ships is impossible.**

## 2. William Froude's Brilliant Hypothesis

In the 1870s, William Froude proposed a practical workaround that forms the basis of all modern ship testing: **Assume the friction drag and wave drag are completely independent of each other.**

Froude's procedure:
1.  **Match the Froude Number:** Tow the scale model in a tank at a speed that matches the Froude number of the real ship. This ensures the wave pattern generated by the model looks exactly like the wave pattern of the real ship.
    $$ V_{model} = V_{prototype} \sqrt{\frac{L_{model}}{L_{prototype}}} $$
2.  **Measure Total Model Resistance ($R_{tm}$):** Read the force required to tow the model at this speed.
3.  **Calculate Model Friction Resistance ($R_{fm}$):** Use empirical formulas for the friction on a flat plate of equivalent length and wetted area at the model's Reynolds number.
4.  **Extract Model Wave Resistance ($R_{wm}$):** Subtract the calculated friction from the measured total.
    $$ R_{wm} = R_{tm} - R_{fm} $$
5.  **Scale the Wave Resistance:** Because the Froude numbers are matched, the dimensionless wave resistance coefficient ($C_w$) is the same for the model and prototype. Scale the model wave resistance up to the prototype size using the ratio of densities and wetted areas.
    $$ R_{wp} = R_{wm} \left( \frac{\rho_p}{\rho_m} \right) \left( \frac{L_p}{L_m} \right)^3 $$
6.  **Calculate Prototype Friction Resistance ($R_{fp}$):** Use the flat-plate empirical formulas again, this time at the massive full-scale Reynolds number.
7.  **Calculate Total Prototype Resistance ($R_{tp}$):** Add the scaled wave resistance and the calculated full-scale friction resistance.
    $$ R_{tp} = R_{wp} + R_{fp} $$

## 3. The "Hull Speed" Limit

Froude number also explains a fundamental physical limit for conventional displacement ships.

A ship creates a wave at its bow and a wave at its stern. The wavelength of these waves ($\lambda$) increases with the speed of the ship ($V \propto \sqrt{\lambda}$).
As a ship accelerates, the bow wave becomes longer. When the speed reaches a specific point, the wavelength of the bow wave exactly equals the waterline length of the ship ($L$). The ship is now supported by the crest of the bow wave and the crest of the stern wave, with a deep trough in the middle.

This occurs at a Froude number of roughly **0.4**.
$$ V_{hull} \approx 1.34 \sqrt{L_{waterline}} \quad \text{(V in knots, L in feet)} $$

If the ship tries to go faster, the wavelength exceeds the ship length. The stern falls into the trough of the bow wave. The ship is literally trying to climb uphill continuously on its own bow wave. The wave-making resistance increases exponentially.
For a non-planing ship, exceeding "hull speed" is economically impossible—adding 10x the engine power might only yield a 5% speed increase. This is why supertankers and cargo ships are long (to increase hull speed) and relatively slow ($Fr \approx 0.15 - 0.25$).
