# Volumes by Cylindrical Shells: Unrolling Geometry

The Disk/Washer method involves slicing the solid *perpendicular* to the axis of revolution. However, sometimes this perpendicular slicing creates mathematical brick walls (e.g., trying to invert a complex function $y = x^3 + 2x$ into $x=f(y)$ is often impossible algebraically). 

The **Method of Cylindrical Shells** offers a powerful alternative: we slice the solid *parallel* to the axis of revolution.

**Engineering Applications:**
*   **Electrical Engineering:** Designing tightly wound electromagnetic coils, solenoids, or transformers, where each "layer" of copper wire acts as a cylindrical shell of a specific radius and height.
*   **Materials Engineering:** Calculating the volume and weight of multi-layered composite pipes or optical fibers, where materials are deposited in concentric cylindrical sheaths.
*   **Metallurgy & Manufacturing:** Determining the total length of sheet metal wound into an industrial roll. Unrolling a mathematical "shell" mimics the physical process of unrolling sheet metal.

---

## 1. The Concept of a Cylindrical Shell

Imagine taking a thin, vertical rectangular strip of area and rotating it around the y-axis. Instead of a solid disc, it sweeps out a hollow, thin-walled cylinder—a "cylindrical shell."

To find the volume of this shell, imagine taking metal snips, cutting it vertically, and unrolling it completely flat. It forms a rectangular slab.
*   The **length** of the slab is the circumference of the unrolled shell: $2\pi r$.
*   The **height** of the slab is the height of the function: $h$.
*   The **thickness** of the slab is the differential width of our slice: $dr$ (or $dx$).

Therefore, the volume of one infinitely thin shell is:
$$ dV = 2\pi \cdot (\text{radius}) \cdot (\text{height}) \cdot (\text{thickness}) $$
$$ dV = 2\pi r \cdot h \cdot dx $$

By integrating, we sum up an infinite nested array of these shells (like Russian nesting dolls) to build the entire solid volume.

---

## 2. Revolution around the y-axis (Integrating with respect to $x$)

If a region is bounded by $y = f(x)$, the x-axis, $x=a$, and $x=b$, and is revolved around the **y-axis**:
*   The **radius** $r$ of a shell at horizontal position $x$ is simply the coordinate distance to the axis: $x$.
*   The **height** $h$ of the shell is the function value evaluated at that point: $f(x)$.
*   The **thickness** is $dx$.

$$ V = \int_{a}^{b} 2\pi x f(x) \, dx $$

**Why use Shells instead of Washers here?**
If you revolve around the y-axis and want to use Washers, you *must* slice horizontally ($dy$), forcing you to rewrite your equation as $x = f(y)$. If your bounding curve is $y = x^2 - x^3$, good luck solving that for $x$! Shells allow you to revolve around a vertical axis while still comfortably integrating with respect to $x$.

### Example 1: Shells around the y-axis (Easy)
**Problem:** Find the volume of the solid generated by revolving the region bounded by $y = 2x - x^2$ and the x-axis around the y-axis.

**Step 1: Find the bounds.**
The curve intersects the x-axis where $y = 0$:
$$ 2x - x^2 = 0 \implies x(2 - x) = 0 $$
Bounds are from $x = 0$ to $x = 2$.

**Step 2: Set up the Shell integral.**
Radius $r = x$. Height $h = 2x - x^2$.
$$ V = 2\pi \int_{0}^{2} x(2x - x^2) \, dx $$

**Step 3: Evaluate.**
$$ V = 2\pi \int_{0}^{2} (2x^2 - x^3) \, dx = 2\pi \left[ \frac{2x^3}{3} - \frac{x^4}{4} \right]_{0}^{2} $$
$$ V = 2\pi \left( \frac{16}{3} - \frac{16}{4} \right) = 2\pi \left( \frac{16}{3} - 4 \right) = 2\pi \left( \frac{16 - 12}{3} \right) = 2\pi \left( \frac{4}{3} \right) = \frac{8\pi}{3} $$

---

## 3. Revolution around the x-axis (Integrating with respect to $y$)

If the region is revolved around the **x-axis**, the shells are horizontal. You must slice horizontally, meaning the thickness is $dy$.

$$ V = \int_{c}^{d} 2\pi y g(y) \, dy $$

*   The **radius** is the vertical distance $y$.
*   The **height** (or length) of the shell is determined by the rightmost curve minus the leftmost curve: $g(y) - h(y)$.

### Example 2: Horizontal Shells (Medium)
**Problem:** Find the volume of the solid generated by revolving the region bounded by $x = \sqrt{y}$, $x = 0$, and $y = 4$ around the x-axis.

**Step 1: Identify shell parameters.**
Since we are revolving around the x-axis using shells, we integrate with respect to $y$.
Radius $r = y$.
The "height" of the horizontal shell is the right curve minus the left curve: $h = \sqrt{y} - 0 = \sqrt{y}$.
The limits of $y$ are from 0 to 4.

**Step 2: Set up and evaluate.**
$$ V = 2\pi \int_{0}^{4} y(\sqrt{y}) \, dy = 2\pi \int_{0}^{4} y^{3/2} \, dy $$
$$ V = 2\pi \left[ \frac{2}{5} y^{5/2} \right]_{0}^{4} $$
$$ V = 2\pi \left( \frac{2}{5} (4)^{5/2} \right) = 2\pi \left( \frac{2}{5} (32) \right) = 2\pi \left( \frac{64}{5} \right) = \frac{128\pi}{5} $$

---

## 4. General Formula and Arbitrary Axes

$$ V = \int 2\pi \cdot (\text{radius}) \cdot (\text{height}) \, d(\text{thickness}) $$

**Revolving around other axes:**
When revolving around a line other than the primary axes, you must carefully adjust the **radius** formula. The height remains the same.
*   Revolving around $x = L$ (where the region is to the left of $L$): Radius $r = L - x$.
*   Revolving around $x = -L$ (where the region is to the right): Radius $r = x - (-L) = x + L$.

### Example 3: Revolving around an arbitrary vertical line (Hard)
**Problem:** The region bounded by $y = 4x - x^2$ and $y = 0$ is revolved about the line $x = 5$. Find the volume.

**Step 1: Bounds and Height.**
Roots are $4x - x^2 = 0 \implies x = 0, x = 4$.
The height of the shell is the top curve minus the bottom curve: $h = (4x - x^2) - 0 = 4x - x^2$.

**Step 2: Determine the Radius.**
The axis of revolution is $x = 5$. Our slices are located at various $x$-coordinates between 0 and 4.
The distance from a slice at $x$ to the axis at $x=5$ is $5 - x$.
So, Radius $r = 5 - x$.

**Step 3: Set up and integrate.**
$$ V = 2\pi \int_{0}^{4} (5 - x)(4x - x^2) \, dx $$
Expand the integrand:
$$ (5 - x)(4x - x^2) = 20x - 5x^2 - 4x^2 + x^3 = x^3 - 9x^2 + 20x $$
$$ V = 2\pi \int_{0}^{4} (x^3 - 9x^2 + 20x) \, dx $$
$$ V = 2\pi \left[ \frac{x^4}{4} - \frac{9x^3}{3} + \frac{20x^2}{2} \right]_{0}^{4} = 2\pi \left[ \frac{x^4}{4} - 3x^3 + 10x^2 \right]_{0}^{4} $$
Evaluate at 4:
$$ \frac{4^4}{4} - 3(4^3) + 10(4^2) = 64 - 3(64) + 10(16) = 64 - 192 + 160 = 32 $$
$$ V = 2\pi (32) = 64\pi $$
