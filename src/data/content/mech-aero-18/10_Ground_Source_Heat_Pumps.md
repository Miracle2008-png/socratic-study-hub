# Ground-Source Heat Pumps (Geothermal)

Air-Source Heat Pumps (ASHPs) suffer degraded performance and frost issues when the winter air turns freezing cold. The solution to this thermodynamic bottleneck is to abandon the fluctuating outside air as a heat source and look downward.

**Ground-Source Heat Pumps (GSHP)**, commonly but inaccurately marketed as "geothermal" heat pumps, utilize the relatively constant temperature of the earth a few meters below the surface.

## 1. The Geothermal Advantage

Below the frost line (typically 1.5 to 3 meters deep depending on latitude), the earth's temperature remains relatively stable year-round, reflecting the annual average air temperature of the region (e.g., 10°C to 15°C in temperate zones).

- **In Winter:** The ground (15°C) is much warmer than the freezing air (-5°C). The heat pump absorbs heat from the ground, enjoying a much smaller temperature lift, resulting in very high heating capacity and a COP consistently between 4.0 and 5.0. Furthermore, there is **zero frost formation** since the heat exchange happens underground.
- **In Summer:** The ground (15°C) is much cooler than the sweltering air (35°C). The heat pump rejects heat into the ground far more easily than it could into hot air, resulting in exceptionally high cooling efficiency (EER/SEER).

## 2. Ground Loop Configurations

The heat exchange with the earth is accomplished by burying a network of high-density polyethylene (HDPE) pipes in the ground, through which an antifreeze solution (water mixed with propylene glycol) is circulated.

**Horizontal Loops:**
- Used when sufficient land area is available.
- Trenches are dug 1 to 2 meters deep, and pipes are laid flat or coiled in a "slinky" shape to maximize heat transfer area.
- Most cost-effective installation method.

**Vertical Loops:**
- Used in urban areas with small lots or where the soil is too rocky for trenching.
- Boreholes are drilled straight down, 50 to 150 meters deep. A U-shaped pipe is inserted, and the hole is backfilled with thermally conductive grout to ensure good heat transfer with the rock.
- More expensive to install, but provides the most stable temperatures.

**Pond/Lake Loops:**
- If a deep body of water is available on the property, coils can simply be submerged at the bottom. This is highly efficient and inexpensive to install, as water has excellent heat transfer properties.

**Open-Loop Systems:**
- Instead of circulating antifreeze in a closed pipe, these systems pump well water directly out of an aquifer, pass it through the heat pump heat exchanger, and discharge it back into a second well or surface water. This provides the highest efficiency but requires abundant groundwater and must meet environmental discharge regulations.

## 3. Economics and Deployment

The thermodynamic superiority of GSHPs is unquestioned. They are the most efficient, longest-lasting, and quietest HVAC systems available.

The primary barrier to universal adoption is **capital cost**. Drilling vertical boreholes or trenching an acre of land requires heavy excavation equipment, making the installation of a GSHP significantly more expensive upfront than an air-source system. The payback period via energy savings is typically 5 to 10 years, making them highly attractive for new construction and long-term institutional building ownership.
