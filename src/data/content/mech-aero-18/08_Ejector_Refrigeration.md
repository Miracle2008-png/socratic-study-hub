# Ejector Refrigeration

**Ejector refrigeration** (also known as steam-jet refrigeration) is another heat-driven cooling cycle. Like absorption refrigeration, it utilizes low-grade thermal energy instead of mechanical work. However, instead of a chemical absorbent and a liquid pump, it uses high-velocity fluid dynamics to compress the refrigerant.

## 1. The Ejector (Steam Jet)

The heart of the system is the **ejector**, a converging-diverging nozzle with no moving parts. It functions as a compressor driven by momentum transfer.

1. **Motive Fluid:** High-pressure, high-temperature vapor (usually steam generated by a boiler or waste heat) enters the primary nozzle of the ejector.
2. **Supersonic Expansion:** As the steam passes through the converging-diverging nozzle, it accelerates to supersonic velocities (Mach 2 to 3). By Bernoulli's principle, this extreme velocity creates a region of very low pressure (a strong vacuum) at the nozzle exit.
3. **Entrainment:** This vacuum is connected to the evaporator. The low pressure draws (entrains) cold, low-pressure refrigerant vapor out of the evaporator.
4. **Mixing and Compression:** The high-velocity motive steam and the entrained vapor mix in the mixing chamber. The combined stream then enters the diffuser section.
5. **Diffusion:** As the diffuser widens, velocity decreases, and kinetic energy is converted back into pressure (pressure recovery). The mixture exits the ejector at a pressure high enough to condense at ambient temperatures.

## 2. The Complete Cycle

The ejector cycle is incredibly simple:
- **Boiler/Generator:** Uses heat to create high-pressure motive vapor.
- **Evaporator:** Water (acting as the refrigerant) boils at very low pressure (a vacuum), providing cooling (e.g., producing chilled water at 5°C).
- **Ejector:** Pulls vapor from the evaporator, mixes it with boiler steam, and compresses it.
- **Condenser:** The mixed stream is condensed by cooling water. A portion of this liquid is throttled back to the evaporator, while the rest is pumped back to the boiler to complete the loop.

Water is almost universally used as the working fluid (safe, cheap, huge latent heat), though it limits cooling to above 0°C (to prevent freezing).

## 3. Advantages and Disadvantages

**Advantages:**
- **Simplicity and Reliability:** The ejector is just a shaped pipe. No moving compressor parts mean nearly zero maintenance and high reliability.
- **Utilizes Waste Heat:** Can run on low-grade industrial waste steam, solar thermal energy, or geothermal heat.
- **Environmentally Friendly:** Uses water as a refrigerant (zero GWP, zero ODP, non-toxic).

**Disadvantages:**
- **Low COP:** The fluid dynamic mixing and shock waves in the ejector are highly irreversible. Typical thermal COP is very low, ranging from **0.2 to 0.4**.
- **Rigid Design Conditions:** Ejectors are fluid-dynamically tuned for specific temperatures and pressures. Their performance drops off a cliff if operating conditions deviate from the design point.

Ejector refrigeration saw heavy use in early 20th-century marine air conditioning and railway cooling, but was largely replaced by vapor-compression. It is seeing a modern resurgence in research for solar-powered air conditioning and recovering waste heat in industrial plants where low COP is acceptable because the heat source is "free."
