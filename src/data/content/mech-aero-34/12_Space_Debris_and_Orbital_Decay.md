# Space Debris and Orbital Decay

The environment of Low Earth Orbit (LEO) is becoming increasingly congested. Since the launch of Sputnik in 1957, humanity has placed thousands of objects into orbit. The long-term sustainability of spaceflight is now threatened by the accumulation of "space junk."

## 1. The Nature of Space Debris

Space debris includes:
-   Dead satellites that have run out of fuel or failed.
-   Upper stages of launch vehicles left in orbit.
-   Debris from collisions or anti-satellite (ASAT) weapons tests.
-   Tiny fragments: paint flecks, solid rocket motor slag, and tools lost during spacewalks.

**The Danger of Velocity:**
In LEO, objects travel at roughly 7.8 km/s (17,500 mph). If two objects in intersecting orbits collide head-on, the relative velocity can exceed 15 km/s. At these hypersonic speeds, kinetic energy is so massive that even a 1-centimeter paint fleck impacts with the energy of a hand grenade, capable of critically damaging a multi-million dollar satellite or puncturing a spacesuit.

## 2. The Kessler Syndrome

Proposed by NASA scientist Donald Kessler in 1978, the Kessler Syndrome describes a catastrophic chain reaction in LEO.
-   If the density of debris becomes high enough, a single collision between two large objects (like dead satellites) generates a massive cloud of thousands of shrapnel fragments.
-   These fragments spread out and collide with other satellites, destroying them and generating even more debris.
-   This cascading effect could render certain critical orbital bands entirely unusable for generations, surrounding Earth in an impenetrable shell of high-speed shrapnel.
-   The 2009 collision between the active Iridium 33 and the defunct Russian Kosmos-2251 satellite was a stark warning, adding thousands of trackable pieces to the debris field.

## 3. Tracking and Conjunction Analysis

The US Space Surveillance Network (using massive ground-based radar and optical telescopes) currently tracks over 27,000 objects larger than a softball (10 cm) in orbit.

-   **Conjunction Assessment:** Organizations continuously calculate the orbits of these objects and propagate them forward in time to predict close approaches.
-   If the probability of collision between an active satellite (or the ISS) and a piece of debris exceeds a certain threshold (e.g., 1 in 10,000), a **Collision Avoidance Maneuver (CAM)** is executed. The active satellite fires its thrusters to slightly alter its orbit and increase the miss distance.

## 4. Mitigation and Orbital Decay

International guidelines now demand debris mitigation strategies for new missions.

**1. The 25-Year Rule:**
Satellites in LEO must be designed to re-enter Earth's atmosphere and burn up no later than 25 years after their mission ends.
-   For lower LEO satellites, natural atmospheric drag will accomplish this automatically.
-   For higher LEO satellites, they must reserve enough fuel to perform a final retrograde burn at end-of-life to lower their perigee deep into the atmosphere.

**2. Graveyard Orbits:**
For Geostationary (GEO) satellites, atmospheric drag is essentially zero. It would take an astronomical amount of fuel to bring a dead GEO satellite all the way down to the atmosphere.
-   Instead, at end-of-life, they use their last bit of fuel to perform a prograde burn, raising their altitude by about 300 km into a "Graveyard Orbit."
-   This removes them from the crowded, highly valuable geostationary belt, parking them out of the way where they will safely drift for millions of years.

**3. Active Debris Removal (ADR):**
The next frontier is developing spacecraft that can actively capture and de-orbit large, dangerous pieces of existing dead debris using robotic arms, nets, or harpoons. Several demonstrator missions (like RemoveDEBRIS) have recently flown.
