# Lagrange Points

In the standard Two-Body Problem, a spacecraft orbits a single dominant mass. However, if we introduce a third body (e.g., the Earth, the Sun, and a spacecraft), we enter the complex realm of the Three-Body Problem.

In the late 18th century, Joseph-Louis Lagrange mathematically proved that in a rotating system with two massive bodies (like the Sun and Earth, or Earth and Moon), there are five specific points in space where the gravitational forces of the two large bodies perfectly balance the centrifugal force felt by a small third body.

If you place a spacecraft at one of these **Lagrange Points** (L-points), it will remain in a fixed relative position to the two large bodies as they rotate.

## 1. The Five Lagrange Points

Let's use the Sun-Earth system as the primary example.

**L1 (The Inner Point):**
-   Located between the Sun and Earth, about 1.5 million km from Earth.
-   Normally, an object closer to the sun must orbit faster than Earth. However, at L1, Earth's gravity pulls backward on the spacecraft, slightly weakening the Sun's net pull. This allows the spacecraft to orbit slower, matching Earth's 365-day period while staying perfectly between them.
-   **Uses:** Ideal for solar observatories (like SOHO). It has an uninterrupted view of the Sun and is not shadowed by Earth.

**L2 (The Outer Point):**
-   Located 1.5 million km "behind" Earth, directly opposite the Sun.
-   Normally, an object further away should orbit slower. But at L2, the combined gravity of the Sun and Earth pulls the spacecraft inward slightly harder, allowing it to orbit faster and keep pace with Earth.
-   **Uses:** The premier location for deep-space telescopes (like the James Webb Space Telescope, JWST). At L2, the Sun, Earth, and Moon are always in the same direction, allowing the telescope's massive sunshield to block out their heat and light simultaneously, looking deep into the cold universe.

**L3 (The Far Point):**
-   Located on the exact opposite side of the Sun from Earth.
-   **Uses:** Rarely used. (It is the classic hiding spot for sci-fi "Counter-Earths").

**L4 and L5 (The Trojan Points):**
-   Located 60 degrees ahead of (L4) and 60 degrees behind (L5) Earth in its orbit around the Sun. They form equilateral triangles with the two large masses.
-   **Stability:** Unlike L1, L2, and L3 (which are unstable equilibria, like a marble balanced on a beach ball, requiring constant station-keeping burns), L4 and L5 are **stable equilibria** (like a marble in a bowl). If an object drifts slightly away, the Coriolis forces nudge it back.
-   **Uses:** Natural dust, asteroids, and debris tend to collect at these points. Jupiter has massive swarms of "Trojan" asteroids at its L4 and L5 points.

## 2. Halo Orbits and Lissajous Orbits

You don't actually park a satellite exactly *on* a Lagrange point.
-   For L1 and L2, pointing an antenna back at Earth would mean pointing it directly at the Sun (blinding receivers with solar noise).
-   Instead, spacecraft are placed into dynamic, complex orbits *around* the empty space of the Lagrange point.
-   These are non-Keplerian orbits driven by three-body dynamics. They trace out large, three-dimensional shapes (Halo or Lissajous orbits) perpendicular to the Earth-Sun line.
-   Because L1 and L2 are unstable, the spacecraft must perform small thruster burns every few weeks (station-keeping) to avoid falling off the saddle-point and drifting away into a heliocentric orbit.
