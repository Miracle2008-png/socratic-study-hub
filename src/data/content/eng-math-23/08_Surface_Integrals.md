# 8. Surface Integrals

Just as there were two types of Line Integrals (Scalar and Vector), there are two types of **Surface Integrals**.

### 1. Surface Integrals of Scalar Functions
Imagine a curved, incredibly thin sheet of metal floating in space (a Surface $S$). The density of the metal changes across the sheet, given by the scalar function $f(x,y,z)$. To find the total mass of the sheet, we integrate the density over the surface area $dS$.

$$
\iint_S f(x,y,z) \, dS
$$

To calculate this, we use the parameterization tools from the previous page:
$$
\iint_D f(\mathbf{r}(u,v)) \, |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v| \, dA
$$
*(Notice how the abstract $dS$ becomes the physical scaling magnitude $|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v| \, dA$. This is exactly how we corrected the stretching of the grid!)*

### 2. Surface Integrals of Vector Fields (Flux)
This is the single most important integral in electromagnetism and fluid dynamics.
Imagine a river flowing steadily (a Vector Field $\mathbf{F}$). You place a curved fishing net (a Surface $S$) into the water.

How much water physically flows *through* the net every single second?
This calculation is called **Flux**.

To calculate flux, we don't care about water sliding sideways along the net. We only care about the water punching directly through the holes. Therefore, we must take the **Dot Product** of the fluid velocity vector ($\mathbf{F}$) and the outward-pointing Normal Vector ($\mathbf{n}$) of the net at every single point!

$$
\text{Flux} = \iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{n} \, dS
$$

### How to Calculate Flux
Because we know from the previous page that $\mathbf{n} \, dS$ is simply generated by the cross product $(\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v) \, dA$, the massive Flux integral simplifies beautifully into:

$$
\iint_D \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(u,v)) \cdot (\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v) \, dA
$$

1. Parameterize the surface into $u$ and $v$.
2. Calculate the cross product $\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v$.
3. Substitute the $u$ and $v$ formulas into the vector field $\mathbf{F}$.
4. Take the dot product. This turns the vectors into a scalar equation.
5. Perform a standard 2D double integral over the flat $uv$-plane.

If the Flux is positive, more fluid is flowing out of the surface. If Flux is negative, fluid is flowing into the surface!
