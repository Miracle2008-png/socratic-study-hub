# Dialysis and Electrodialysis

Not all membrane processes are driven by mechanical pressure. Dialysis and Electrodialysis use concentration gradients and electrical voltage as their driving forces, respectively.

## 1. Dialysis

Dialysis is a separation process driven purely by a **concentration gradient** ($\Delta C$). There is no pressure applied.

**The Process:**
A feed solution (containing both large and small molecules) is placed on one side of a porous membrane. A clean solvent (the *dialysate*) is placed on the other side.
Because of the concentration gradient, molecules naturally want to diffuse from the feed into the clean dialysate. However, the membrane pores are sized such that small molecules (salts, urea) can diffuse across, while large macromolecules (proteins, red blood cells) are physically trapped.

**Hemodialysis (The Artificial Kidney):**
The most vital application of dialysis is in medicine. When human kidneys fail, toxins (like urea and creatinine) and excess salts build up in the blood.
*   The patient's blood is pumped down one side of a hollow-fiber dialyzer module.
*   A specially formulated, ultra-pure water solution (dialysate) is pumped counter-current down the other side.
*   The toxic urea and excess potassium diffuse out of the blood, across the membrane, and into the dialysate to be flushed away.
*   The red blood cells and vital blood proteins are too large to cross the membrane and safely return to the patient.

## 2. Electrodialysis (ED)

Electrodialysis is used exclusively to separate charged ions (salts) from uncharged molecules (water, sugar). The driving force is an **electrical potential gradient** ($\Delta V$).

**The Membranes:**
ED uses highly specialized Ion-Exchange Membranes.
*   **Cation-Exchange Membranes (CEM):** Have a permanent negative charge built into the polymer. They repel negative ions, but allow positive cations (like $Na^+$) to pass through.
*   **Anion-Exchange Membranes (AEM):** Have a permanent positive charge. They repel positive ions, but allow negative anions (like $Cl^-$) to pass through.

**The Process:**
Hundreds of these membranes are stacked in an alternating pattern (CEM - AEM - CEM - AEM). Salty feed water flows between all the sheets.
A massive DC voltage is applied across the entire stack.
1.  The positive electrode (Anode) pulls all the negative $Cl^-$ ions towards it.
2.  The negative electrode (Cathode) pulls all the positive $Na^+$ ions towards it.
3.  As the $Na^+$ moves towards the cathode, it easily passes through the first CEM it hits. But then it gets trapped, because the very next membrane is an AEM, which repels it.
4.  Similarly, the $Cl^-$ moving towards the anode passes through the AEM, but gets trapped by the next CEM.
5.  The result is that alternate channels in the stack become completely depleted of salt (the purified product), while the adjacent channels become highly concentrated brine (the waste).

**Applications of ED:**
*   **Desalination of Brackish Water:** Extremely energy-efficient for moderately salty groundwater, though Reverse Osmosis is better for seawater.
*   **Food Industry:** De-salting soy sauce or fruit juices without heating them.
*   **Zero Liquid Discharge (ZLD):** Concentrating industrial wastewater brine to the absolute maximum limit before sending it to an evaporator.
