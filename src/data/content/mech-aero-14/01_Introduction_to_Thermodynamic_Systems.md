# Introduction to Thermodynamic Systems

**Thermodynamics** is the science of energy — its forms, its transformations, and the limits of converting it from one form to another. Where mechanics deals with forces and motion, thermodynamics deals with heat, work, and the state of matter. It underpins every power plant, engine, refrigerator, and jet turbine ever built.

## 1. Key Definitions

**System:** A quantity of matter or a region in space chosen for analysis. The boundary of the system separates it from the **surroundings**. Everything outside the system boundary is the surroundings.

**Closed System (Control Mass):** A fixed quantity of matter. No mass crosses the boundary. Energy (heat and work) may cross. Example: a piston-cylinder assembly with a sealed gas.

**Open System (Control Volume):** A region in space. Both mass and energy may cross the boundary. Example: a turbine, compressor, or heat exchanger in steady operation.

**Isolated System:** Neither mass nor energy can cross the boundary. The universe is an isolated system.

**State:** The condition of a system described by a set of properties. When all properties are specified, the state is fully defined.

**Process:** A change from one state to another. If during a process a system passes through equilibrium states slowly enough that it can be considered in equilibrium at each instant, the process is **quasi-static** (reversible in the thermodynamic sense).

**Cycle:** A series of processes that returns a system to its initial state.

## 2. Thermodynamic Properties

**Intensive properties:** Independent of mass — temperature $T$, pressure $P$, density $\rho$, specific volume $v = 1/\rho$.

**Extensive properties:** Proportional to mass — total volume $V$, total internal energy $U$, total enthalpy $H$. Dividing by mass converts to intensive (specific) quantities: $v = V/m$, $u = U/m$, $h = H/m$.

**State Postulate (for simple compressible systems):** The thermodynamic state of a simple compressible system is completely specified by **two independent intensive properties**. If you know $T$ and $P$ (for a single-phase substance), all other properties ($\rho, h, u, s$, etc.) are uniquely determined from equations of state or thermodynamic tables.

## 3. Thermal Equilibrium and Temperature

**Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics:** If body A is in thermal equilibrium with body C, and body B is also in thermal equilibrium with body C, then A and B are in thermal equilibrium with each other. This establishes the concept of **temperature** as a measurable property and validates the use of thermometers.

Temperature scales:
*   **Celsius (°C):** Water freezes at 0°C, boils at 100°C (at 1 atm).
*   **Kelvin (K):** $T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15$. Absolute scale — zero is absolute zero.
*   **Fahrenheit (°F):** $T(°F) = 1.8 T(°C) + 32$.
*   **Rankine (°R):** $T(°R) = T(°F) + 459.67$. US absolute scale.

All thermodynamic equations use absolute temperatures (K or °R). Never substitute Celsius or Fahrenheit into thermodynamic formulae.
