# Pure Substances and Phase Diagrams

A **pure substance** is a substance with a fixed chemical composition throughout. Water ($\text{H}_2\text{O}$), nitrogen ($\text{N}_2$), and refrigerant R-134a are pure substances. Air (a mixture of $\text{N}_2$, $\text{O}_2$, Ar) behaves as a pure substance as long as no condensation occurs.

## 1. Phases of a Pure Substance

A pure substance exists in three phases:
*   **Solid:** Molecules are in a fixed lattice. Strong intermolecular forces. Definite shape and volume.
*   **Liquid:** Molecules move freely but remain close. Definite volume but no fixed shape.
*   **Gas (Vapor):** Molecules move rapidly and are widely separated. No definite shape or volume. Compressible.

## 2. The Compressed Liquid → Saturated Mixture → Superheated Vapor Sequence

Consider heating water at constant pressure (1 atm) starting from 20°C:

1.  **Compressed (Subcooled) Liquid:** Water is entirely liquid and below the boiling point. Adding heat raises $T$ without phase change.
2.  **Saturated Liquid (Point f):** Temperature reaches the boiling point (100°C at 1 atm). Further heat addition does not raise $T$ — it converts liquid to vapor. This is the **saturation temperature** ($T_{sat}$).
3.  **Wet Mixture (Two-Phase Region):** Both liquid and vapor coexist in equilibrium. Temperature and pressure are both constant (tied together by the saturation curve). Quality $x$ rises from 0 to 1.
4.  **Saturated Vapor (Point g):** All liquid has converted to vapor ($x = 1$). Further heat addition raises $T$.
5.  **Superheated Vapor:** Temperature exceeds $T_{sat}$ at the given pressure. The gas behaves increasingly like an ideal gas.

## 3. The P-T Diagram (Phase Diagram)

On a Pressure-Temperature ($P$-$T$) diagram, the three phases are separated by lines:
*   **Fusion curve (Solid-Liquid):** Melting/freezing boundary. For water it slopes slightly backward (unique — most substances slope forward).
*   **Vaporization curve (Liquid-Vapor):** Boiling/condensation boundary. Ends at the **Critical Point** $(P_c, T_c)$.
*   **Sublimation curve (Solid-Vapor):** Direct solid-to-gas transition.

All three curves meet at the **Triple Point** — the unique $P$ and $T$ where all three phases coexist in equilibrium.
*   Water: Triple point at $T = 273.16 \text{ K}$, $P = 611.7 \text{ Pa}$.

**The Critical Point:** Above the critical pressure $P_c$, no phase boundary exists between liquid and vapor. The substance exists as a **supercritical fluid** — properties intermediate between liquid and gas. Water: $P_c = 22.06 \text{ MPa}$, $T_c = 374.1°\text{C}$.
Supercritical CO₂ is commercially used as a green solvent for extracting caffeine from coffee.
