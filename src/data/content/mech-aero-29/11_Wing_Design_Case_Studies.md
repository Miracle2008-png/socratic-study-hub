# Wing Design Case Studies

Examining how real aircraft wings were designed reveals how the theoretical tools of finite wing theory are applied to practical engineering constraints. Each aircraft generation has pushed the limits of aerodynamic efficiency, enabled by advances in computational methods and materials.

## 1. The Spitfire (WWII Elliptical Wing)

**Problem:** Prandtl's theory had just shown (1918) that an elliptical planform minimizes induced drag for a given span and lift. The RAF needed a high-performance interceptor.

**Solution:** R.J. Mitchell at Supermarine chose an actual elliptical planform for the Spitfire's wing. At the time, elliptical wings were considered impractical due to manufacturing difficulty — every rib and skin panel has a different shape with no straight-line elements.

**Result:** The Spitfire's wing was extraordinarily aerodynamically efficient — low induced drag, very thin (causing problems at high speed; it was structurally very stiff), and the elliptical loading delayed tip stall. 

**Lesson:** Manufacturing complexity was a real cost — later marks used clipped wings (cut the elliptical tip to a square tip) for faster roll rate at the expense of some efficiency. In engineering, the optimal theoretical solution is rarely the optimal practical solution.

## 2. Boeing 707 (First Swept Jet Transport)

**Problem:** Design a jetliner that cruises at Mach 0.82 efficiently. Straight wings would suffer enormous transonic wave drag at this speed.

**Solution:** 35° swept wings. The 707's wings were the result of intensive wind tunnel testing (thousands of hours at the Boeing Transonic Wind Tunnel) and early application of swept-wing theory. Key features: moderate aspect ratio (AR ≈ 7), leading-edge slats, multi-slotted trailing edge flaps for low-speed performance.

**Lesson:** The swept wing introduced new problems (tip stall, low-speed handling, structural complexity) that required new design tools — not just sweeping the wing, but correctly designing the twist, camber, and high-lift system for the swept configuration.

## 3. Boeing 747 (High Bypass Turbofan Era)

**Problem:** Carry 400+ passengers with acceptable fuel efficiency on long-range routes. Need very high lift at low speed for heavy MTOW.

**Solution:** AR ≈ 7.7 with advanced triple-slotted Fowler flaps (achieving $C_{L,max} \approx 3.0$) and Krueger leading-edge devices. The wing uses variable camber — sections vary from root to tip in thickness and camber to optimize the load distribution across the very large span.

**Lesson:** High-lift system design becomes as important as cruise efficiency. The 747's massive flap system (mechanically the most complex ever built at that time) was the engineering key to enabling the aircraft to take off at its enormous weight.

## 4. Airbus A350 (Composite Wing Era)

**Problem:** Maximize fuel efficiency for the 2010s market. Composites allow wings that were previously structurally impossible.

**Solution:** Carbon fiber composite wing with AR ≈ 9.5 — higher than was previously structurally feasible in aluminum for this aircraft class. Sharklet winglets (3.5m tall) reduce induced drag by ~3.5%. Advanced supercritical airfoil sections with optimized spanwise twist for near-elliptical loading at cruise. Active load alleviation reduces wing root bending moment by adjusting control surface deflections.

**Lesson:** Materials technology unlocked a step-change in aerodynamic performance. The composite wing enables aspect ratios that aluminum would make impossibly heavy, directly reducing induced drag and improving fuel efficiency by 15–25% over previous-generation aircraft.
