# 6. Span of a Set of Vectors

If you are handed a set of vectors $\{\mathbf{v_1}, \mathbf{v_2}, \dots, \mathbf{v_p}\}$, you can create thousands of new vectors by mixing them together using different weights in a linear combination.

If you generate *every single possible* linear combination—using every possible scalar number from $-\infty$ to $\infty$ for the weights—the massive collection of all the resulting vectors is called the **Span** of the original set.

We denote this as $\text{Span}\{\mathbf{v_1}, \mathbf{v_2}, \dots, \mathbf{v_p}\}$.

### Geometric Meaning of Span

The Span describes the entire "reachable space" that the given vectors can build.

1.  **Span of 1 Vector:** 
    If you are given one non-zero vector $\mathbf{v_1}$, the only linear combinations you can make are scalar multiples: $c\mathbf{v_1}$. 
    If you plot every possible multiple of this vector, you trace out an infinite, straight **Line** passing through the origin. Therefore, the span of one vector is a 1D Line.

2.  **Span of 2 Independent Vectors:** 
    If you are given two vectors $\mathbf{v_1}$ and $\mathbf{v_2}$ that are NOT parallel (they are independent), their linear combinations ($c_1\mathbf{v_1} + c_2\mathbf{v_2}$) can reach anywhere on a flat 2D surface. 
    Therefore, the span of two independent vectors is a flat **Plane** passing through the origin.

3.  **Span of 2 Dependent Vectors:**
    What if the two vectors are parallel? (e.g., $\mathbf{v_1} = \langle 1,0 \rangle$ and $\mathbf{v_2} = \langle 2,0 \rangle$).
    Because $\mathbf{v_2}$ lies directly on top of $\mathbf{v_1}$, it provides no new geometric reach. Even with both vectors, you can still only reach points along that single line. 
    The span of two dependent vectors is just a 1D **Line**, not a plane!

### The Ultimate Theorem
If you calculate the Span of *any* set of vectors from a vector space $V$, that Span is mathematically guaranteed to form a perfectly valid **Subspace** of $V$. 

Even if you just pick three random, garbage vectors in $\mathbb{R}^5$, the infinite geometric space generated by their span will perfectly pass all the 10 axioms of a Vector Space!
