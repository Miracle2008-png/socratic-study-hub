# Organic Chemistry III: Carbonyls, Alcohols, and Amines

## 1. Reactions of Alcohols
Alcohols (R-OH) can act as nucleophiles, electrophiles (after activation), or acids.

### 1.1 Oxidation of Alcohols
- **Primary ($1^\circ$) alcohols** oxidize to aldehydes, then to carboxylic acids.
  - PCC or Swern oxidation stops at the aldehyde: $\text{R-CH}_2\text{OH} \xrightarrow{\text{PCC}} \text{R-CHO}$
  - Chromic acid ($\text{H}_2\text{CrO}_4$, Jones reagent) oxidizes to the carboxylic acid: $\text{R-CH}_2\text{OH} \xrightarrow{\text{H}_2\text{CrO}_4} \text{R-COOH}$
- **Secondary ($2^\circ$) alcohols** oxidize to ketones: $\text{R-CH(OH)-R'} \xrightarrow{[\text{O}]} \text{R-CO-R'}$
- **Tertiary ($3^\circ$) alcohols** cannot be oxidized under normal conditions because there is no $\alpha$-hydrogen.

**Worked Example 1.1: Multi-Step Synthesis with Alcohols**
Synthesize 2-butanone from 1-butene.
**Step 1:** Hydrate the alkene to form an alcohol. Using acid-catalyzed hydration ($\text{H}_2\text{O}, \text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$), Markovnikov addition occurs, yielding 2-butanol.
$$ \text{CH}_2\text{=CH-CH}_2\text{-CH}_3 \xrightarrow{\text{H}_2\text{O}, \text{H}^+} \text{CH}_3\text{-CH(OH)-CH}_2\text{-CH}_3 $$
**Step 2:** Oxidize the secondary alcohol to a ketone. We can use $\text{Na}_2\text{Cr}_2\text{O}_7$ / $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$ or PCC.
$$ \text{CH}_3\text{-CH(OH)-CH}_2\text{-CH}_3 \xrightarrow{\text{Na}_2\text{Cr}_2\text{O}_7, \text{H}^+} \text{CH}_3\text{-C(=O)-CH}_2\text{-CH}_3 $$
**Result:** 2-butanone is successfully synthesized.

## 2. Aldehydes and Ketones: Nucleophilic Addition
The carbonyl carbon is electrophilic due to the polar C=O bond. Nucleophiles attack this carbon, breaking the $\pi$ bond to form a tetrahedral intermediate.

### 2.1 Grignard Addition
Grignard reagents (R-MgX) act as strong carbanion nucleophiles ($\text{R}^-$) and add to carbonyls to form alcohols.
- Formaldehyde $\rightarrow$ $1^\circ$ alcohol
- Aldehyde $\rightarrow$ $2^\circ$ alcohol
- Ketone $\rightarrow$ $3^\circ$ alcohol

**Worked Example 2.1: Grignard Synthesis**
Predict the product of the reaction between cyclopentanone and methylmagnesium bromide ($\text{CH}_3\text{MgBr}$), followed by aqueous workup ($\text{H}_3\text{O}^+$).
**Step 1:** The nucleophilic methyl group from $\text{CH}_3\text{MgBr}$ attacks the electrophilic carbonyl carbon of cyclopentanone.
**Step 2:** The C=O $\pi$ electrons push onto the oxygen, forming an alkoxide intermediate (a tetrahedral carbon bonded to the ring carbons, the new methyl group, and an $\text{O}^-$).
**Step 3:** Aqueous workup protonates the alkoxide oxygen.
**Result:** The final product is 1-methylcyclopentanol (a tertiary alcohol).

### 2.2 Acetal Formation
Aldehydes and ketones react with two equivalents of an alcohol under acidic conditions to form acetals.
$$ \text{R}_2\text{C=O} + 2\text{R'OH} \xrightleftharpoons{\text{H}^+} \text{R}_2\text{C(OR')}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} $$
Acetals are commonly used as protecting groups because they are stable to base but can be hydrolyzed back to the carbonyl in aqueous acid.

## 3. Carboxylic Acid Derivatives: Nucleophilic Acyl Substitution
Derivatives (acid chlorides, anhydrides, esters, amides) undergo substitution rather than addition. The tetrahedral intermediate expels a leaving group to regenerate the carbonyl.
Reactivity order: Acid Chloride > Anhydride > Ester > Amide.

**Worked Example 3.1: Fischer Esterification**
Provide the mechanism for the reaction of acetic acid with ethanol and a catalytic amount of sulfuric acid.
**Step 1:** Protonation of the carbonyl oxygen makes the carbonyl carbon more electrophilic.
**Step 2:** Nucleophilic attack by ethanol (lone pairs on O) on the carbonyl carbon forms a tetrahedral intermediate.
**Step 3:** Proton transfer. The proton moves from the ethanol oxygen to one of the hydroxyl oxygens, making it a good leaving group ($-\text{OH}_2^+$).
**Step 4:** A lone pair from the other oxygen drops down to reform the C=O double bond, expelling water as the leaving group.
**Step 5:** Deprotonation of the carbonyl oxygen yields the neutral ester product.
**Result:** Ethyl acetate and water.

## 4. Amines
Amines ($\text{R-NH}_2$, $\text{R}_2\text{NH}$, $\text{R}_3\text{N}$) are basic and nucleophilic.

### 4.1 Synthesis via Reductive Amination
Reacting an aldehyde or ketone with an amine produces an imine or enamine intermediate, which is reduced in situ to yield a new amine. Commonly $\text{NaBH}_3\text{CN}$ is used as the reducing agent.

**Worked Example 4.1: Reductive Amination**
Synthesize N-ethylcyclohexanamine from cyclohexanone.
**Step 1:** Choose the correct amine. We need to add an ethyl group to the nitrogen, so we use ethylamine ($\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{NH}_2$).
**Step 2:** Mix cyclohexanone and ethylamine in the presence of mild acid to form the imine intermediate.
$$ \text{Cyclohexanone} + \text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{NH}_2 \rightleftharpoons \text{N-ethylcyclohexanimine} + \text{H}_2\text{O} $$
**Step 3:** Reduce the imine with sodium cyanoborohydride ($\text{NaBH}_3\text{CN}$).
**Result:** N-ethylcyclohexanamine is formed.
