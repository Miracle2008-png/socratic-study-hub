# Non-Isothermal Reactor Design

Until now, we have assumed our reactors are perfectly Isothermal (the temperature never changes). 
In the real world, virtually all chemical reactions either release massive amounts of heat (exothermic) or absorb massive amounts of heat (endothermic). 

If a reaction is exothermic, the heat released raises the temperature of the fluid. According to the Arrhenius equation, this higher temperature exponentially increases the reaction rate. This faster rate releases even more heat, which raises the temperature further!

This feedback loop means we can no longer treat Temperature ($T$) as a constant. We must solve the Mass Balance (Conversion $X$) and the Energy Balance (Temperature $T$) simultaneously.

## 1. The Energy Balance Equation

To find the temperature inside the reactor, we write a macroscopic Energy Balance:

$$ \text{Heat IN} - \text{Heat OUT} + \text{Heat of Reaction} - \text{Heat Removed by Cooling} = 0 $$

Let's break this down for a continuous flow reactor (CSTR or PFR):
1.  **Heat IN - Heat OUT:** This is the sensible heat gained by the fluid moving through the reactor. It is calculated using the mass flow rate ($m$), specific heat capacity ($C_p$), and the temperature difference: $\Sigma F_{i0} C_{pi} (T - T_0)$.
2.  **Heat of Reaction ($Q_G$):** This is the heat physically generated by the chemical bonds breaking and forming. It is the moles reacted ($F_{A0} \cdot X$) multiplied by the Enthalpy of Reaction ($\Delta H_{Rx}$). 
    *If Exothermic, $\Delta H_{Rx}$ is negative, so $-(\Delta H_{Rx})$ generates positive heat.*
3.  **Heat Removed ($Q_R$):** This is the heat removed by cooling jackets or coils. $Q_R = U \cdot A \cdot (T - T_{coolant})$.

Combining these into the standard adiabatic energy balance (assuming the reactor is heavily insulated and $Q_R = 0$ for simplicity):

$$ \Sigma F_{i0} C_{pi} (T - T_0) = F_{A0} \cdot X \cdot (-\Delta H_{Rx}) $$

Rearranging to solve for Temperature ($T$) as a function of Conversion ($X$):

$$ T = T_0 + \frac{(-\Delta H_{Rx})}{\Sigma \theta_i C_{pi}} \cdot X $$

This beautiful, simple equation is the **Adiabatic Energy Balance**. It states that the temperature in the reactor ($T$) increases linearly with conversion ($X$).

## 2. Simultaneous Solution

Because Temperature depends on Conversion (Energy Balance), and Conversion depends on Temperature (Arrhenius Rate Law), they must be solved together.

1.  Pick a Conversion $X$.
2.  Use the Energy Balance to calculate the Temperature $T$ at that conversion.
3.  Plug that $T$ into the Arrhenius equation to find $k(T)$.
4.  Plug $k(T)$ and $X$ into the Rate Law to find $-r_A$.
5.  Use $-r_A$ to size the reactor using the Levenspiel plot.

## 3. The PFR "Hot Spot"

In an adiabatic PFR carrying out a highly exothermic reaction, the temperature starts at $T_0$. As the fluid flows down the pipe, $X$ increases, so $T$ increases.
Because $T$ is increasing, the reaction rate skyrockets. The fluid releases heat even faster. 

The temperature profile down the pipe will spike dramatically into a **Hot Spot**. 
If the engineer does not add cooling jackets ($Q_R$), this hot spot will easily exceed the melting point of the steel pipe or cause the catalyst to fuse into a solid block of slag. Designing the heat exchangers to successfully flatten this hot spot is the most critical task in PFR design.

## 4. CSTR Multiple Steady States

The CSTR is completely different. Because it is perfectly mixed, the temperature inside the tank ($T$) is uniform everywhere.
However, the feedback loop between heat generation and Arrhenius kinetics can cause a terrifying phenomenon in CSTRs: **Multiple Steady States**.

Depending solely on how you start the reactor up, the exact same CSTR with the exact same feed could settle into:
1.  **A Lower Steady State:** The tank is cold. The reaction is barely happening. Conversion is 5%.
2.  **An Upper Steady State:** The tank is boiling hot. The reaction is screaming fast. Conversion is 99%.
3.  **An Unstable Middle State:** (Mathematically exists but physically impossible to maintain).

If the reactor is running safely at the lower steady state, and a tiny burp in the feed temporarily increases the temperature, the reactor can "ignite." The kinetics take over, the heat generation outpaces the cooling jacket, and the reactor spontaneously accelerates to the upper steady state. This is known as **Reactor Runaway**, and it is the cause of most major chemical plant explosions.
