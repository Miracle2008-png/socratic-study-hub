# The Energy Balance in Reactors

While the adiabatic energy balance ($T = T_0 + \dots X$) is useful for simple estimates, real industrial reactors require active heating or cooling to maintain safe and optimal temperatures. We must incorporate the heat transfer term ($Q_R$) into our rigorous reactor design.

## 1. The Rigorous Energy Balance

The fundamental energy balance for any steady-state flow reactor is:
$$ \frac{dT}{dV} = \frac{Ua(T_a - T) + (-r_A)(-\Delta H_{Rx})}{\Sigma F_i C_{pi}} $$

Let's dissect this critical differential equation term by term. This equation describes how the temperature ($T$) changes as you move down the volume ($V$) of a PFR.

### The Denominator: Thermal Mass
*   $\Sigma F_i C_{pi}$: This is the total heat capacity of the flowing stream. It tells you how much energy is required to raise the temperature of the mixture by 1 degree. If you pump massive amounts of inert gas (like Nitrogen) through the reactor, this term becomes huge, meaning the temperature will barely change regardless of how much heat the reaction generates.

### The Numerator, Part 1: Heat Generation ($Q_G$)
*   $(-r_A)(-\Delta H_{Rx})$: This is the heat generated by the chemical reaction.
    *   $-r_A$ is the speed of the reaction (moles per second per volume).
    *   $-\Delta H_{Rx}$ is the heat released per mole.
    *   Because $-r_A$ is an Arrhenius function ($k_0 e^{-E/RT}$), this heat generation term increases **exponentially** as the temperature $T$ rises.

### The Numerator, Part 2: Heat Removal ($Q_R$)
*   $Ua(T_a - T)$: This is the heat removed by the cooling jacket.
    *   $U$ is the overall heat transfer coefficient.
    *   $a$ is the heat transfer surface area per unit volume of the reactor (e.g., $4/D$ for a pipe).
    *   $T_a$ is the ambient temperature of the cooling fluid in the jacket.
    *   $T$ is the hot temperature of the reacting fluid.
    *   Notice that this term increases **linearly** as the reactor temperature $T$ rises.

## 2. The Battle: Generation vs. Removal

The safety and stability of a chemical reactor are entirely determined by the battle between $Q_G$ (Exponential) and $Q_R$ (Linear).

If we plot both $Q_G$ and $Q_R$ on the y-axis against Temperature ($T$) on the x-axis:
*   The $Q_R$ cooling line is a straight line sloping upward.
*   The $Q_G$ generation curve is an exponential S-curve. It starts flat, curves violently upward as the reaction ignites, and then flattens out again at high temperatures when the reactant $A$ is entirely consumed.

**Steady State Intersections:**
The reactor can only operate at a steady state where the heat generated exactly equals the heat removed. Visually, these are the points where the linear $Q_R$ line intersects the exponential $Q_G$ curve.

Because a straight line can intersect an S-curve up to three times, a CSTR can have **Multiple Steady States**:
1.  **Intersection 1 (Low T):** Stable. $Q_R$ is steeper than $Q_G$. If the reactor gets slightly hotter, the cooling jacket removes heat faster than the reaction generates it, pushing the reactor back to safety.
2.  **Intersection 2 (Medium T):** UNSTABLE. The exponential $Q_G$ is steeper than $Q_R$. If the reactor gets slightly hotter, the reaction generates heat faster than the jacket can remove it. The temperature rockets upward uncontrollably until it hits...
3.  **Intersection 3 (High T):** Stable, but very hot. 

## 3. Designing for Safety (Avoiding Runaway)

To prevent reactor runaway, an engineer must ensure the $Q_R$ cooling line is always steeper than the $Q_G$ curve, or that it only intersects the curve once at a safe, low temperature.

How do we do this?
1.  **Increase $U$:** Use better heat transfer fluids or more turbulent flow in the jacket (increases the slope of $Q_R$).
2.  **Increase $a$:** Use a bundle of many narrow tubes instead of one giant pipe (massively increases surface area).
3.  **Lower $T_a$:** Use a colder refrigerant in the cooling jacket (shifts the $Q_R$ line to the left).
4.  **Add Diluent:** Pump inert solvent into the reactor. This increases the denominator ($\Sigma F_i C_{pi}$), acting as a massive thermal heat sink to absorb the reaction energy.
