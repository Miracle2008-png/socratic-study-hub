# The Torsion Formula

Having established that shear strain $\gamma$ varies linearly from the center to the outer surface of a circular shaft, we can now determine the distribution of shear stress $\tau$ and relate it to the internal torque $T$.

## 1. Linear Variation of Shear Stress

We restrict our analysis to the **linear elastic region** of the material, where Hooke's Law for shear applies: $\tau = G \gamma$.

Because the material property $G$ is constant, and the strain $\gamma$ varies linearly with the radius $\rho$, the shear stress $\tau$ must also vary linearly with the radius:
$$ \tau = \left(\frac{\rho}{c}\right) \tau_{max} $$

The shear stress is zero at the central longitudinal axis and reaches a maximum at the outer surface of the shaft.

## 2. Deriving the Torsion Formula

The internal torque $T$ is statically equivalent to the resultant moment of all the microscopic shear stresses acting on the cross-section.

Consider an infinitesimally small area $dA$ at a distance $\rho$ from the center. The shear force on this area is $dF = \tau dA$.
The moment (torque) generated by this tiny force about the center axis is $dT = \rho (\tau dA)$.

Integrating this over the entire cross-sectional area $A$ gives the total internal torque:
$$ T = \int_A \rho \tau \, dA $$

Substitute the linear stress distribution $\tau = (\rho/c)\tau_{max}$:
$$ T = \int_A \rho \left( \frac{\rho}{c} \tau_{max} \right) dA = \frac{\tau_{max}}{c} \int_A \rho^2 dA $$

The integral $\int_A \rho^2 dA$ is a purely geometric property of the cross-section known as the **polar moment of inertia**, denoted by the letter **$J$**.

Substituting $J$ into the equation and rearranging to solve for the maximum shear stress yields the famous **Torsion Formula**:
$$ \tau_{max} = \frac{T c}{J} $$

To find the shear stress at any intermediate radial distance $\rho$:
$$ \tau = \frac{T \rho}{J} $$

Where:
- $\tau$ = shear stress.
- $T$ = internal resultant torque at the section.
- $\rho$ = radial distance from the center to the point of interest.
- $c$ = outer radius of the shaft.
- $J$ = polar moment of inertia of the cross-sectional area.

## 3. Polar Moment of Inertia ($J$)

The polar moment of inertia measures the cross-section's geometric resistance to twisting.

- **Solid Circular Shaft** (radius $c$):
  $$ J = \frac{\pi}{2} c^4 $$
- **Hollow Circular Tube** (outer radius $c_o$, inner radius $c_i$):
  $$ J = \frac{\pi}{2} (c_o^4 - c_i^4) $$

*(Note: Do not confuse $c_o^4 - c_i^4$ with $(c_o - c_i)^4$. They are vastly different).*
