# Acoustics and Aerodynamic Noise

When flow velocities are very low, we ignore compressibility. But even in low-speed flows, small pressure fluctuations propagate as sound waves. The study of sound generated by fluid flow is called **aeroacoustics**. This field is critical for designing quiet aircraft, wind turbines, and HVAC systems.

## 1. The Physics of Sound

Sound is a weak, propagating pressure wave in a compressible medium.

*   **Acoustic Pressure ($p'$):** The small fluctuation in pressure above and below the ambient static pressure ($p_0$). $p = p_0 + p'$.
*   **Speed of Sound ($c$):** The velocity at which these infinitesimal pressure waves propagate. In air at 20°C, $c \approx 343$ m/s.
*   **Decibel Scale (dB):** Because acoustic pressure variations span many orders of magnitude, we use a logarithmic scale based on the Sound Pressure Level (SPL):
    $$ SPL = 20 \log_{10} \left( \frac{p_{rms}'}{p_{ref}} \right) $$
    Where $p_{ref} = 20 \, \mu\text{Pa}$ (the threshold of human hearing at 1 kHz).

## 2. Lighthill's Acoustic Analogy

In 1952, Sir James Lighthill formulated the foundation of aeroacoustics to understand the roaring noise of early jet engines. He manipulated the Navier-Stokes equations to look like the classical wave equation, but with a source term on the right-hand side.

Lighthill identified three fundamental types of aerodynamic sound sources:

**1. Monopoles (Mass injection):**
Sound generated by fluctuating volume or mass addition.
*   *Example:* A siren, a pulsing exhaust pipe, cavitation bubbles collapsing in a pump.
*   *Efficiency:* Very efficient sound radiators. Acoustic power scales with $U^4$ (velocity to the 4th power).

**2. Dipoles (Fluctuating forces on surfaces):**
Sound generated when unsteady flow interacts with a solid object, exerting a fluctuating force on it.
*   *Example:* Aeolian tones (wind whistling over a wire), helicopter rotor noise, trailing edge noise from a wind turbine blade or aircraft wing.
*   *Efficiency:* Moderately efficient. Acoustic power scales with $U^6$.

**3. Quadrupoles (Turbulence in free space):**
Sound generated purely by turbulent mixing and fluctuating shear stresses in the fluid itself, without any solid boundaries.
*   *Example:* The roaring noise of a jet engine exhaust plume mixing with ambient air.
*   *Efficiency:* Very inefficient at low speeds, but acoustic power scales with **$U^8$**. Because of the 8th-power scaling, quadrupole noise dominates at high jet velocities.

## 3. Jet Engine Noise Reduction

Lighthill's $U^8$ law for turbulent jet noise had a profound impact on aviation.

*   **Early Turbojets:** Exhausted a small mass of air at extremely high velocity ($U$). The $U^8$ scaling meant they were deafeningly loud.
*   **High-Bypass Turbofans:** The solution was to move a much larger mass of air at a much lower velocity to generate the same thrust. Because velocity decreased, the quadrupole noise dropped dramatically. Modern turbofans are remarkably quiet compared to 1960s jets.
*   **Chevrons:** The jagged trailing edges seen on the engine nacelles of the Boeing 787 and 747-8. They force the hot core exhaust and the cooler bypass air to mix more rapidly and smoothly, reducing the intensity of the turbulent quadrupole noise source.

## 4. Sonic Booms

A sonic boom is not a single "bang" that happens only when an aircraft breaks the sound barrier. It is a continuous acoustic phenomenon caused by the shock waves dragging along the ground continuously while the aircraft is flying supersonic.

*   **The N-Wave:** An aircraft generates a complex system of shocks (nose, canopy, wings, tail). As these shocks propagate through the atmosphere to the ground, they merge into a bow shock (compression) and a tail shock (recompression).
*   A person on the ground experiences an abrupt pressure rise (boom), a linear expansion to below atmospheric pressure, and a sudden jump back to atmospheric (second boom). This creates the characteristic "N-wave" pressure signature, heard as a double bang.
*   **Low-Boom Design:** NASA's X-59 QueSST (Quiet SuperSonic Technology) is an experimental aircraft shaped specifically to prevent its individual shock waves from coalescing into an N-wave. The goal is to produce a gentle "thump" instead of a boom, potentially allowing supersonic flight over land.
