# Converging-Diverging Nozzles

The converging-diverging (C-D) nozzle — also called the De Laval nozzle after Gustaf de Laval who used it in steam turbines in the 1880s — is the fundamental device for accelerating a compressible gas to supersonic speeds. Every liquid-propellant rocket engine, every solid-propellant booster, and every supersonic wind tunnel uses this geometry.

## 1. Operating Modes

The behavior of flow through a C-D nozzle depends critically on the ratio of back pressure (pressure at the nozzle exit, $P_b$) to chamber pressure ($P_0$). As $P_b$ decreases from $P_0$:

**Mode 1 — Subsonic Throughout ($P_b$ close to $P_0$):**
Flow accelerates through the converging section (Mach increases), reaches a peak velocity at the throat (but $M < 1$), and then decelerates in the diverging section. The diverging section acts as a diffuser. Mass flow is low.

**Mode 2 — Choked Flow, Subsonic in Diverging Section:**
As $P_b$ decreases, mass flow increases until the throat reaches $M = 1$ (choked). Any further reduction in $P_b$ cannot increase mass flow — the throat is choked. The flow in the diverging section is still subsonic (decelerating).

**Mode 3 — Choked with a Normal Shock in Diverging Section:**
$P_b$ is reduced further. The flow in the diverging section begins to accelerate supersonically, but the pressure at the exit is still too high. A **normal shock** forms inside the diverging section. Across this shock, the flow abruptly jumps from supersonic to subsonic with a large pressure rise. As $P_b$ decreases, the shock moves downstream toward the exit.

**Mode 4 — Normal Shock at the Exit:**
The shock has moved to the exit plane.

**Mode 5 — Overexpanded (Oblique Shocks Outside):**
$P_b$ has fallen below the design exit pressure but is still above it. The nozzle exits at a pressure below ambient, and oblique compression shocks form outside the nozzle to bring the flow back to ambient pressure. An "X" shaped shock pattern (Mach diamonds) forms in the plume.

**Mode 6 — Design Point (Fully Expanded):**
$P_b$ exactly equals the design exit pressure. The flow exits the nozzle supersonically and no shocks form. This is the optimal operating condition for a rocket nozzle.

**Mode 7 — Underexpanded (Expansion Waves Outside):**
$P_b$ has fallen below the design pressure. Flow exits above ambient pressure. Prandtl-Meyer expansion fans form outside the nozzle. Still no internal shocks, but the nozzle is over-sized for the ambient condition.

## 2. Critical Nozzle Area Ratio

The relationship between the exit area ($A_e$) and throat area ($A^*$) determines the design exit Mach number:

$$ \frac{A}{A^*} = \frac{1}{M} \left[\frac{2}{k+1}\left(1 + \frac{k-1}{2}M^2\right)\right]^{(k+1)/(2(k-1))} $$

This equation has two solutions for every area ratio $A/A^* > 1$: one subsonic and one supersonic. The designer chooses which regime by adjusting the back pressure.

**Example:** A nozzle with $A_e/A^* = 2.0$ can operate at $M_e \approx 0.31$ (subsonic) or $M_e \approx 2.20$ (supersonic).

## 3. Nozzle Thrust and Efficiency

The thrust generated by a nozzle is:
$$ F = \dot{m} V_e + (P_e - P_{amb}) A_e $$

*   **Momentum thrust:** $\dot{m} V_e$ — always present.
*   **Pressure thrust:** $(P_e - P_{amb}) A_e$ — positive if the nozzle is underexpanded ($P_e > P_{amb}$), zero at the design point, and negative if overexpanded.

**Nozzle efficiency** (coefficient of thrust) is maximized when $P_e = P_{amb}$ (perfect expansion). In practice, a rocket nozzle is designed for a specific altitude (e.g., sea level or vacuum) to maximize average thrust over the trajectory.

**Nozzle Losses:**
*   **Divergence loss:** The nozzle exit flow is not perfectly parallel — radial velocity components represent wasted momentum.
*   **Friction losses:** Viscous boundary layers on the nozzle walls.
*   **Combustion inefficiency:** Non-ideal chemical reactions.
*   **Flow non-uniformity and recirculation:** Near the chamber injector face.
