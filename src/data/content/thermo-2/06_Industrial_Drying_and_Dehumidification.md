# Industrial Drying and Dehumidification

Beyond human comfort, the control of moisture is critical in manufacturing, agriculture, pharmaceuticals, and food processing. Industrial drying consumes approximately 10–15% of all industrial energy worldwide, making it one of the most energy-intensive unit operations.

## 1. The Driving Force for Drying

Drying is the removal of moisture from a solid material by evaporation. The thermodynamic driving force is the difference in water vapor partial pressure between the surface of the wet material and the surrounding air.

*   If the air is very dry (low partial pressure of water vapor), moisture evaporates rapidly from the material surface.
*   If the air is nearly saturated, evaporation slows and eventually stops.

This is why you can dry laundry outdoors on a dry windy day but not on a humid, still day.

## 2. The Drying Rate Curve

When a wet solid is placed in a stream of hot, dry air, the drying process proceeds in two distinct phases:

**Constant Rate Period:**
*   The surface of the material is saturated with liquid water (the material is wet enough that liquid migrates to the surface as fast as it evaporates).
*   Drying occurs entirely at the surface, like evaporation from a free water surface.
*   The surface temperature equals the wet-bulb temperature of the drying air.
*   The drying rate (kg water removed / hour) is constant.
*   This period ends at the **Critical Moisture Content** — the point where the surface can no longer be kept saturated by liquid migrating from the interior.

**Falling Rate Period:**
*   Below the critical moisture content, the liquid surface recedes into the solid.
*   Water must now both travel to the surface (by diffusion, capillary action, or vapor transport) AND evaporate at the surface.
*   The rate of internal moisture transport becomes the limiting step.
*   The drying rate continuously decreases.
*   This is the slow, expensive phase of drying — and the phase where most industrial dryer design effort focuses.

## 3. Types of Industrial Dryers

**1. Spray Dryers:**
Hot drying gas and liquid feed are both sprayed into a large chamber. Tiny liquid droplets (100–300 µm) are created by an atomizer. Their enormous surface-area-to-volume ratio causes instantaneous evaporation (1–10 seconds).
*   *Products:* Milk powder, instant coffee, pharmaceuticals, ceramics powders.
*   The outlet temperature must be carefully controlled to avoid heat damage to sensitive products.

**2. Rotary Dryers:**
A slightly inclined, slowly rotating cylinder (drum). Wet material (granules, coal, minerals) enters at the high end. As the drum rotates, internal flights (scoops) lift and shower the material through a hot airstream. Material exits at the low end after 15–60 minutes.
*   Robust, handles abrasive and coarse materials.
*   High energy consumption.

**3. Fluidized Bed Dryers:**
Drying air is forced upward through a bed of granular solid at a velocity high enough to suspend ("fluidize") the particles. The intense mixing and surface renewal provide extremely uniform drying and high heat transfer.
*   *Products:* Pharmaceutical tablets, food granules, fertilizers.
*   Gentle on fragile particles.

**4. Freeze Dryers (Lyophilizers):**
The material is first frozen, then placed under vacuum. The ice sublimes directly to vapor (bypassing the liquid phase). The product is dried at temperatures below 0°C, perfectly preserving fragile biological structure, flavor, and nutrition.
*   *Products:* Freeze-dried coffee, pharmaceutical proteins and vaccines, military rations.
*   Extremely energy-intensive and slow, but produces the highest-quality dried product.

## 4. Desiccant Dehumidification

For industrial applications requiring very low humidity (< 20% RH) — impossible with refrigeration-based cooling — desiccant systems are used.

A **desiccant** is a hygroscopic material that adsorbs water vapor by chemical affinity (not evaporation). Common desiccants include silica gel, molecular sieves, and lithium chloride.

Rotary desiccant wheels work similarly to rotary heat regenerators: one sector adsorbs moisture from the process air stream; the other sector is "regenerated" (the adsorbed water is driven off) by passing hot air (80–150°C) through it.

*   *Applications:* Pharmaceutical cleanrooms, semiconductor fabrication, food storage, museum preservation.
