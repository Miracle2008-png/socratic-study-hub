# District Energy and Thermal Storage

As cities look to decarbonize their energy infrastructure, district-scale heating and cooling systems — combined with thermal energy storage — offer compelling efficiency and flexibility advantages over individual building-level systems.

## 1. District Heating

District heating distributes thermal energy (hot water or steam) from a central plant through insulated underground pipes to multiple buildings. Instead of each building having its own boiler, a single large efficient plant serves dozens or hundreds of buildings.

**Generations of District Heating:**

| Generation | Medium | Supply Temp | Era |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1st | Steam | >200°C | Pre-1930s |
| 2nd | Pressurized water | 100–150°C | 1930s–1970s |
| 3rd | Hot water | 60–100°C | 1980s–2010s |
| 4th (4DH) | Low-temperature water | 40–70°C | 2010s–present |

**Fourth-Generation District Heating (4DH):**
Low supply temperatures are key to unlocking new heat sources:
*   Heat pumps can supply 60°C efficiently (COP = 3–4), extracting heat from rivers, sewage water, or the ground.
*   Industrial waste heat at 40–60°C (data centers, food processing) can be utilized.
*   Solar thermal collectors work well at 60–80°C.
*   Buildings with low-temperature heating systems (underfloor heating, oversized radiators) are compatible.

In Denmark, district heating serves >65% of all buildings. It is a cornerstone of their nearly carbon-neutral electricity grid.

## 2. District Cooling

The logical extension of district heating — a central chilled water plant distributes cooling to multiple buildings.

**Advantages over individual building chillers:**
*   Economy of scale: Larger chillers are far more efficient than small rooftop units.
*   Eliminates hundreds of rooftop condensers and their cooling towers from individual buildings.
*   Enables large-scale thermal energy storage (ice tanks) that shifts cooling load off the peak electricity demand period.

## 3. Thermal Energy Storage (TES)

Thermal energy storage decouples *when* you generate cooling (or heating) from *when* it is needed. This provides enormous operational flexibility and cost savings.

**Chilled Water Storage:**
Large insulated tanks (5,000–100,000 m³) of chilled water. Chillers run at night (cheap electricity, cool ambient) to fill the tank. During the day (expensive peak electricity, hot ambient), the tank supplies most of the cooling load without running chillers.

**Ice Storage:**
Water is frozen to ice overnight by chillers. Ice stores ~334 kJ/kg of latent heat — 80× more energy per degree of temperature change than liquid water. Much smaller tank volume needed than chilled water storage for the same cooling energy. The chillers run at -6 to -8°C (to make ice) rather than 6°C — reducing chiller COP. But the electricity cost savings and demand charge reduction typically outweigh the efficiency penalty.

**Phase Change Materials (PCMs):**
Materials engineered to melt/freeze at specific temperatures (e.g., 18°C for space cooling, 58°C for solar thermal storage). Can be incorporated directly into building materials (PCM-impregnated wallboard) for distributed distributed thermal mass.

## 4. Seasonal Thermal Energy Storage (STES)

The ultimate thermal storage challenge: storing summer solar heat for winter heating, or storing winter cold for summer cooling.

**Aquifer Thermal Energy Storage (ATES):**
Two groundwater wells — warm and cold — are alternately charged and discharged seasonally. In summer, the building is cooled by pumping groundwater through a heat exchanger; the now-warm water is injected into the warm well. In winter, the warm well supplies heating; the now-cooled water goes to the cold well.

Used extensively in the Netherlands and Scandinavia. Stockholm's Arlanda Airport and many university campuses use ATES systems, covering up to 80% of their annual heating and cooling from ground-stored solar energy.
