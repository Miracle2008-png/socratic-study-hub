# Screening Criteria and Economics of EOR

A petroleum engineer has three powerful weapons in the Enhanced Oil Recovery arsenal: Thermal, Chemical, and Miscible Gas. 

However, you cannot simply choose a method based on preference. If you inject expensive Surfactant into a reservoir that is too hot, the chemicals will instantly vaporize. If you inject $CO_2$ into a reservoir that is too shallow, the gas will refuse to dissolve. 

Selecting the correct EOR method is a rigid, mathematical process governed by **Screening Criteria** and brutal **Economics**. 

## 1. Technical Screening Criteria

Before spending \$100 million on a pilot project, the engineer checks the physical parameters of the reservoir against hard scientific limits.

### A. Fluid Viscosity (API Gravity)
*   **Heavy Oil (< 20 API, > 100 cP):** The oil is too thick. Chemical and Gas will finger violently and fail. **Thermal Methods (Steam, SAGD)** are the only physically viable option to melt the viscosity.
*   **Medium/Light Oil (> 25 API, < 10 cP):** Thermal is a waste of energy. The oil is already mobile. The trap is capillary forces. **Miscible Gas ($CO_2$) or Chemical (ASP)** are the prime candidates.

### B. Reservoir Depth and Pressure
*   **Deep Reservoirs (> 5,000 feet):** Thermal (Steam) is impossible; the steam will lose all its heat to the cold earth while traveling 5,000 feet down the steel pipe. However, deep reservoirs have massive natural pressure. This high pressure easily exceeds the MMP, making **Miscible Gas ($CO_2$)** perfect.
*   **Shallow Reservoirs (< 3,000 feet):** The natural rock pressure is very low. You cannot safely inject $CO_2$ at 3,000 psi into a shallow rock; you will fracture the earth and the gas will vent to the surface. You cannot achieve miscibility. **Chemical Methods (Polymer/Surfactant)** or **Thermal Methods** are required.

### C. Reservoir Temperature
*   **High Temperature (> $200^\circ\text{F}$):** Complex synthetic polymers and fragile surfactant molecules will literally cook, break down, and disintegrate in the extreme heat. Chemical EOR is eliminated. **Miscible Gas ($CO_2$)** is required.
*   **Low Temperature (< $150^\circ\text{F}$):** Perfect for delicate **Chemical EOR (ASP)**.

### D. Rock Lithology and Salinity
*   **High Salinity Water (> 100,000 ppm):** Salt violently destroys standard HPAM polymers and deactivates surfactants. If the native water is hyper-saline, Chemical EOR is extremely difficult and expensive.
*   **Carbonate Rock (Limestone/Dolomite):** Carbonate rock has a strong positive electrical charge. It aggressively absorbs and destroys anionic surfactants. Chemical EOR is very difficult. **Miscible Gas ($CO_2$)** is usually preferred.

## 2. The Brutal Economics of EOR

Even if a project is technically flawless, it might be an economic disaster. EOR projects are highly sensitive to the global price of crude oil because the operational costs (OPEX) are astronomical.

1.  **The Front-Loaded Capital (CAPEX):** Before you produce a single drop of EOR oil, you must spend hundreds of millions of dollars building massive surface infrastructure. 
    *   *Thermal:* Building massive natural gas boilers and water treatment plants.
    *   *Chemical:* Building massive chemical mixing vats and logistics networks to truck thousands of tons of hazardous chemicals to the middle of the desert.
    *   *Miscible:* Building a 300-mile high-pressure pipeline to deliver the $CO_2$, and a massive \$50 million compressor station to recycle the gas.
2.  **The Time Delay:** You inject \$10 million of Surfactant today. It takes 12 to 18 months for that chemical slug to travel through the rock and push the oil bank to the producing well. The company must survive two years of massive negative cash flow before the first barrel of oil is sold.
3.  **The Cost per Barrel:** A primary flowing well might cost \$5 to produce a barrel of oil. An advanced ASP Chemical flood might cost \$35 in pure chemical and pumping costs to produce one barrel. 
    *   If the global price of oil drops from \$80 to \$40 a barrel, the primary well still makes a profit. The EOR project instantly loses money on every single barrel and must be immediately shut down.

Because of this extreme economic risk, engineers use advanced Reservoir Simulation supercomputers to run thousands of complex financial "What-If" scenarios to absolutely guarantee profitability before the Board of Directors signs the \$500 million check.
