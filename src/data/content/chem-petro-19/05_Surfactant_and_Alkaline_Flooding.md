# Surfactant and Alkaline Flooding

Polymer flooding fixes the Macroscopic sweep (viscous fingering), but it does absolutely nothing to the Microscopic sweep. It cannot force the capillary-trapped residual oil droplets ($S_{or}$) out of the microscopic pore throats.

To mobilize the residual oil, you must increase the Capillary Number by a factor of 1,000. To do this, you must drop the Interfacial Tension (IFT) between the oil and water from 30 dynes/cm down to an ultra-low 0.001 dynes/cm. 

To destroy IFT, engineers use industrial soaps: **Surfactants** and **Alkalines**.

## 1. The Chemistry of Surfactants

"Surfactant" is short for Surface Active Agent. They are exactly the same chemicals used in laundry detergent to remove grease from clothes.

A surfactant molecule is a chemical bridge. It is shaped like a tadpole:
*   **The Head:** Is highly polar and **Hydrophilic** (it loves water and hates oil).
*   **The Tail:** Is a long hydrocarbon chain that is **Lipophilic** (it loves oil and hates water).

When you inject a massive slug of water mixed with 1% Surfactant into the reservoir, the surfactant molecules rush directly to the boundary (the interface) between the trapped oil droplets and the water. 
The molecules embed themselves on the boundary, with their heads in the water and their tails in the oil. This violently disrupts the rigid chemical skin (IFT). 
The rigid oil droplet instantly becomes hyper-flexible. The flowing water easily pushes the floppy, deformed oil droplet right through the microscopic pore throat, sweeping the rock completely clean.

## 2. The Nightmare of Surfactant Retention

If surfactants are so effective, why are they rarely used alone? Because they are astronomically expensive, and the rock actively destroys the project.

Deep reservoir rock (especially sandstone and clay) has a natural electrical charge on its surface. Surfactant molecules also have an electrical charge.
As the expensive surfactant slug slowly moves through the rock, the molecules are electrically attracted to the rock walls. They stick to the rock permanently. This is called **Adsorption**.

Within the first few hundred feet of the injection well, the rock acts like a massive chemical sponge, absorbing 100% of the surfactant out of the water. The water continues across the field, but it is now just plain water. The \$20 million chemical slug was completely consumed by the dirt.

## 3. Alkaline Flooding (The Cheap Solution)

To solve the cost and adsorption problems, engineers invented Alkaline Flooding. 

Instead of buying expensive synthetic soap, you simply pump incredibly cheap, highly caustic, high-pH chemicals (like Sodium Carbonate or Sodium Hydroxide) into the ground.

1.  **In-Situ Saponification:** Heavy crude oils naturally contain complex organic naphthenic acids. When the high-pH Alkaline water hits the crude oil deep underground, an intense chemical reaction occurs. The alkaline reacts with the organic acids to literally **manufacture soap in-situ** (directly on the boundary of the oil droplet). This naturally generated surfactant drops the IFT and frees the oil.
2.  **Rock Neutralization:** The harsh alkaline violently attacks the rock walls, changing the electrical charge of the clay minerals. This stops the rock from absorbing chemicals.

*The Flaw:* Alkaline flooding only works if the crude oil contains a massive amount of natural acids. If it is a light, sweet crude, there are no acids to react with, and the Alkaline water does absolutely nothing.

## 4. ASP Flooding (The Ultimate Synergy)

Modern Chemical EOR rarely uses one chemical. It uses all three simultaneously in an **Alkaline-Surfactant-Polymer (ASP) Flood**.

1.  **Alkaline:** Injected first. It neutralizes the electrical charge of the rock and generates a small amount of natural soap.
2.  **Surfactant:** Because the rock is neutralized by the alkaline, the expensive synthetic Surfactant is not absorbed by the rock. It travels deep into the reservoir and drops the IFT to ultra-low levels, freeing the trapped oil droplets.
3.  **Polymer:** The entire chemical slug is heavily thickened with Polymer. This ensures the slug moves as a solid wall, preventing it from fingering through the oil and ensuring it contacts every single inch of the reservoir.

ASP is the most complex fluid operation in the world. When engineered perfectly, the rock is left looking like it was scrubbed with bleach, and the recovery factor can exceed 70%.
