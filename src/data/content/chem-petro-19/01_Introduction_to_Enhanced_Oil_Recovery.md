# Introduction to Enhanced Oil Recovery

When an oil well is first drilled, the immense natural pressure of the deep earth pushes the oil to the surface. This is **Primary Recovery**, and it typically only extracts 10% to 20% of the original oil in place (OOIP).

When the pressure dies, engineers pump massive oceans of water or natural gas into the ground to physically push the oil toward the producing wells. This is **Secondary Recovery** (Waterflooding or Pressure Maintenance), and it can extract another 10% to 20%.

After 30 years of waterflooding, the well hits its economic limit. The water breaks through, and the well produces 98% water and 2% oil. At this exact moment, **up to 60% of the original oil is still sitting in the ground**, permanently trapped.

This remaining, massive volume of trapped oil is the target of **Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR)** (sometimes called Tertiary Recovery).

## 1. The Physics of Trapped Oil

Why doesn't the waterflood sweep all the oil out? The oil is trapped by two distinct, violent physical forces:

### 1. Viscous Forces (The Macro Problem)
If the crude oil is heavy and thick like molasses (e.g., 1,000 cP viscosity), and the injected water is thin and fast (1 cP viscosity), the water will refuse to act like a solid wall. Instead, the water will violently stab through the thick oil in chaotic spikes called "Viscous Fingers." The water races to the producing well, completely bypassing 70% of the reservoir rock. The oil is left behind simply because the water refused to push it.

### 2. Capillary Forces (The Micro Problem)
Even in the exact areas where the water *did* sweep through the rock, it leaves oil behind. 
Oil and water hate each other (they are immiscible). In the microscopic pore throats of the rock (which are the size of a human hair), the boundary between the oil and water acts like a rigid, tense rubber band. This is **Interfacial Tension (IFT)**.
When the water tries to push a microscopic oil droplet through a tiny pore throat, the rigid rubber band of the oil droplet gets stuck. The physical force required to squeeze the droplet through the bottleneck is millions of times stronger than the physical push of the flowing water. The oil droplet is permanently, chemically locked in place as "Residual Oil" ($S_{or}$).

## 2. The Three Pillars of EOR

To get the trapped 60% of the oil out, you cannot just pump more water. You must fundamentally alter the chemistry and thermodynamics of the fluid itself. 

EOR is categorized into three primary disciplines:

1.  **Thermal Methods:** Used for Heavy Oil. By injecting massive amounts of heat (Steam), the viscosity of the heavy tar is destroyed. It melts into a thin liquid, completely solving the Viscous Fingering problem. 
    *   *Techniques:* Cyclic Steam Stimulation (CSS), Steam Flooding, SAGD, In-Situ Combustion.
2.  **Chemical Methods:** Used for Light Oil trapped by capillary forces. By injecting exotic industrial chemicals (Soaps and Polymers), engineers can chemically destroy the Interfacial Tension (melting the rigid rubber band) or artificially thicken the water to prevent viscous fingering.
    *   *Techniques:* Polymer Flooding, Surfactant Flooding, Alkaline Flooding (ASP).
3.  **Miscible Gas Injection:** Used for deep, Light Oil. By injecting a gas at astronomical pressures ($CO_2$, Methane, Nitrogen), the gas physically dissolves perfectly into the crude oil. Because the two fluids merge into one phase, Interfacial Tension drops to absolute zero, completely eliminating the Capillary trapping forces.

## 3. The Economics of EOR

If EOR can recover billions of barrels of trapped oil, why isn't every oil well an EOR project?

**Cost.** 
Primary recovery is practically free (the earth does the work). Secondary waterflooding is cheap (saltwater is free).
EOR requires building massive, multi-million dollar chemical plants, boiling oceans of water into steam, or laying 500-mile pipelines just to deliver liquid $CO_2$. 
The chemicals and energy required for EOR are so expensive that the projects are only economically viable when the global price of crude oil is consistently high. If oil drops below \$50 a barrel, almost all Chemical and Thermal EOR projects are instantly shut down because the chemical costs more than the oil it recovers.
