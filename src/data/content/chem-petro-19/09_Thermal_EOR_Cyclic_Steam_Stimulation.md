# Thermal EOR: Cyclic Steam Stimulation

If you discover a massive oil field in California or Canada, but the oil is an incredibly thick, black tar with a viscosity of 100,000 cP, you have a massive problem.

At 100,000 cP, the oil is practically a solid at room temperature. You cannot pump water into the ground to push it; the water will instantly finger through. You cannot pump $CO_2$; the thick oil will block it. 
The oil simply refuses to flow into the wellbore. 

The only way to produce this oil is to fundamentally change its physical state. You must melt it. This requires **Thermal Enhanced Oil Recovery**.

## 1. The Physics of Heat and Viscosity

Unlike water, the viscosity of heavy hydrocarbon liquid is incredibly sensitive to temperature. The relationship is exponential, not linear.

A heavy crude oil might have a viscosity of 100,000 cP at $50^\circ\text{C}$ (solid asphalt). 
If you inject massive amounts of heat and raise the rock temperature to $200^\circ\text{C}$, the complex hydrocarbon chains become highly energetic. The viscosity plummets exponentially to 5 cP. The solid asphalt instantly transforms into a highly mobile, flowing liquid, as thin as warm maple syrup.

To deliver this massive amount of heat deep underground, engineers do not use electric heaters (they are too weak). They use **Steam**.
Steam carries an astronomical amount of Latent Heat energy. When steam hits the cold rock underground, it condenses back into liquid water. At that exact moment of condensation, it violently releases all of its latent heat directly into the rock and the oil.

## 2. Cyclic Steam Stimulation (CSS)

The absolute simplest and most common thermal method is applied to a single, individual well. It is called **Cyclic Steam Stimulation (CSS)**, or colloquially, "Huff and Puff."

It is a three-stage, repeating cycle:

### 1. The Huff (Injection Phase)
A massive, natural-gas-fired boiler on the surface generates high-pressure, $300^\circ\text{C}$ boiling steam. This steam is pumped violently straight down the wellbore and injected into the heavy oil rock for 2 to 4 weeks. 
Because the oil is solid, the steam cannot travel far. It only heats a small, 50-foot radius of rock immediately surrounding the wellbore.

### 2. The Soak (Heating Phase)
The massive surface valves are slammed shut, completely sealing the well. The well sits perfectly quiet for 3 to 7 days.
During this time, the massive thermal energy of the steam conducts outward. It literally bakes the surrounding rock, melting the solid heavy tar into a thin, hot liquid.

### 3. The Puff (Production Phase)
The surface valves are opened, and a massive mechanical pump is turned on. 
The now-mobile, $200^\circ\text{C}$ liquid oil easily flows out of the hot rock, into the wellbore, and is pumped to the surface.
The well will produce heavily for 3 to 6 months. 
Eventually, the deep rock slowly cools down. The oil begins to freeze back into a solid tar, and the flow rate dies. 
At this point, the cycle is restarted. The engineer pumps more steam (Huff), lets it sit (Soak), and produces it again (Puff).

## 3. The Flaws of CSS

CSS is brilliant because it is cheap. You only need one well. You do not have to worry about pushing the oil across the entire field. 

However, it has massive physical limitations:
1.  **Limited Radius:** Heat conduction through solid rock is incredibly slow. The heat will never reach the oil sitting 200 feet away from the well. Therefore, CSS leaves massive amounts of oil completely untouched between the wells. The recovery factor is usually only 10% to 20%.
2.  **Casing Destruction:** The wellbore is a steel pipe cemented into solid rock. When you pump $300^\circ\text{C}$ steam down a 2,000-foot steel pipe, the steel violently thermally expands. The pipe literally stretches by several feet. When the well cools down during the production phase, the steel violently shrinks. This continuous, violent stretching and shrinking will eventually rip the steel casing apart, destroying the well. Engineers must use highly specialized "Thermal Cements" and prestressed steel to survive the cycle.
