# Turboprops and Turboshafts

While turbojets and turbofans produce thrust directly via a high-velocity exhaust jet, turboprops and turboshafts use the gas turbine core primarily to generate mechanical shaft power. This shaft power is then used to drive a propeller, a helicopter rotor, or even a tank transmission.

## 1. The Turboprop Engine

A turboprop engine couples a gas turbine core to a conventional airplane propeller.

**How it works:**
1.  The core (compressor, combustor, turbine) operates identically to a turbojet, generating a stream of hot, high-pressure gas.
2.  Instead of passing this gas immediately through a nozzle to create thrust, the gas is expanded through additional turbine stages (the power turbine).
3.  The power turbine extracts almost all the available energy from the exhaust stream, converting it into rotational mechanical energy on a shaft.
4.  This shaft drives a heavy-duty **reduction gearbox**. Because gas turbines spin efficiently at very high speeds (e.g., 20,000 - 40,000 RPM) and propellers must spin at low speeds (to keep the tips subsonic, typically 1,000 - 2,000 RPM), the gearbox is an absolutely critical, heavy, and complex component of a turboprop.
5.  The residual exhaust gas exiting the nozzle provides only a small fraction (typically 5-10%) of the total thrust.

**Advantages:**
- **Exceptional low-speed efficiency:** At speeds below roughly Mach 0.6 (400 mph), a propeller is vastly more efficient at moving a large mass of air than any jet exhaust. Turboprops are highly fuel-efficient for regional, short-haul flights.
- **Takeoff and Short Field Performance:** Propellers generate massive static thrust at zero airspeed, giving turboprops excellent acceleration and short-takeoff-and-landing (STOL) capabilities.
- **Reverse Thrust:** The propeller pitch can be reversed immediately upon touchdown to act as a powerful aerodynamic brake.

**Disadvantages:**
- **Speed Limit:** As the aircraft approaches Mach 0.6 - 0.7, the propeller tips begin to hit supersonic speeds, creating shockwaves that destroy efficiency and generate immense noise. Turboprops are generally not viable for high-subsonic commercial flight.
- **Gearbox Complexity:** The reduction gearbox is heavy, requires extensive lubrication and cooling, and is a major maintenance item.

## 2. The Turboshaft Engine

A turboshaft engine is conceptually identical to a turboprop, but instead of driving an airplane propeller, the output shaft drives something else—most commonly the main transmission of a helicopter.

**Key Features:**
- **Free Power Turbine:** Most modern turboshafts (and many turboprops) use a "free turbine" design. There is no physical shaft connecting the gas generator core (compressor + its driving turbine) to the power turbine. The only connection is aerodynamic—the hot gas flowing from one to the other.
- **Advantage of a Free Turbine:** This allows the helicopter rotor (driven by the power turbine) to spin at a constant, governed speed, while the gas generator core can speed up or slow down independently to meet changing power demands. It also makes starting the engine much easier, as the starter motor only needs to spin the lightweight gas generator core, not the massive helicopter rotor system.

**Applications:**
- **Helicopters:** The dominant power source for medium to heavy helicopters due to their incredibly high power-to-weight ratio compared to piston engines.
- **Marine:** Used to power fast ferries, naval destroyers, and hovercraft.
- **Tanks:** The M1 Abrams main battle tank uses a 1,500 horsepower Honeywell AGT1500 turboshaft engine.
- **APUs:** Auxiliary Power Units in commercial airliners are small turboshaft engines located in the tail, used to run generators for electrical power and bleed air for cabin conditioning and starting the main engines.
