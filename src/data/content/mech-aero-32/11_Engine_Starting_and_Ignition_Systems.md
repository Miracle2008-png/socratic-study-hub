# Engine Starting and Ignition Systems

Unlike a piston engine that can be started with a small electric motor turning a crankshaft, starting a gas turbine is a complex sequence requiring the precise coordination of airflow, fuel delivery, and ignition. A jet engine is not self-sustaining until the compressor is spinning fast enough to provide sufficient high-pressure air to the combustor.

## 1. The Starting Sequence

The basic sequence to start a jet engine follows these steps:

1.  **Rotation (Motoring):** An external power source physically spins the engine's high-pressure spool (the shaft connecting the HPC and HPT). Air begins to flow through the engine.
2.  **Ignition On:** Once the compressor reaches a specific RPM (ensuring enough airflow so that fuel won't just pool in the combustor and cause a catastrophic fire), the high-energy igniter plugs are turned on.
3.  **Fuel On:** Shortly after ignition is active, the fuel valves open, spraying atomized fuel into the combustion chamber.
4.  **Light-off:** The fuel-air mixture ignites. Exhaust gas temperature (EGT) rises rapidly.
5.  **Acceleration:** The hot exhaust gas drives the turbine, which now starts assisting the starter motor in spinning the compressor.
6.  **Starter Cutout:** Once the engine reaches "self-sustaining speed" (the point where the turbine extracts enough power to drive the compressor entirely on its own, overcoming internal friction), the starter motor is disengaged, and the ignition is turned off. The combustion is now a continuous, self-sustaining flame.

## 2. Starter Systems

The starter must be powerful enough to accelerate the heavy compressor and turbine assemblies to self-sustaining speed (often around 20-30% of maximum RPM).

**1. Pneumatic Starters (Air Turbine Starters):**
The most common system on commercial airliners.
- A small air turbine is connected via a clutch to the engine's accessory gearbox.
- High-pressure air is routed to this starter, spinning it at high speed, which turns the engine.
- The high-pressure air usually comes from the aircraft's Auxiliary Power Unit (APU) located in the tail, or from a ground support cart (a "huffer" cart).
- Once the first engine is started, "bleed air" from that running engine can be used to start the other engines (a cross-bleed start).

**2. Electric Starters:**
Used on smaller engines, regional jets, and APUs.
- A heavy-duty electric motor draws power from the aircraft battery or ground power.
- On some modern aircraft (like the Boeing 787, which uses a "more electric" architecture, eliminating pneumatic bleed air systems), massive electric starter/generators are used. They act as motors to start the engine, and then switch to acting as generators to provide electrical power to the aircraft once the engine is running.

## 3. Ignition Systems

Jet engine igniters look like spark plugs on steroids.

- Because jet fuel (kerosene) is difficult to ignite, the ignition system must deliver massive, high-energy sparks (often measured in Joules per spark, emitting a loud "snap").
- A typical engine has two independent ignition systems and two igniter plugs located in the combustor.
- **Continuous Ignition:** Unlike a car engine, the igniters in a jet engine are usually turned off once the engine is running—the flame is self-sustaining. However, pilots can select "Continuous Ignition" during heavy rain, severe turbulence, or icing conditions. This keeps the igniters firing constantly to ensure the engine immediately relights if a massive ingestion of water or ice temporarily extinguishes the flame (a flameout).

## 4. Hot Starts and Hung Starts

Starting an engine requires monitoring to prevent severe damage.
- **Hot Start:** Too much fuel relative to the airflow. The EGT rises uncontrollably and will quickly exceed the melting point of the turbine blades. The start must be immediately aborted by shutting off the fuel.
- **Hung Start:** The engine lights off, but fails to accelerate to self-sustaining idle speed, stagnating at a low RPM. Often caused by a weak starter or low fuel scheduling.
