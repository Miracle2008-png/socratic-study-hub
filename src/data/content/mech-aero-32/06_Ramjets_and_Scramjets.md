# Ramjets and Scramjets

Ramjets and Scramjets represent the simplest and most extreme forms of air-breathing jet propulsion. They contain no moving parts—no compressor blades to compress air, and no turbine blades to extract power. They rely entirely on the high forward speed of the vehicle to ram air into the engine and compress it.

## 1. The Ramjet

A ramjet operates on the Brayton cycle, just like a turbojet, but it achieves compression aerodynamically rather than mechanically.

**How it works:**
1.  **Compression (The Inlet):** As the vehicle flies at supersonic speeds, high-velocity air enters the specially shaped inlet. Through a series of shock waves and a divergent duct, the air is decelerated to *subsonic* speeds. This drastic reduction in kinetic energy translates into a massive increase in static pressure and temperature (the "ram effect").
2.  **Combustion:** Fuel is injected into this high-pressure, subsonic air and burned.
3.  **Expansion (The Nozzle):** The hot, expanding gases are accelerated through a convergent-divergent (CD) nozzle back to supersonic speeds, exiting faster than the incoming air to produce thrust.

**Characteristics:**
- **Zero Static Thrust:** Because it relies on forward speed for compression, a ramjet cannot produce thrust at rest. It must be boosted to a high speed (typically Mach 2.5 to 3.0) by another propulsion system (like a solid rocket booster or a carrier aircraft) before the ramjet can "start" and take over.
- **Operating Range:** Ramjets are highly efficient between Mach 3 and Mach 5.
- **Limitations:** As speed pushes past Mach 5, the air entering the combustor is compressed so intensely that its temperature rises close to the structural melting points of the engine, and near the dissociation temperature of the combustion products (where adding fuel doesn't increase temperature, the heat just breaks the chemical bonds apart).

## 2. The Scramjet (Supersonic Combustion Ramjet)

To fly faster than Mach 5 (hypersonic flight), engineers developed the scramjet. The fundamental difference between a ramjet and a scramjet lies in the speed of the air flowing through the combustion chamber.

- In a **ramjet**, incoming supersonic air is slowed to *subsonic* speeds for combustion.
- In a **scramjet**, the incoming hypersonic air is slowed down to compress it, but it remains at *supersonic* speeds as it passes through the entire engine, including the combustion chamber.

**How it works (and why it's so difficult):**
1.  **Supersonic Combustion:** Burning fuel in a supersonic airflow is incredibly difficult. It has been compared to keeping a match lit in a hurricane. The fuel must be injected, mixed with the air, ignited, and burned in the few milliseconds it takes the air to traverse the engine.
2.  **Thermal Management:** The friction and shock wave heating at Mach 6+ are extreme. Scramjets often use their own fuel (like liquid hydrogen) as a coolant. The cold fuel is pumped through channels in the engine walls to keep them from melting before it is injected into the combustor.
3.  **Vehicle Integration:** At hypersonic speeds, the entire underbody of the aircraft acts as the engine inlet (generating the pre-compression shock waves), and the aft body acts as the expansion nozzle. The engine and airframe cannot be designed separately; they are highly integrated.

**Applications:**
Scramjets are currently highly experimental. They promise the ability to fly globally in just a few hours or to act as the first stage for highly efficient single-stage-to-orbit spaceplanes. Notable demonstrators include NASA's X-43 (Mach 9.6) and the Boeing X-51 Waverider.
