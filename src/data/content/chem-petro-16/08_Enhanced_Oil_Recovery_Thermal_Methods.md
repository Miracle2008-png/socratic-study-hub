# Enhanced Oil Recovery: Thermal Methods

When a conventional waterflood runs its course and reaches its economic limit, up to 70% of the oil is often left in the ground. 
If the oil left behind is an incredibly thick, heavy, tar-like crude (like the oil sands of Canada or the heavy oil fields of California), the problem is almost entirely **Viscosity**. 

Heavy oil simply refuses to flow through the microscopic pore throats of the rock. To get it out, you must change its physical properties. You must add heat.
This is **Thermal EOR**.

## 1. The Physics of Heat and Viscosity

Unlike water, the viscosity of heavy hydrocarbon liquid is incredibly sensitive to temperature.
A heavy crude oil might have a viscosity of 100,000 cP at $50^\circ\text{C}$ (it is essentially solid, like cold asphalt). If you heat that exact same oil to $200^\circ\text{C}$, its viscosity plummets exponentially to 5 cP (it becomes as thin as warm maple syrup).

If you can successfully inject massive amounts of heat into a rock buried a mile underground, you can instantly turn a solid block of tar into a highly mobile, flowing liquid.

## 2. Cyclic Steam Stimulation (CSS)

Also known as "Huff and Puff." This is the simplest thermal method, applied to a single well.

1.  **Huff (Injection):** Massive surface boilers generate high-pressure, $300^\circ\text{C}$ steam. The steam is pumped down the wellbore and injected violently into the heavy oil rock for 2 to 4 weeks. The rock surrounding the wellbore absorbs the heat.
2.  **Soak:** The well is shut in and sealed for several days. The heat conducts outward, melting the heavy tar into thin liquid.
3.  **Puff (Production):** The well is opened. The now-mobile, hot oil flows easily into the wellbore and is pumped to the surface for several months. 

Once the rock eventually cools down and the oil turns back into solid tar, the production dies, and the cycle is repeated. 
*The Catch:* The heat only penetrates a radius of maybe 100 feet around the well. It cannot sweep the oil between the wells.

## 3. Steam Flooding

This is a continuous, field-wide operation similar to waterflooding, but using boiling steam.
*   Continuous injection of high-pressure steam into central injection wells.
*   The steam forms a massive, growing bubble of $250^\circ\text{C}$ gas that slowly expands outward through the rock.
*   As the steam hits the cold, virgin oil ahead of it, the steam condenses into boiling water, instantly melting the heavy oil and pushing it forcefully toward the producing wells.

*The Catch:* Steam is a gas. It is incredibly light. It suffers massively from **Gravity Override**. The steam will instantly float to the very top of the 100-foot-thick reservoir, travel rapidly along the ceiling, and hit the producing well, leaving the bottom 80 feet of the oil completely cold and untouched.

## 4. Steam Assisted Gravity Drainage (SAGD)

SAGD (pronounced "Sag-Dee") is the pinnacle of modern thermal engineering, invented specifically to solve the Gravity Override problem in the Canadian oil sands.

It relies on horizontal drilling.
1.  Engineers drill two horizontal wells, perfectly parallel, exactly 5 meters apart, stacked vertically. (One well is 5m directly above the other, both extending for a mile inside the solid tar sand).
2.  High-pressure steam is continuously injected into the **Top Well**.
3.  Because steam naturally floats up, the steam bubble expands upward and outward, melting the solid tar above it into a thin liquid.
4.  The melted, hot oil (and the condensed water) are now heavy liquids. Gravity pulls them straight down. 
5.  The hot liquids rain down through the rock and fall directly into the **Bottom Well**, which pumps them to the surface.

SAGD creates a massive, continuously growing "steam chamber" that systematically melts the reservoir from the top down, achieving incredible recovery factors (up to 60%) in reservoirs that were historically impossible to produce.

## 5. In-Situ Combustion (Fireflooding)

If steam is too expensive to generate, why not just set the reservoir on fire?

In a Fireflood, engineers inject massive amounts of highly compressed pure oxygen (or air) into the injection well and literally ignite the oil two miles underground. 
*   A massive wall of fire (reaching $600^\circ\text{C}$) slowly burns its way through the porous rock.
*   The fire completely consumes the heaviest, worst parts of the crude oil as fuel (coke).
*   The intense heat creates a massive wall of boiling water and vaporized light oil that acts as a steam-cleaner, forcefully driving the remaining, upgraded oil toward the producing wells.

While brilliant in theory, Fireflooding is terrifyingly difficult to control in practice. If the fire burns out of control, it can melt the steel casings of the producing wells, causing catastrophic blowouts and the permanent loss of the entire field.
