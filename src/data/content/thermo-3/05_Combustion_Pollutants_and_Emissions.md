# Combustion Pollutants and Emissions

Every combustion process generates pollutants alongside useful thermal energy. Understanding the formation mechanisms of these pollutants is essential for designing cleaner engines, burners, and industrial furnaces — and for complying with increasingly stringent environmental regulations.

## 1. Carbon Monoxide (CO)

**Formation:** CO is an intermediate product in all hydrocarbon combustion. In the reaction chain, fuel breaks down to CO before the final oxidation to CO₂. CO formation is promoted by:
*   Locally rich (fuel-excess) regions where there isn't enough O₂ to complete oxidation.
*   Quenching — the flame is rapidly cooled (e.g., against cold cylinder walls in an engine) before CO can finish oxidizing to CO₂.
*   Very lean mixtures near the lean blowout limit (incomplete combustion from poor ignition).

**Health Effects:** CO is an odorless, colorless gas that binds to hemoglobin 200× more strongly than O₂, causing oxygen deprivation at even low concentrations.

**Control:** Ensure stoichiometric or slightly lean operation; maximize mixing quality; avoid cold quench zones. Three-way catalytic converters in cars oxidize CO → CO₂.

## 2. Oxides of Nitrogen (NOₓ = NO + NO₂)

NOₓ is the most regulated combustion pollutant for stationary sources. It contributes to smog formation, acid rain, and is a precursor to ground-level ozone.

**Three Formation Mechanisms:**

**1. Thermal NOₓ (Zeldovich Mechanism):** The dominant mechanism in most combustors. At high temperatures (> 1,800 K), the triple bond in atmospheric N₂ breaks and N atoms react with O₂ to form NO.
*   Rate is exponentially temperature-dependent. Doubling the temperature can increase the NO formation rate by 10,000×.
*   Reduction strategy: Keep flame temperature below ~1,700 K using lean combustion, EGR, or water injection.

**2. Prompt NOₓ (Fenimore Mechanism):** CH radicals in the flame front react rapidly with N₂ to form HCN intermediates that eventually convert to NO. Significant in rich-premixed flames and diffusion flames. Faster than thermal NOₓ at low temperatures.

**3. Fuel NOₓ:** If the fuel itself contains nitrogen (coal, heavy fuel oil, ammonia), that bound nitrogen is easily oxidized to NO during combustion. The dominant source of NOₓ from coal combustion.

**NOₓ Control Technologies:**
*   **Lean Premixed Combustion:** Gas turbines use $\phi \approx 0.5$ to limit flame temperature.
*   **Flue Gas Recirculation (EGR):** Dilutes the charge, lowering $T_{AFT}$.
*   **Selective Catalytic Reduction (SCR):** Ammonia (NH₃) or urea is injected into the exhaust; a catalyst converts NOₓ + NH₃ → N₂ + H₂O. Achieves >95% NOₓ reduction. Used in power plants, ships, and large diesel vehicles.
*   **Staged Combustion:** Burn fuel-rich (suppresses thermal NOₓ due to lower O₂) then complete combustion in a secondary zone with excess air.

## 3. Particulate Matter (Soot / Black Carbon)

**Formation:** In fuel-rich (diffusion flame) zones, large hydrocarbon molecules pyrolyze (break apart without enough O₂ to burn) into small carbon nuclei (PAH — polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons) that grow into soot particles (5–100 nm).

*   Diesel engines are the primary anthropogenic source of particulate (PM2.5) emissions from transportation.
*   Soot is a potent short-lived climate forcer (warms the atmosphere by absorbing solar radiation).

**Control:**
*   **Diesel Particulate Filters (DPF):** Ceramic filter traps soot; periodically regenerated (burned off) at high temperature. Achieves >99% PM reduction.
*   **Better Fuel-Air Mixing:** High-pressure direct injection (common rail diesel) produces finer fuel droplets → better mixing → less fuel-rich zones → less soot.

## 4. Unburned Hydrocarbons (UHC)

Fuel that escapes combustion entirely:
*   Quenched in crevices (cylinder ring grooves in engines).
*   Overly lean mixtures that fail to ignite.
*   Misfires.

UHC contribute to photochemical smog and are direct greenhouse gases. Three-way catalysts oxidize UHC → CO₂ + H₂O.

## 5. Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)

The unavoidable product of complete hydrocarbon combustion. Not a traditional "pollutant" but the primary driver of anthropogenic climate change. Reduction strategies include efficiency improvement, fuel switching (hydrogen, biofuels), carbon capture and storage (CCS), and electrification.
