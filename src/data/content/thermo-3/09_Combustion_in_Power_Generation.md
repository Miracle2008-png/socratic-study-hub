# Combustion in Power Generation

The vast majority of global electricity is generated by burning fossil fuels in power plants. Understanding the thermodynamics and engineering of these combustion systems is essential for improving their efficiency and reducing their environmental impact.

## 1. Coal-Fired Power Plants (Pulverized Coal)

Coal is the most energy-dense fossil fuel in terms of global reserves and was the backbone of industrialization. Modern supercritical pulverized coal (PC) plants operate as follows:

**Process:**
1.  Coal is ground to a fine powder (~75 µm) in large ball mills.
2.  The pulverized coal is pneumatically conveyed to burners where it combusts in suspension in the furnace.
3.  Combustion gases at ~1,200–1,400°C pass over thousands of boiler tubes, generating steam.
4.  Steam drives a steam turbine connected to a generator.
5.  Exhaust gas passes through:
    *   **Selective Catalytic Reduction (SCR):** NOₓ reduction.
    *   **Electrostatic Precipitator (ESP) or Fabric Filter:** Particulate removal.
    *   **Flue Gas Desulfurization (FGD) / Scrubber:** SO₂ removal with limestone slurry.

**Efficiency Evolution:**
*   **Subcritical plants** (1950s–1980s): Steam at 160 bar / 540°C. Efficiency ~36%.
*   **Supercritical plants**: Steam at 250 bar / 600°C. Efficiency ~42%.
*   **Ultra-supercritical (USC)**: Steam at 300 bar / 620°C. Efficiency ~46%.
*   **Advanced USC (A-USC)**: Research target of 700°C steam using nickel superalloy boiler tubes. Efficiency ~52%.

Higher temperature requires exotic high-chromium steels and eventually nickel alloys — dramatically increasing cost.

## 2. Natural Gas Combined Cycle (NGCC)

The most efficient large-scale power generation technology commercially available today. It "combines" two power cycles sequentially:

**Top Cycle (Brayton — Gas Turbine):**
Natural gas burns in a gas turbine combustor. The expanding hot gases spin a gas turbine to generate electricity. Efficiency: ~40%.

**Bottom Cycle (Rankine — Steam Turbine):**
The hot exhaust from the gas turbine (~600°C) flows through a **Heat Recovery Steam Generator (HRSG)** — a large heat exchanger that generates steam. The steam drives a steam turbine to generate additional electricity. Efficiency of this bottom cycle alone: ~30–35%.

**Combined Result:**
Total plant efficiency: **58–63%** LHV. The best-available NGCC plants (e.g., GE H-class) achieve ~64% efficiency — extraordinary for large-scale electricity generation.

This roughly halves CO₂ emissions compared to coal for the same electricity output: natural gas (CH₄) has a high H:C ratio, producing more H₂O and less CO₂ per unit of energy than coal (which is nearly pure carbon).

## 3. Integrated Gasification Combined Cycle (IGCC)

Coal is gasified (reacted with steam and controlled amounts of O₂) to produce **synthesis gas (syngas)**: a mixture of H₂ and CO.

$C + H_2O \rightarrow CO + H_2 \quad$ (steam reforming / gasification)

The syngas is cleaned (removing sulfur, mercury, and particulates) and then burned in a gas turbine combined cycle. CO₂ can be removed from the syngas before combustion (pre-combustion carbon capture) since it is at high pressure in a concentrated stream.

*   Higher capital cost than conventional PC or NGCC plants.
*   Better environmental performance (lowest SO₂, NOₓ, particulates).
*   Enables carbon capture and storage (CCS) economically.
*   Has not achieved wide commercial deployment due to capital cost and operational complexity.

## 4. Hydrogen Combustion

Hydrogen is emerging as a potential zero-carbon fuel for power generation. Key challenges:
*   **High laminar burning velocity** (~8× that of methane/air): Increased risk of flashback in burners designed for natural gas.
*   **Wide flammability range** (4–75% in air, vs. 5–15% for methane): Greater explosion risk in fuel handling.
*   **High flame temperature:** More thermal NOₓ production. Requires very lean operation or steam/water dilution to control NOₓ.
*   GE, Siemens, and Mitsubishi have demonstrated gas turbines capable of burning 20–100% hydrogen blends. 100% hydrogen gas turbines are a near-term commercial target.
