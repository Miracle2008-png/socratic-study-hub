# Future of Combustion: Alternative Fuels and Carbon Capture

As the world decarbonizes, combustion engineering faces its most profound transformation. The challenge is not to eliminate combustion — its energy density and existing infrastructure are irreplaceable in the near term — but to dramatically reduce or eliminate its CO₂ emissions through alternative fuels and carbon capture.

## 1. Hydrogen as a Fuel

Hydrogen is the ultimate clean combustion fuel: when burned with oxygen, it produces only water vapor.
$$ H_2 + \frac{1}{2}O_2 \rightarrow H_2O \quad \Delta H = -286 \text{ kJ/mol} $$

**Challenges:**
*   **Production:** 96% of current hydrogen production uses steam methane reforming (SMR) of natural gas — itself a CO₂-emitting process (called "grey hydrogen"). "Green hydrogen" from water electrolysis using renewable electricity is clean but currently expensive.
*   **Storage:** Hydrogen has extremely low volumetric energy density. Compressed at 700 bar, it is ~4× less energy-dense per liter than gasoline. Liquid hydrogen at -253°C (20 K) is more compact but requires cryogenic insulation.
*   **NOₓ:** Hydrogen has a higher adiabatic flame temperature than hydrocarbons. Without mitigation (lean burn, EGR, water injection), thermal NOₓ emissions are very high.
*   **Embrittlement:** Hydrogen diffuses into steel and causes embrittlement — existing natural gas pipelines may require lining or replacement for 100% hydrogen service.

## 2. Ammonia (NH₃) as a Fuel

Ammonia is emerging as a practical hydrogen carrier and direct fuel:
*   Can be liquefied at -33°C (vs. -253°C for hydrogen) or stored at ~10 bar at room temperature — far easier than hydrogen.
*   High energy density relative to hydrogen per unit volume.
*   Zero carbon combustion: $4NH_3 + 3O_2 \rightarrow 2N_2 + 6H_2O$
*   **Challenge:** Very slow burning velocity (~15% of methane/air); requires careful combustion design. Risk of N₂O (a potent greenhouse gas) and NO emissions from fuel-nitrogen pathway if combustion is not complete.
*   Currently being developed for large marine shipping engines and power generation.

## 3. Biofuels

Fuels derived from biomass are considered carbon-neutral if the CO₂ released during combustion was absorbed from the atmosphere during the plant's growth.

*   **Bioethanol (from corn, sugarcane):** Blended with gasoline (E10, E85). Infrastructure-compatible.
*   **Biodiesel (from vegetable oils, animal fats):** FAME (Fatty Acid Methyl Esters). Blend with petroleum diesel.
*   **Sustainable Aviation Fuel (SAF):** Drop-in replacement for jet fuel. Can be made from municipal waste, agricultural residues, or CO₂ + H₂ (electrofuels). Critical for decarbonizing aviation (no practical battery alternative for long-haul flight).

## 4. Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS)

For systems where combustion of fossil fuels is unavoidable, CCS removes CO₂ from the flue gas and stores it permanently underground.

**Post-Combustion Capture:** Flue gas is contacted with an amine solvent that absorbs CO₂. The solvent is regenerated by heating, releasing a concentrated CO₂ stream for compression and injection into geological formations. Captures ~90% of CO₂ but requires ~25–30% of the plant's output energy for regeneration.

**Pre-Combustion Capture (IGCC):** Fuel is gasified to syngas (CO + H₂); the CO is shifted to CO₂ + H₂ via the water-gas shift reaction; CO₂ is removed from the high-pressure gas at high concentration (cheaper than post-combustion capture); pure H₂ is burned.

**Oxyfuel Combustion:** Burn fuel in nearly pure oxygen (from an air separation unit) instead of air. The flue gas is almost pure CO₂ + H₂O. Condense the water → pure CO₂ stream ready for storage. Energy penalty for air separation is the main cost.

**Direct Air Capture (DAC):** Remove CO₂ directly from the atmosphere using chemical sorbents. Very energy-intensive but not location-constrained. Currently $300–$600/ton CO₂. Engineering targets aim for < $100/ton.
