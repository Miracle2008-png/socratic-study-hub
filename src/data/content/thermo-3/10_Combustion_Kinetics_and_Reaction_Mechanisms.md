# Combustion Kinetics and Reaction Mechanisms

Real combustion does not occur in a single step like $CH_4 + 2O_2 \rightarrow CO_2 + 2H_2O$. This overall reaction is actually the net result of hundreds of elementary molecular-scale reactions involving highly reactive radical species. Combustion kinetics is the study of the rates and pathways of these detailed elementary reactions.

## 1. Why Kinetics Matters

Thermodynamic equilibrium tells us *where* a combustion system is headed (the final state). Kinetics tells us *how fast* it gets there.

**Engineering Consequences:**
*   An engine cylinder might not have enough time for CO to oxidize to CO₂ before the piston exhausts the gases — leading to CO emissions even in a lean engine.
*   Ignition delay in diesel engines: the time between fuel injection and auto-ignition. Too long a delay allows too much fuel to accumulate, causing a sharp pressure spike.
*   Quenching: if gases cool too rapidly, reactions "freeze" at an intermediate composition.

## 2. Elementary Reactions and Radicals

A combustion flame is not a continuous "fire" — it is a chain-reaction process driven by highly reactive free radicals: atoms and molecular fragments with unpaired electrons.

The major radical species in hydrocarbon combustion are:
*   **H** (hydrogen atom)
*   **O** (oxygen atom)
*   **OH** (hydroxyl radical)
*   **CH₃** (methyl radical)
*   **HO₂** (hydroperoxyl radical)

**The H₂/O₂ Mechanism (Core of all hydrocarbon combustion):**
The simplest combustion system reveals the key radical chain:

1.  **Initiation:** $H_2 + O_2 \rightarrow 2OH$ (slow; only happens above ignition temperature)
2.  **Chain Propagation:** $OH + H_2 \rightarrow H_2O + H$
3.  **Chain Branching:** $H + O_2 \rightarrow OH + O$ *(Critical step — one radical becomes two!)*
4.  **Chain Branching:** $O + H_2 \rightarrow OH + H$
5.  **Termination:** $H + H + M \rightarrow H_2 + M$ (radicals recombine at walls or via three-body collisions)

Steps 3 and 4 are **chain branching** reactions — they multiply the number of reactive radicals exponentially. Once the branching rate exceeds the termination rate, the reaction accelerates explosively — this is ignition.

## 3. Ignition

**Thermal Ignition:** 
Heat is added to the mixture (by a spark, hot surface, or compression). The temperature rises, increasing the reaction rate. If the heat generated by reaction exceeds heat lost to surroundings, a runaway thermal explosion (ignition) occurs. This is the **Semenov explosion criterion**.

**Autoignition Temperature (AIT):**
The minimum temperature at which a fuel-air mixture self-ignites without an external ignition source. AIT depends on pressure, mixture composition, and surface effects.
*   Methane: ~540°C
*   Gasoline: ~247°C
*   Hydrogen: ~500°C
*   Diesel: ~210°C (low AIT — why diesel engines can use compression ignition)

**Ignition Delay:**
The time between when conditions for ignition are met (e.g., fuel injected into hot compressed air) and when ignition actually occurs. During this delay, fuel decomposes into reactive intermediates (pre-flame chemistry). Longer ignition delay → more fuel accumulates → harder "knock" when ignition finally occurs.

## 4. Detailed Chemical Kinetic Mechanisms

Comprehensive kinetic models for hydrocarbon combustion are enormous:
*   **Methane (GRI-Mech 3.0):** 53 species, 325 elementary reactions.
*   **n-Heptane (a diesel surrogate):** 560 species, 2,540 reactions.
*   **Real gasoline surrogate (4 components):** >1,000 species, >5,000 reactions.

Computing the solution of these mechanisms for every computational cell in a CFD simulation of an engine would require millions of CPU-years. **Reduced mechanisms** (simplified models retaining the key pathways) and **tabulated chemistry** (pre-computing equilibrium libraries) are used instead.
