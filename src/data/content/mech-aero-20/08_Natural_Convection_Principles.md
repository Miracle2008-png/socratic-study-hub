# Natural Convection Principles

In natural (free) convection, there is no fan or pump. Fluid motion is driven entirely by internal **buoyancy forces** generated by density differences, which are in turn caused by temperature differences within the fluid.

## 1. The Mechanism of Buoyancy

Consider a hot vertical plate in a cold, stagnant room.
1. The air adjacent to the plate is heated by conduction.
2. Because gases expand when heated, the density of this hot air decreases.
3. The surrounding cold air is denser and heavier. Gravity pulls the heavier cold air down, which displaces the lighter hot air, forcing it upward.
4. This creates a continuous upward boundary layer flow along the plate, purely from thermal buoyancy.

The net buoyancy force per unit volume driving the flow is the difference between the weight of the cold fluid displaced and the weight of the hot fluid:
$$F_{buoyancy} = (\rho_\infty - \rho) g$$

## 2. Volumetric Thermal Expansion Coefficient ($\beta$)

To relate density differences directly to temperature differences, we use the volumetric thermal expansion coefficient $\beta$ [$1/K$], a thermodynamic property defining how volume changes with temperature at constant pressure:

$$\beta = -\frac{1}{\rho}\left(\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial T}\right)_P \approx -\frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\Delta \rho}{\Delta T} = \frac{\rho_\infty - \rho}{\rho (T - T_\infty)}$$

Substituting this into the buoyancy force gives the driving term in the Navier-Stokes momentum equation for natural convection (the Boussinesq approximation):
$$F_{buoyancy} = \rho g \beta (T - T_\infty)$$

**For Ideal Gases:** $\beta = 1/T$, where $T$ is the absolute temperature in Kelvin. (This is a huge simplification unique to ideal gases).
For liquids, $\beta$ must be looked up in property tables.

## 3. The Grashof Number ($Gr$)

In forced convection, the Reynolds number governs flow regimes. In natural convection, the **Grashof Number ($Gr$)** dictates the flow behavior. It represents the ratio of buoyancy forces driving the flow to viscous forces resisting the flow.

$$Gr_L = \frac{g \beta (T_s - T_\infty) L_c^3}{\nu^2}$$

- $g$: gravitational acceleration ($9.81 m/s^2$)
- $\beta$: thermal expansion coefficient ($1/K$)
- $T_s - T_\infty$: surface and free-stream temperatures ($K$ or $^\circ C$)
- $L_c$: characteristic length (e.g., height of a vertical plate) ($m$)
- $\nu$: kinematic viscosity ($m^2/s$)

Just as $Re$ dictates transition in forced flow, $Gr$ dictates transition in natural flow. For a vertical plate, the natural convection boundary layer transitions from laminar to turbulent when $Gr_L \cdot Pr \approx 10^9$.

## 4. The Rayleigh Number ($Ra$)

Almost all empirical correlations for natural convection heat transfer are expressed in terms of the **Rayleigh Number ($Ra$)**, which is simply the product of the Grashof and Prandtl numbers:

$$Ra_L = Gr_L \cdot Pr = \frac{g \beta (T_s - T_\infty) L_c^3}{\nu \alpha}$$

The Rayleigh number elegantly captures the ratio of buoyancy-driven momentum advection to thermal diffusion. Heat transfer correlations universally take the form $Nu = C \cdot Ra^n$.
