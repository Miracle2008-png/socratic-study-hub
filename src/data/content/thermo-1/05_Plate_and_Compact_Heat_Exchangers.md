# Plate and Compact Heat Exchangers

While shell-and-tube heat exchangers dominate heavy industry, two other types — Plate Heat Exchangers and Compact Heat Exchangers — are preferred in applications where small size, weight, and high efficiency are paramount.

## 1. Plate Heat Exchangers (PHE)

A plate heat exchanger consists of a stack of thin, corrugated metal plates clamped together between two thick end plates by long bolts. The hot and cold fluids flow in alternating gaps between consecutive plates.

### Key Features

**Corrugated (Chevron) Pattern:**
The plates are not flat — they are stamped with a herringbone or "chevron" wave pattern. When two plates are pressed together, their patterns create complex narrow channels that:
*   Force the fluid into vigorous turbulent mixing, even at very low flow rates (low Reynolds numbers). This dramatically increases the convective heat transfer coefficient.
*   Create a self-supporting structure where the plates press against each other at thousands of contact points, allowing the assembly to withstand significant pressure.

**Counter-Flow:**
The design naturally promotes counter-flow arrangement — the hot fluid enters from one side at the top, and the cold fluid enters from the other side at the bottom, flowing in opposite directions through alternating plate gaps.

**Gasketed vs. Brazed:**
*   **Gasketed PHE:** Elastomeric sealing gaskets separate each plate. The big advantage is that the unit can be completely disassembled — unclamp the bolts and the plates fall apart for thorough cleaning. The limitation is that the gasket material limits maximum temperature (~200°C) and pressure (~25 bar).
*   **Brazed PHE:** The plates are brazed (soldered) together with copper or nickel. No gaskets means they can operate at much higher temperatures and pressures. But they cannot be disassembled — if badly fouled, the entire unit must be replaced.

### Advantages Over Shell-and-Tube

*   **Extremely High U:** Because the corrugated channels are narrow and force intense turbulence, PHEs achieve overall heat transfer coefficients 3–5x higher than shell-and-tube exchangers. Much less area is needed for the same duty.
*   **True Counter-Flow:** Very close temperature approaches are achievable (hot outlet within 1–2°C of cold inlet).
*   **Compact:** For the same heat duty, a PHE can be 80–90% smaller and lighter than a shell-and-tube exchanger.
*   **Easy Cleaning:** Gasketed units can be fully disassembled and pressure-washed in minutes.
*   **Flexible:** Adding or removing plates changes the capacity instantly.

### Limitations

*   Cannot handle very high pressure (>25 bar for gasketed).
*   Susceptible to fouling in the narrow channels (especially with particles or fibrous materials).
*   Cannot handle two-phase flows with large vapor fractions.

## 2. Compact Heat Exchangers

"Compact" is a specific technical term: a heat exchanger with a surface area density exceeding 700 m²/m³ (compared to ~100 m²/m³ for shell-and-tube).

These are used where weight and size are critical — aircraft, spacecraft, automobiles, and gas turbine recuperators.

### Plate-Fin Heat Exchangers

The most common compact design. Corrugated metal fins are brazed between flat metal "parting sheets." Multiple layers are stacked together.
*   *Used in:* Aircraft air cycle machines, natural gas liquefaction plants, cryogenic air separation units, high-performance automotive intercoolers.
*   The fins can be plain, wavy, serrated (offset-strip), or louvered — each promoting increasingly aggressive turbulence.

### Tube-Fin Heat Exchangers

Round tubes are mechanically expanded into a stack of flat aluminum fins. Air flows across the fins; liquid flows inside the tubes.
*   *Used in:* Automobile radiators and air conditioning condensers.
*   Because air has a very poor convection coefficient, the fins on the air side provide the large surface area needed to compensate.

### Microchannel Heat Exchangers

Channels as small as 10–1000 µm. Used in electronics cooling (CPU cold plates), microreactors, and advanced aerospace applications. Very high surface area densities (up to 50,000 m²/m³).
