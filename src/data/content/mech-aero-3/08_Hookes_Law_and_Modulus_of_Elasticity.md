# Hooke's Law and the Modulus of Elasticity

The most important region of the stress-strain diagram for practical engineering design is the initial linear elastic region. Structures are almost always designed such that the internal stresses never exceed the yield point of the material.

## 1. Hooke's Law

In 1676, Robert Hooke observed that for many materials, the deformation is directly proportional to the applied load, provided the load is not too large.

In modern mechanics of materials, this observation translates to: *In the linear elastic region, stress is directly proportional to strain.*

This relationship is expressed mathematically by **Hooke's Law**:
$$ \sigma = E \epsilon $$
where:
- $\sigma$ is the normal stress.
- $\epsilon$ is the normal strain.
- $E$ is the constant of proportionality.

## 2. Modulus of Elasticity ($E$)

The constant $E$ is known as the **Modulus of Elasticity** or **Young's Modulus**. 

Geometrically, $E$ represents the *slope* of the straight-line portion of the stress-strain diagram. 
Because strain $\epsilon$ is dimensionless, the Modulus of Elasticity has the exact same units as stress (Pa, MPa, GPa, or psi).

### Physical Interpretation
The Modulus of Elasticity is the fundamental measure of a material's **stiffness**—its inherent resistance to elastic deformation. 
- A material with a massive $E$ (like steel, $E \approx 200 \text{ GPa}$) is very stiff. It requires a tremendous stress to produce a small strain.
- A material with a small $E$ (like rubber or soft plastics) is very flexible. A small stress produces a massive strain.

## 3. Hooke's Law in Shear

A completely analogous relationship exists for shear stress and shear strain in the linear elastic region.

**Hooke's Law for Shear** is given by:
$$ \tau = G \gamma $$
where:
- $\tau$ is the shear stress.
- $\gamma$ is the shear strain (in radians).
- $G$ is the **Shear Modulus of Elasticity** (or Modulus of Rigidity).

$G$ represents the material's stiffness against shear distortion. Like $E$, $G$ is a fundamental material property determined via experiment (usually a torsion test), and shares the units of stress.
