# Volumes by Disk and Washer Method: Solids of Revolution

When a 2D region is rotated around an axis, it sweeps out a 3D solid known as a **solid of revolution**. We can find the volume of this solid by slicing it perpendicular to the axis of revolution.

**Engineering Applications:**
*   **Manufacturing (Lathes):** Any object manufactured on a lathe—such as engine pistons, drive shafts, custom bolts, and chess pieces—is mathematically a solid of revolution. By defining the curve of the cutting tool, engineers can compute the exact volume and weight of the finished steel or aluminum part.
*   **Aerospace:** Rocket engine nozzles and jet engine nacelles are designed by revolving precise aerodynamic curves around a central axis to optimize fluid and gas flow.
*   **Fluid Storage:** Determining the exact capacity of uniquely shaped pressure vessels, fuel tanks, or chemical mixing vats.

---

## 1. The Disk Method

Use the disk method when the axis of revolution forms one of the boundaries of the 2D region. When you slice the resulting solid perpendicular to the axis of revolution, the cross-section is a solid circle (a "disk").

The volume of a very thin cylinder (a disk) is $V = \pi r^2 h$.
*   If revolving around a horizontal axis (like the x-axis), the radius $r$ is the y-value of the curve, $f(x)$. The thickness $h$ is the infinitesimally small $dx$.
*   Therefore, the volume of one differential slice is $dV = \pi [f(x)]^2 \, dx$.

**Revolution around the x-axis:**
If the region bounded by $y = f(x)$, the x-axis, $x=a$, and $x=b$ is revolved around the x-axis:
$$ V = \pi \int_{a}^{b} [f(x)]^2 \, dx $$

**Revolution around the y-axis:**
If the region bounded by $x = g(y)$, the y-axis, $y=c$, and $y=d$ is revolved around the y-axis, you must integrate with respect to $y$:
$$ V = \pi \int_{c}^{d} [g(y)]^2 \, dy $$

### Example 1: Creating a Solid Cone (Easy)
**Problem:** Find the volume of the solid generated by revolving the region bounded by $y = \frac{1}{2}x$, the x-axis, and $x = 4$ about the x-axis. (This forms a cone).

**Step 1: Identify the radius function.**
The radius is the distance from the axis of revolution (x-axis, $y=0$) to the curve $y = \frac{1}{2}x$.
Thus, $r(x) = \frac{1}{2}x$.

**Step 2: Set up the integral.**
The bounds are from $x = 0$ (where the line intersects the origin) to $x = 4$.
$$ V = \pi \int_{0}^{4} \left( \frac{1}{2}x \right)^2 \, dx $$

**Step 3: Evaluate.**
$$ V = \pi \int_{0}^{4} \frac{1}{4}x^2 \, dx = \frac{\pi}{4} \left[ \frac{x^3}{3} \right]_{0}^{4} $$
$$ V = \frac{\pi}{12} (4^3 - 0) = \frac{64\pi}{12} = \frac{16\pi}{3} $$
The volume of the cone is $\frac{16\pi}{3}$ cubic units.

## 2. The Washer Method

Use the washer method when the region being revolved does *not* sit completely flush against the axis of revolution. When the solid is formed, there will be a hollow cavity running through the middle (like a pipe or a mechanical bushing). A cross-section sliced perpendicular to the axis of revolution will look like a circle with a hole in it—a "washer."

The area of a washer is the area of the outer circle minus the area of the inner hole:
$$ A = \pi R_{\text{outer}}^2 - \pi r_{\text{inner}}^2 = \pi (R_{\text{outer}}^2 - r_{\text{inner}}^2) $$

**Revolution around the x-axis:**
Let $R(x)$ be the outer radius (the curve further from the axis of revolution) and $r(x)$ be the inner radius (the curve closer to the axis).
$$ V = \pi \int_{a}^{b} \left( [R(x)]^2 - [r(x)]^2 \right) \, dx $$
*Warning: The integrand is $(R^2 - r^2)$, NOT $(R - r)^2$. This is a very common and fatal mistake in engineering calculations!*

### Example 2: Hollow Mechanical Part (Medium)
**Problem:** A region is bounded by $y = \sqrt{x}$ and $y = x^2$. Find the volume when this region is revolved around the x-axis.

**Step 1: Find intersection points to get limits.**
$$ \sqrt{x} = x^2 \implies x = x^4 \implies x(x^3 - 1) = 0 $$
The curves intersect at $x = 0$ and $x = 1$.

**Step 2: Identify outer and inner radii.**
Between 0 and 1, $\sqrt{x} > x^2$.
Outer radius $R(x) = \sqrt{x}$
Inner radius $r(x) = x^2$

**Step 3: Set up and evaluate the Washer integral.**
$$ V = \pi \int_{0}^{1} \left( (\sqrt{x})^2 - (x^2)^2 \right) \, dx $$
$$ V = \pi \int_{0}^{1} (x - x^4) \, dx $$
$$ V = \pi \left[ \frac{x^2}{2} - \frac{x^5}{5} \right]_{0}^{1} $$
$$ V = \pi \left( \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{5} \right) = \pi \left( \frac{5}{10} - \frac{2}{10} \right) = \frac{3\pi}{10} $$

### Example 3: Revolving Around an Arbitrary Line (Hard)
**Problem:** Find the volume of the solid generated by revolving the region bounded by $y = 2 - x^2$ and $y = 1$ around the line $y = -1$.

**Step 1: Intersection points.**
$$ 2 - x^2 = 1 \implies x^2 = 1 \implies x = \pm 1 $$

**Step 2: Determine radii relative to $y = -1$.**
The axis of revolution is $y = -1$.
The distance from the axis to the top curve is the outer radius:
$R(x) = (2 - x^2) - (-1) = 3 - x^2$
The distance from the axis to the bottom curve is the inner radius:
$r(x) = 1 - (-1) = 2$

**Step 3: Integrate.**
$$ V = \pi \int_{-1}^{1} \left( (3 - x^2)^2 - (2)^2 \right) \, dx $$
$$ V = \pi \int_{-1}^{1} (9 - 6x^2 + x^4 - 4) \, dx = \pi \int_{-1}^{1} (x^4 - 6x^2 + 5) \, dx $$
Because the function is even and symmetric around the y-axis, we can integrate from 0 to 1 and double it:
$$ V = 2\pi \int_{0}^{1} (x^4 - 6x^2 + 5) \, dx = 2\pi \left[ \frac{x^5}{5} - 2x^3 + 5x \right]_{0}^{1} $$
$$ V = 2\pi \left( \frac{1}{5} - 2 + 5 \right) = 2\pi \left( 3 + \frac{1}{5} \right) = 2\pi \left( \frac{16}{5} \right) = \frac{32\pi}{5} $$

## 3. General Cross Sections (Advanced 3D Printing)

The disk and washer methods are specific cases of a more general principle. If you know the formula for the area of a cross-section $A(x)$ perpendicular to the x-axis, the volume of the solid is simply:
$$ V = \int_{a}^{b} A(x) \, dx $$

This is highly relevant in additive manufacturing (3D printing), where parts are built layer by layer, and the area of each layer $A(x)$ varies across the build height.

### Example 4: Square Cross-Sections
**Problem:** The base of a solid is the region bounded by the circle $x^2 + y^2 = 4$. Every cross-section perpendicular to the x-axis is a square. Find the volume.

**Step 1: Express the geometry of the base.**
The circle equation gives the top half as $y = \sqrt{4 - x^2}$ and the bottom half as $y = -\sqrt{4 - x^2}$.
The total length of a slice across the base at position $x$ is $2y = 2\sqrt{4 - x^2}$.

**Step 2: Relate base to cross-sectional area.**
The cross section is a square. The base of the square lies on the circular region.
Side of square $s(x) = 2\sqrt{4 - x^2}$
Area of square $A(x) = [s(x)]^2 = \left(2\sqrt{4 - x^2}\right)^2 = 4(4 - x^2) = 16 - 4x^2$

**Step 3: Integrate.**
The circular base spans from $x = -2$ to $x = 2$.
$$ V = \int_{-2}^{2} (16 - 4x^2) \, dx $$
By symmetry, double the integral from 0 to 2:
$$ V = 2 \int_{0}^{2} (16 - 4x^2) \, dx = 2 \left[ 16x - \frac{4x^3}{3} \right]_{0}^{2} $$
$$ V = 2 \left( 32 - \frac{32}{3} \right) = 2 \left( \frac{96 - 32}{3} \right) = 2 \left( \frac{64}{3} \right) = \frac{128}{3} $$
