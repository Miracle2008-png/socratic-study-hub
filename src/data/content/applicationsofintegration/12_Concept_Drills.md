# Concept Drills: Applications of Integration

Test your conceptual understanding of the applications of integration, connecting abstract mathematical setups with physical and engineering realities.

## Conceptual Questions

1.  **True or False:** The area between $y = x^2$ and $y = -x^2$ from $x=-1$ to $x=1$ is zero because the integrals cancel out due to symmetry.
2.  If you revolve the region bounded by $y = x$ and $y = x^2$ around the x-axis, do you use the disk method or the washer method? Why?
3.  Why is the formula for arc length $L = \int \sqrt{1 + (f'(x))^2} \, dx$ derived using the Pythagorean theorem?
4.  In the formula for pumping water out of a tank, what does the differential term $(\rho g \cdot \text{Area}(y) \cdot dy)$ represent physically in a fluid mechanics context?
5.  Why is the area moment of inertia of a rectangular cross-section beam proportional to $h^3$ but only proportional to $b^1$? How does this impact civil engineering design?
6.  If the average value of a continuous signal $f(t)$ on $[0, 10]$ is $5\text{V}$, does the signal $f(t)$ *have* to exactly equal $5\text{V}$ at some point between $t=0$ and $t=10$?

## Analytical Problems

1.  **Area Between Curves (Easy):** Find the area of the region bounded by $y = 5 - x^2$ and $y = 1$.
2.  **Volume by Washers (Medium):** Set up and evaluate the integral for the volume generated by revolving the region bounded by $y = \sqrt{x}$ and $y = x^2$ around the x-axis.
3.  **Volume by Cylindrical Shells (Medium):** Set up the integral for the volume generated by revolving the region bounded by $y = \sqrt{x}$, $x=4$, and $y=0$ around the y-axis, using cylindrical shells.
4.  **Work and Springs (Medium):** A mechanical spring has a natural length of 20 cm. If a force of 40 N is required to keep it stretched to a length of 30 cm, how much work is done in stretching it from 20 cm to 40 cm?
5.  **Average Value of a Signal (Hard):** Find the average value of the power signal $P(t) = t \sin(t^2)$ on the interval $[0, \sqrt{\pi}]$.
6.  **Improper Integral in Probability (Hard):** Evaluate the improper integral $\int_{2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x \ln(x)^2} \, dx$. Does it converge or diverge?

---

## Solutions

### Conceptual Solutions

1.  **False.** Area is always a positive geometric quantity. The formula is $\int (\text{top} - \text{bottom}) \, dx = \int [x^2 - (-x^2)] \, dx = \int 2x^2 \, dx$, which yields a positive area. Standard integration without the "top-bottom" formula gives net signed area, but "area between curves" implies physical area.
2.  **Washer method.** Because the region sits between $y=x$ and $y=x^2$, it does not sit flush against the axis of revolution (x-axis). When revolved, there will be a hollow trumpet-shaped cavity in the center, necessitating the subtraction of an inner volume (the "hole" of the washer).
3.  Because the curve is approximated by tiny straight line segments $ds$. The hypotenuse of the right triangle formed by infinitesimal changes $dx$ and $dy$ is $ds = \sqrt{dx^2 + dy^2}$. Factoring out $dx$ yields $ds = \sqrt{1 + (dy/dx)^2} \, dx$, which integrates to the arc length formula.
4.  It represents the **weight** of an infinitesimally thin, horizontal slice of fluid at height $y$. Specifically, $\rho g$ is the specific weight (weight density), and $\text{Area}(y) \cdot dy$ is the differential volume $dV$ of the slice. Force (Weight) = Density $\times$ Gravity $\times$ Volume.
5.  Because the integral for the moment of inertia $I_x$ is $\int y^2 \, dA$. The distance $y$ from the neutral axis is squared. Material placed further from the neutral axis (higher $h$) contributes quadratically to the beam's bending stiffness, whereas making the beam wider (increasing $b$) just adds material linearly. This is why I-beams are tall rather than wide!
6.  **Yes.** This is guaranteed by the Mean Value Theorem for Integrals. Since the function is continuous, it cannot jump from below $5\text{V}$ to above $5\text{V}$ without crossing exactly $5\text{V}$.

### Analytical Solutions

1.  **Area Between Curves:**
    *   **Intersections:** $5 - x^2 = 1 \implies x^2 = 4 \implies x = \pm 2$.
    *   **Integral:** $\int_{-2}^{2} ((5 - x^2) - 1) \, dx = \int_{-2}^{2} (4 - x^2) \, dx$.
    *   **Evaluate:** $\left[ 4x - \frac{x^3}{3} \right]_{-2}^2 = \left( 8 - \frac{8}{3} \right) - \left( -8 + \frac{8}{3} \right) = 16 - \frac{16}{3} = \frac{32}{3}$.

2.  **Volume by Washers:**
    *   **Curves:** Top curve $R(x) = \sqrt{x}$, bottom curve $r(x) = x^2$. Intersect at $x=0, 1$.
    *   **Setup:** $V = \pi \int_{0}^{1} ((\sqrt{x})^2 - (x^2)^2) \, dx = \pi \int_{0}^{1} (x - x^4) \, dx$.
    *   **Evaluate:** $V = \pi \left[ \frac{x^2}{2} - \frac{x^5}{5} \right]_0^1 = \pi \left( \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{5} \right) = \frac{3\pi}{10}$.

3.  **Volume by Cylindrical Shells:**
    *   **Dimensions:** Radius $r=x$, Height $h=\sqrt{x}$.
    *   **Setup:** $V = \int_{0}^{4} 2\pi x (\sqrt{x}) \, dx = 2\pi \int_{0}^{4} x^{3/2} \, dx$.
    *   **Evaluate:** $V = 2\pi \left[ \frac{2}{5} x^{5/2} \right]_0^4 = 2\pi \left( \frac{2}{5} (32) \right) = \frac{128\pi}{5}$.

4.  **Work and Springs:**
    *   **Find $k$:** Displacement $x = 0.1$ m. Hooke's Law $F = kx \implies 40 = k(0.1) \implies k = 400$ N/m.
    *   **Setup:** Work from $x=0$ to $x=0.2$ m (displacement from natural length).
    *   **Evaluate:** $W = \int_{0}^{0.2} 400x \, dx = \left[ 200x^2 \right]_0^{0.2} = 200(0.04) = 8$ Joules.

5.  **Average Value of a Signal:**
    *   **Setup:** $P_{avg} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi} - 0} \int_{0}^{\sqrt{\pi}} t \sin(t^2) \, dt$.
    *   **Substitution:** Let $u = t^2$, $du = 2t \, dt \implies \frac{1}{2} du = t \, dt$. Bounds: $t=0 \implies u=0$, $t=\sqrt{\pi} \implies u=\pi$.
    *   **Evaluate:** $P_{avg} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi}} \int_{0}^{\pi} \frac{1}{2} \sin(u) \, du = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{\pi}} \left[ -\cos(u) \right]_0^\pi$.
    *   **Result:** $\frac{1}{2\sqrt{\pi}} (-\cos(\pi) - (-\cos(0))) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{\pi}} (1 + 1) = \frac{2}{2\sqrt{\pi}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\pi}}$.

6.  **Improper Integral in Probability:**
    *   **Limit Setup:** $\lim_{t \to \infty} \int_{2}^{t} \frac{1}{x \ln(x)^2} \, dx$.
    *   **Substitution:** Let $u = \ln(x)$, $du = \frac{1}{x} dx$. Integral becomes $\int \frac{1}{u^2} du = -\frac{1}{u} = -\frac{1}{\ln(x)}$.
    *   **Evaluate:** $\lim_{t \to \infty} \left[ -\frac{1}{\ln(x)} \right]_2^t = \lim_{t \to \infty} \left( -\frac{1}{\ln(t)} - \left( -\frac{1}{\ln(2)} \right) \right)$.
    *   **Result:** As $t \to \infty$, $\ln(t) \to \infty$, so $-\frac{1}{\ln(t)} \to 0$. The limit is $\frac{1}{\ln(2)}$. The integral **converges**.
