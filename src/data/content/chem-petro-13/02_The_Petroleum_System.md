# The Petroleum System

Finding a pool of oil in the ground is not a random occurrence. It requires a highly specific, chronological sequence of geological events that must happen in perfect order. 

Geologists refer to this perfect recipe as **The Petroleum System**. If even a single ingredient or step is missing, or if the timing is wrong, there will be no oil to drill.

There are five absolutely essential physical components of a working petroleum system.

## 1. The Source Rock

The story always begins with the **Source Rock**. This is the rock where the organic matter (dead plankton) was originally deposited, buried, and cooked.

*   Source rocks are almost always **Black Shales**. Shale is a sedimentary rock formed from deeply compacted mud and clay. It is very fine-grained and incredibly dense.
*   To be a viable source rock, it must have a high Total Organic Carbon (TOC) content (usually $>2\%$).
*   The source rock must be buried deep enough to reach the "Oil Window" ($60^\circ\text{C}$ to $150^\circ\text{C}$) so the kerogen can thermally mature into liquid petroleum.

## 2. Migration Pathway

Oil is generated inside the dense, microscopic pores of the source rock shale. But shale is so tight and impermeable that it is almost impossible to drill a well directly into it and pump the oil out (until the modern invention of hydraulic fracturing, or "fracking").

Therefore, in conventional oil fields, the oil must escape the source rock. Because oil and gas are lighter (less dense) than the saltwater that fills the crust of the Earth, they naturally want to float upwards toward the surface.

The **Migration Pathway** is the route the oil takes. It squeezes out of the source rock and travels upwards through networks of microscopic fractures, faults, and porous rock layers, migrating toward the surface over millions of years.

## 3. The Reservoir Rock

If the oil migrates all the way to the surface, it evaporates or gets eaten by surface bacteria (creating tar pits, like the La Brea Tar Pits in Los Angeles). This ruins the oil.

To form a drillable oil field, the migrating oil must get caught and stored in a **Reservoir Rock** deep underground.
*   A reservoir rock acts like a giant, hard, geologic sponge. It must have empty spaces (pores) to hold the oil, and those spaces must be connected so the fluid can flow.
*   The two most common reservoir rocks are **Sandstones** (ancient beaches or riverbeds turned to stone) and **Limestones/Carbonates** (ancient coral reefs).

## 4. The Seal (Cap Rock)

If the reservoir rock is a sponge, what stops the oil from just continuing to float right through it and escaping to the surface? 

There must be a **Seal** or **Cap Rock** sitting directly on top of the reservoir rock.
*   A seal is a layer of rock that is absolutely impermeable. Fluids cannot pass through it.
*   Common seals are thick layers of Shale, Clay, or massive underground beds of Halite (Rock Salt) left over from evaporated ancient oceans.
*   When the rising oil hits the bottom of the seal rock, it is blocked. It can go no further upwards.

## 5. The Trap

Even with a seal on top, the oil could just slide sideways along the bottom of the seal rock and eventually escape out the sides. 
The rocks must be physically bent or broken into a shape that forms a three-dimensional **Trap**.

A trap is a geometric configuration of the reservoir and seal rocks that acts like an upside-down bowl. The oil floats up into the bowl, hits the seal, tries to move sideways, but gets trapped by the walls of the bowl. It accumulates there, slowly filling the bowl with oil and gas over millions of years, creating an oil field.

## The Sixth Element: Timing

You can have all five physical components, but if the **Timing** is wrong, you get a dry hole.
*   The Trap and the Seal must physically form (via earthquakes or folding) *before* the oil is cooked and begins to migrate. 
*   If the trap forms 10 million years *after* the oil migrated through that area, the trap will be perfectly shaped, perfectly sealed, and completely empty. The oil already passed by.

Petroleum geologists spend their entire careers acting as geologic detectives, analyzing seismic waves and rock core samples to prove that all six elements of the petroleum system exist simultaneously miles beneath the surface before a company risks $100 million drilling a well.
