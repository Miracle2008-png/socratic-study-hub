# Structural Geology and Faulting

Structural geology is the study of how rocks deform under the immense stresses of tectonic plates grinding against each other. For petroleum geologists, structural geology is the key to finding the traps where oil accumulates.

Sedimentary rocks are originally deposited flat and horizontal at the bottom of ancient oceans (the Law of Original Horizontality). If you find rocks that are tilted, folded, or broken, they have undergone structural deformation.

## 1. Stress and Strain

Rocks deform in response to **Stress** (force applied per unit area). 
There are three main types of tectonic stress:
1.  **Compressional Stress:** Rocks are pushed together (convergent boundaries). This causes rocks to fold into mountains or break and slide over one another.
2.  **Tensional Stress:** Rocks are pulled apart (divergent boundaries). This causes the crust to thin, stretch, and break, forming massive rift valleys.
3.  **Shear Stress:** Rocks slide horizontally past one another (transform boundaries, like the San Andreas Fault).

**Strain** is the physical deformation of the rock caused by the stress. 
*   *Elastic Strain:* The rock bends slightly but springs back to its original shape when the stress is removed.
*   *Ductile (Plastic) Strain:* The rock is permanently bent and folded, but does not break. This usually happens deep underground where rocks are hot and under immense pressure.
*   *Brittle Strain:* The rock violently snaps and fractures. This happens near the cold, shallow surface of the Earth.

## 2. Folds: Anticlines and Synclines

When compressional stress causes ductile strain, rocks fold. Folds are described by two main features:
*   **Hinge/Axis:** The line along the crest of the fold where the curvature is greatest.
*   **Limbs:** The sloping sides of the fold that dip away from the hinge.

There are two primary types of folds:
1.  **Anticlines:** "A-shaped" upward folds. The limbs dip away from the center. The oldest rocks are exposed in the dead center of the fold. **Anticlines form the absolute best and most common structural oil traps.**
2.  **Synclines:** "U-shaped" downward folds. The limbs dip inward toward the center. The youngest rocks are in the center. Synclines do not trap oil (the oil simply floats out of them), but the deep troughs of synclines often act as the "kitchens" where source rocks are buried deep enough to cook.

To describe a tilted limb of a fold in 3D space, geologists measure the **Strike** (the compass direction of the horizontal line on the rock surface, e.g., North-South) and the **Dip** (the angle the rock tilts downward into the earth, e.g., $30^\circ$ East).

## 3. Faults: The Broken Crust

When stress causes brittle strain, rocks fracture. A fracture where there is no movement is a *joint*. A fracture where the rocks physically slide past each other is a **Fault**.

To understand faults, you must define the two blocks of rock on either side of the fracture plane:
*   **The Hanging Wall:** The block of rock that rests *on top* of the slanted fault plane. (If you were a miner standing in the fault tunnel, this block would hang over your head).
*   **The Footwall:** The block of rock that lies *beneath* the slanted fault plane. (This is the block you would stand on).

### Types of Faults

1.  **Normal Faults (Tensional Stress):** 
    *   Caused by the crust being pulled apart. 
    *   The Hanging Wall slides **DOWN** relative to the Footwall. 
    *   This extends and thins the crust, creating "graben" valleys (like the North Sea or the East African Rift). Normal faults are excellent at dropping porous reservoir rocks down against impermeable shales to create traps.
2.  **Reverse/Thrust Faults (Compressional Stress):** 
    *   Caused by the crust being smashed together. 
    *   The Hanging Wall is pushed violently **UP** and over the Footwall. 
    *   This shortens and thickens the crust, creating massive mountain ranges. If the fault plane is at a very shallow angle ($< 30^\circ$), it is called a Thrust Fault.
3.  **Strike-Slip Faults (Shear Stress):** 
    *   The blocks do not move up or down; they slide horizontally past each other. They rarely form traps on their own, but the complex "flower structures" that form where strike-slip faults bend can create fantastic traps.

## 4. Faults: Seals or Conduits?

For an oil trap, a fault is a double-edged sword.
*   **A Sealing Fault:** If a normal fault drops a porous sandstone down against a thick wall of impermeable shale, the fault perfectly traps the oil. Furthermore, the physical grinding of the rocks during the earthquake can pulverize the sand grains into an impermeable clay "fault gouge," sealing the fault completely.
*   **A Leaking Fault:** If the fault plane remains an open, shattered fracture, it acts as a massive geologic highway. Oil from a deep reservoir will hit the fault and shoot straight up the fracture to the surface, destroying the trap. 
Geologists spend immense effort calculating "Shale Gouge Ratios" to predict if a specific fault deep underground will seal or leak.
