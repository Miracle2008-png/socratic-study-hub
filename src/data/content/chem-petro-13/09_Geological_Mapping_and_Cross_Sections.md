# Geological Mapping and Cross-Sections

To find oil buried three miles underground, a geologist must be able to visualize the complex 3D geometry of the rock layers without actually being able to see them. 

They do this by gathering sparse data points (from surface rock outcrops or scattered well logs) and connecting the dots to create specialized 2D maps and cross-sections that reveal the hidden 3D structure.

## 1. Contour Maps

The most fundamental tool in geological mapping is the **Contour Map**. 
A contour line is a line drawn on a map connecting points of equal value. (Like the elevation lines on a hiker's topographic map). 
The space between two adjacent contour lines is the "contour interval."

Petroleum geologists use several types of contour maps to define the shape and thickness of an oil reservoir.

### Structural Maps (Structure Contour Maps)
A structural map shows the physical elevation of the *top* of a specific rock layer (the reservoir rock) relative to sea level. 
*   Because the rock is buried underground, these elevations are almost always negative (e.g., $-5,000 \text{ ft}$ subsea).
*   By looking at a structural map, you can instantly see the shape of the trap. 
*   If the contour lines form concentric, closed circles with the highest numbers in the center (like a bullseye, e.g., $-4000 \text{ ft}$ in the middle, $-4500 \text{ ft}$ on the outside), you have found an **Anticline** (a dome). This is where you drill.
*   If the contour lines show a sudden, sharp, jagged break where the elevation jumps instantly from $-4000 \text{ ft}$ to $-4500 \text{ ft}$, you have mapped a **Fault**.

### Isopach Maps
An isopach map does not show elevation; it shows **thickness**.
*   An isopach line connects points where the rock layer is exactly the same thickness.
*   If you map a beach sandstone, the isopach map might show a thickness of 100 feet near the ancient shoreline, tapering off to 0 feet (pinching out) toward the deep ocean.
*   Isopach maps are critical for calculating exactly how much rock volume exists in the reservoir to estimate how much oil is trapped inside.

## 2. Geological Cross-Sections

A map looks down from the sky. A **Cross-Section** looks at the Earth from the side, as if you took a giant knife and sliced the Earth in half like a cake.

To build a cross-section:
1.  Draw a straight line across your map, intersecting several drilled wells.
2.  Plot the vertical data from each well side-by-side. 
3.  Identify the exact depth where Well A hit the top of the "Red Sandstone," and the depth where Well B hit it.
4.  **Correlation:** Draw a line connecting the top of the Red Sandstone from Well A to Well B. Do this for all rock layers.

This visualizes the folding, faulting, and thickness changes of the rock layers across a massive distance. 

## 3. Correlating Well Logs

"Connecting the dots" on a cross-section sounds easy, but it is incredibly difficult. If you have two wells drilled 5 miles apart, how do you mathematically prove that the 50-foot thick sandstone found at 8,000 feet in Well A is the exact same continuous rock layer as the 40-foot thick sandstone found at 8,200 feet in Well B?

Geologists use **Well Logs** (electrical/radioactive measurements taken inside the borehole) to "correlate" the rocks.
*   A specific layer of rock often has a highly unique "fingerprint" on the log (e.g., an unusually high spike in gamma radiation caused by an ancient layer of volcanic ash).
*   By finding this exact same unique gamma-ray spike in both Well A and Well B, the geologist has a "Marker Bed." They can definitively tie the two wells together and prove the layers are continuous.

## 4. The Fluid Contacts on Maps

Once the trap is mapped, the final step is to map the fluids inside it.
If one well drilled the trap and hit oil, but the well was drilled near the edge of the dome, where exactly does the oil end and the water begin?

By analyzing the pressures in the well and the logs, the geologist determines the depth of the **Oil-Water Contact (OWC)** (e.g., the oil turns to water at exactly $-4,200 \text{ ft}$ subsea).

Because fluid contacts are governed by gravity, the OWC is almost always perfectly flat and horizontal. 
Therefore, on the Structural Contour Map, the geologist simply traces the $-4,200 \text{ ft}$ contour line. 
Everything inside that line (higher elevation) is the oil field. Everything outside that line (lower elevation) is worthless saltwater. 
The area inside that line is the "Spill Point" — the maximum possible volume of the trap before the oil spills out from under the seal.
