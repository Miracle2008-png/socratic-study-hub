# Source Rocks and Maturation

The success of any petroleum exploration venture hinges on the quality of the "kitchen" — the source rock where the hydrocarbons are generated. If the source rock is poor, or if it was never cooked properly, the rest of the petroleum system is irrelevant.

Understanding source rocks requires analyzing their organic richness, the type of organic matter they contain, and their thermal maturity.

## 1. Evaluating Organic Richness (TOC)

Not all black shales contain oil. The fundamental metric for evaluating a potential source rock is its **Total Organic Carbon (TOC)** content, measured as a weight percentage of the rock.

*   **TOC < 0.5%:** Poor source rock. Cannot generate commercial quantities of oil.
*   **TOC 1.0% - 2.0%:** Good source rock.
*   **TOC > 4.0%:** Excellent, world-class source rock. (Some exceptional shales, like the Green River Formation in the US, can have TOCs exceeding 10%).

However, TOC only tells you *how much* organic carbon is present. It does not tell you if that carbon is capable of generating oil or gas. For example, a rock filled with pure coal has a massive TOC, but it will primarily generate dry gas, not valuable liquid oil.

## 2. Types of Kerogen

The specific type of hydrocarbon generated (oil vs. gas) depends entirely on the original biological organism that died to form the kerogen. Geochemists classify kerogen into four distinct types based on their Hydrogen-to-Carbon (H/C) and Oxygen-to-Carbon (O/C) ratios.

*   **Type I Kerogen (Algal):** Derived primarily from algae in freshwater lakes. It has a very high Hydrogen content. When cooked, it generates **highly prized, lightweight, high-quality liquid crude oil**. It is relatively rare globally.
*   **Type II Kerogen (Marine):** Derived from marine phytoplankton and zooplankton in ancient oceans. This is the most common type of oil-producing kerogen globally. It generates a mix of **good quality crude oil and natural gas**.
*   **Type III Kerogen (Terrestrial):** Derived from woody land plants, trees, and cellulose. Because wood is very low in hydrogen and high in oxygen, it cannot chemically form liquid oil. When cooked, Type III kerogen generates almost exclusively **Natural Gas (Methane)** and coal.
*   **Type IV Kerogen (Inert):** Organic matter that has been heavily oxidized (burned or decayed) before being buried. It looks like charcoal. It has no hydrogen left and generates **absolutely nothing**. It is dead carbon.

## 3. Thermal Maturation: The Cooking Process

A rock with 5% Type II kerogen is a fantastic potential source rock, but if it is only buried 1,000 feet deep, it is useless. The temperature is too low ($30^\circ\text{C}$). The kerogen is "immature."

To track how "cooked" a source rock is, geologists use several geochemical indicators.

### Vitrinite Reflectance ($R_o$)
This is the most widely used measure of thermal maturity. Vitrinite is a specific shiny organic compound found in plant matter (often mixed into marine shales). 
As the rock gets hotter over millions of years, the vitrinite undergoes chemical changes that make it physically shinier (more reflective to light).
By bouncing a microscopic beam of light off a polished rock sample and measuring the percentage of light reflected ($R_o$), geologists can determine the exact maximum temperature the rock has ever experienced.

*   **$R_o < 0.6\%$:** Immature. The rock has not reached the oil window.
*   **$R_o$ between $0.6\%$ and $1.3\%$:** **The Oil Window.** The rock is actively generating liquid crude oil. Peak oil generation usually occurs around $R_o = 0.9\%$.
*   **$R_o$ between $1.3\%$ and $2.0\%$:** **The Gas Window.** The heat is cracking the oil into natural gas.
*   **$R_o > 2.0\%$:** Overmature. The hydrocarbons are destroyed. Only graphite remains.

## 4. Rock-Eval Pyrolysis

To determine both TOC, Kerogen Type, and Maturity in a single test, labs use a machine called a Rock-Eval Pyrolyzer.
A small sample of the crushed source rock is placed in an oven and slowly heated to $600^\circ\text{C}$ in an oxygen-free environment.

The machine measures the hydrocarbons released at different temperatures:
*   **S1 Peak:** Hydrocarbons that are *already* liquid oil sitting in the rock. They evaporate at low temperatures.
*   **S2 Peak:** Hydrocarbons generated by actively cracking the solid kerogen at high temperatures. This represents the *remaining potential* of the source rock.
*   **Tmax:** The specific temperature at which the S2 peak reaches its absolute maximum. A higher Tmax indicates the rock is more thermally mature.

By analyzing the S1, S2, and Tmax data, geologists can definitively say: "This rock was a Type II kerogen, it has already generated half of its oil, and it still has the potential to generate more if buried deeper."
