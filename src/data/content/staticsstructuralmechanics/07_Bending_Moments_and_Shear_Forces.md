# Bending Moments and Shear Forces

## Transverse Loading on Beams

While columns carry vertical axial loads, beams are horizontal structural elements designed to carry transverse loads—forces applied perpendicularly to their longitudinal axis. A floor joist carrying the weight of a living room, a massive concrete bridge girder carrying highway traffic, and the wing of an aircraft carrying aerodynamic lift are all classic examples of beams. 

When a transverse load is placed on a beam, the load attempts to push the beam downward. Because the beam is supported at its ends (by columns or abutments), these supports push back upward to maintain static equilibrium. This conflict of forces creates two profound internal reactions within the beam: **Shear Force ($V$)** and **Bending Moment ($M$)**. Understanding and quantifying these two internal effects at every single millimeter along the length of the beam is the most fundamental task in structural design.

## The Nature of Shear and Moment

**Internal Shear Force ($V$)** acts vertically inside the beam. Imagine a stack of playing cards acting as a beam spanning across two books. If you press down on the center, the cards will try to slide vertically past each other. This tendency for adjacent vertical slices of the beam to slide past one another is driven by the shear force. If a beam fails in shear, it typically tears vertically right next to its heavy column supports, plunging the span downward.

**Internal Bending Moment ($M$)** is the rotational torque generated inside the beam that causes it to bow or bend into a curve. When a beam sags downward in the middle under a heavy load, it takes on a "U" shape. The material at the very bottom of the beam physically stretches and is put into tension, while the material at the very top of the beam compresses together. This simultaneous tension and compression creates an internal force couple—a bending moment. This moment dictates the size of the beam required; a deeper beam provides a larger lever arm between the top compression flange and the bottom tension flange, making it vastly more efficient at resisting bending moments.

## Shear and Moment Diagrams

Because the loads on civil structures are complex—ranging from uniformly distributed dead weights of concrete slabs to concentrated moving point loads of heavy semi-trucks—the internal shear and moment are not constant. They vary drastically from one end of the beam to the other. Engineers visualize this variation by drawing Shear Force Diagrams (SFD) and Bending Moment Diagrams (BMD).

These diagrams are generated by mathematically "cutting" the beam at an arbitrary distance $x$ from the support and using static equilibrium equations to solve for $V$ and $M$ as functions of $x$. The diagrams visually reveal the critical design locations: where the shear force is mathematically at its absolute maximum (typically at the supports) and where the bending moment is at its peak (often near the midspan for simply supported beams). There is also a profound calculus relationship between the two: the internal shear force is the exact mathematical derivative of the bending moment equation ($V = dM/dx$). Consequently, the maximum bending moment occurs precisely at the point where the shear force diagram crosses zero.

## Mathematical Examples

### Example 1: Point Load on a Simply Supported Beam
Consider a wooden beam of length $L = 10 \, \text{m}$ resting on two simple supports at its ends ($A$ and $B$). A single concentrated heavy machine weighing $P = 50 \, \text{kN}$ is placed exactly in the middle ($x = 5 \, \text{m}$). Determine the maximum shear and moment.
By symmetry, the upward reaction forces at the supports are equal:
$$ R_A = R_B = \frac{P}{2} = \frac{50}{2} = 25 \, \text{kN} $$
The shear force ($V$) immediately jumps to $+25 \, \text{kN}$ at support $A$, remains constant until the midspan, drops by $50 \, \text{kN}$ to $-25 \, \text{kN}$ at the load, and remains constant until support $B$. 
Maximum Shear $V_{max} = 25 \, \text{kN}$ at the supports.
The bending moment ($M$) is zero at the pinned ends and increases linearly to a maximum directly under the point load. The formula for maximum moment for a center point load is:
$$ M_{max} = \frac{P \cdot L}{4} = \frac{50 \times 10}{4} = 125 \, \text{kN}\cdot\text{m} $$
The beam must be sized to resist $125 \, \text{kN}\cdot\text{m}$ of bending torque at its center.

### Example 2: Uniformly Distributed Load (UDL)
Consider a concrete roof beam spanning $L = 8 \, \text{m}$. It carries a uniform dead load of concrete and roofing materials equal to $w = 12 \, \text{kN/m}$ spread evenly across the entire length.
The total downward force is $W = w \cdot L = 12 \times 8 = 96 \, \text{kN}$.
By symmetry, the end reactions are:
$$ R_A = R_B = \frac{96}{2} = 48 \, \text{kN} $$
The shear force is maximum at the supports ($V_{max} = 48 \, \text{kN}$) and decreases linearly at a rate of $-12 \, \text{kN/m}$ until it crosses zero exactly at the midspan ($x = 4 \, \text{m}$).
Because the shear crosses zero at the midspan, the bending moment is maximum there. The formula for the maximum moment of a simply supported beam with a UDL is a fundamental equation in civil engineering:
$$ M_{max} = \frac{w \cdot L^2}{8} $$
$$ M_{max} = \frac{12 \times (8)^2}{8} = \frac{12 \times 64}{8} = 12 \times 8 = 96 \, \text{kN}\cdot\text{m} $$
Notice that while the total load is roughly twice that of Example 1, spreading it out results in a lower peak bending moment.

### Example 3: The Calculus Relationship
Suppose the shear force diagram of a beam over the span from $x=0$ to $x=4\text{m}$ is a constant $V(x) = 20 \, \text{kN}$. If the bending moment at the support ($x=0$) is $M(0) = 0$, what is the bending moment at $x = 4\text{m}$?
We use the integral relationship: the change in moment is equal to the area under the shear force diagram.
$$ M(4) - M(0) = \int_{0}^{4} V(x) dx $$
Since $V$ is a constant $20$, the integral (area of a rectangle) is simply base times height:
$$ M(4) - 0 = (4 \, \text{m}) \times (20 \, \text{kN}) = 80 \, \text{kN}\cdot\text{m} $$
The bending moment at $x=4\text{m}$ is exactly $80 \, \text{kN}\cdot\text{m}$.
