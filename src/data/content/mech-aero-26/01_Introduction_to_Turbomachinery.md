# Introduction to Turbomachinery

**Turbomachines** are devices that transfer energy between a fluid and a rotating shaft using dynamically shaped blades or vanes. They are the workhorses of modern engineering, powering everything from municipal water supplies to jet aircraft and hydroelectric dams.

Unlike positive displacement machines (like a piston-cylinder or gear pump), which trap discrete volumes of fluid and physically squeeze them, turbomachines operate on a continuous, steady flow of fluid relying entirely on dynamic interactions (momentum transfer).

## 1. Classification by Energy Transfer Direction

Turbomachines are broadly divided into two categories based on whether they add energy to the fluid or extract energy from it.

### Power-Absorbing Machines (Pumps, Fans, Compressors)
These devices receive mechanical work from a rotating shaft (driven by an electric motor or engine) and transfer that energy *into* the fluid, increasing its pressure, velocity, or elevation.
- **Pumps:** Handle liquids (incompressible). Primarily increase pressure to move water or oil through pipes.
- **Fans:** Handle gases (mostly air) at very low pressure ratios. Designed to move large volumes of air for ventilation or cooling, rather than to compress it.
- **Blowers:** Handle gases at moderate pressure ratios.
- **Compressors:** Handle gases at high pressure ratios. Because gases are compressible, compressors significantly reduce the fluid's volume and increase its density and temperature.

### Power-Producing Machines (Turbines)
These devices extract energy *from* a flowing fluid and convert it into mechanical work on a rotating shaft (which often drives an electrical generator).
- **Hydraulic Turbines:** Extract energy from liquids (usually water in hydroelectric dams).
- **Steam Turbines:** Extract energy from high-pressure, high-temperature steam (used in nuclear and coal power plants).
- **Gas Turbines:** Extract energy from high-temperature combustion gases (used in jet engines and natural gas power plants).
- **Wind Turbines:** Extract kinetic energy from atmospheric wind.

## 2. Classification by Flow Direction

Turbomachines are also classified by the primary path the fluid takes as it passes through the rotating blades (the **rotor** or **impeller**).

1. **Radial-Flow (Centrifugal) Machines:** 
   - Fluid enters axially (parallel to the shaft) and is flung outward radially (perpendicular to the shaft) by centrifugal force.
   - Example: A standard centrifugal water pump or a turbocharger compressor.
   - Best for: High pressure changes, relatively lower flow rates.
2. **Axial-Flow Machines:** 
   - Fluid flows parallel to the axis of rotation entirely through the machine.
   - Example: A desk fan, an airplane propeller, or a jet engine compressor.
   - Best for: Very high mass flow rates, relatively lower pressure changes.
3. **Mixed-Flow Machines:** 
   - Fluid has both significant radial and axial velocity components as it exits the rotor. It represents a compromise between radial and axial designs.

## 3. Key Components

A typical turbomachine consists of a few essential parts:
- **Rotor (Impeller, Runner):** The rotating assembly of blades attached to the shaft. This is where the actual energy transfer occurs.
- **Stator (Volute, Casing, Guide Vanes):** The stationary housing. In a pump, the volute collects the fast-moving fluid exiting the rotor and slows it down to convert kinetic energy into static pressure. In a turbine, stationary guide vanes (nozzles) accelerate the fluid and direct it onto the rotor blades at the optimal angle.
