# Residual Stresses in Bending

When a beam is bent heavily into the inelastic (elasto-plastic) region and the applied bending moment is subsequently removed, the beam springs back, but it does not return perfectly to its original straight shape. 

It retains a permanent curve (permanent set), and massive internal stresses remain locked inside the cross-section, even with zero external load. These are **residual stresses**.

## 1. The Superposition of Unloading

Calculating residual stresses in bending follows the exact same logical procedure as in torsion: we superimpose the loading and unloading phases.

1.  **Loading Phase:** The beam is loaded to a moment $M$ that causes partial plastic yielding. The stress distribution consists of flat plastic zones ($\sigma = \sigma_Y$) at the outer edges and a linear elastic core in the middle.
2.  **Unloading Phase:** The removal of the moment is modeled as applying a fictitious reverse moment equal to $-M$. 
    Because unloading is almost perfectly elastic (the material recovers along a line parallel to the initial linear region of the stress-strain curve), the stress distribution caused by the $-M$ unloading moment is perfectly linear, calculated using the standard flexure formula: $\sigma = \frac{-(-M)y}{I}$.

## 2. Calculating the Residual Profile

The final locked-in residual stress at any depth $y$ is the algebraic sum of the stress from the loading phase and the stress from the elastic unloading phase:

$$ \sigma_{residual} = \sigma_{loading} + \sigma_{unloading} $$

## 3. Characteristics of the Residual Stress Profile

The resulting residual stress profile is highly counter-intuitive:
*   **Outer Fibers:** During loading, the top fibers were in heavy compression ($\sigma = -\sigma_Y$). During the massive elastic unloading, they spring back intensely. The resulting residual stress at the top outer fiber is actually **tensile**.
*   **Inner Fibers:** Just inside the elastic core, the loading stress was moderate. The massive elastic unloading overpowers it, resulting in a residual stress of the opposite sign. 
*   **Neutral Axis:** The stress remains exactly zero.

The total summation of the forces from this residual stress profile across the entire cross-section is zero ($\sum F = 0$), and the total internal moment generated by these residual stresses is exactly zero ($\sum M = 0$).

## 4. Springback and Permanent Curvature

When the moment is removed, the beam undergoes an elastic recovery (springback), curving back toward its original shape by a specific angle.

Because the loading was partially plastic, the elastic springback is not enough to return the beam to straightness. It is left with a permanent radius of curvature $\rho_{residual}$.

This principle is heavily utilized in manufacturing operations like sheet metal rolling and brake-press bending. Engineers must intentionally "over-bend" the metal past the desired angle, precisely calculating the elastic springback, so that the final piece rests exactly at the required dimension, harboring locked-in residual stresses.
