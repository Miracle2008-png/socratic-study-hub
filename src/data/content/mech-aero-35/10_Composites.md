# Composites

When monolithic materials (metals, ceramics, polymers) reach their performance limits, engineers turn to composites. A composite material is a macroscopic combination of two or more distinct materials, with a distinct interface between them, designed to achieve a combination of properties that cannot be met by any of the constituents alone.

The Principle of Combined Action: A composite aims to maximize the strengths of its components while mitigating their weaknesses.

## 1. Anatomy of a Composite

Composites generally consist of two phases:

1.  **The Matrix Phase:** The continuous phase that surrounds and binds the other material together. Its primary functions are:
    -   To hold the reinforcement in the correct orientation.
    -   To transfer applied loads (stress) to the reinforcement.
    -   To protect the reinforcement from environmental damage (corrosion, abrasion).
    -   *Types:* Polymer Matrix (PMC), Metal Matrix (MMC), Ceramic Matrix (CMC).

2.  **The Dispersed Phase (Reinforcement):** The material embedded within the matrix, usually added to enhance strength, stiffness, thermal conductivity, or wear resistance.

## 2. Types of Reinforcement

Composites are classified by the geometry of their dispersed phase.

**1. Particle-Reinforced Composites:**
-   Small particles are dispersed uniformly throughout the matrix.
-   *Examples:* Concrete (gravel/sand dispersed in a cement matrix). Automobile tire rubber (carbon black particles added to rubber to massively increase tensile strength and wear resistance). Tungsten Carbide cutting tools (extremely hard WC particles embedded in a tough Cobalt matrix to prevent shattering).

**2. Fiber-Reinforced Composites (FRCs):**
The most important class of composites in aerospace and high-performance engineering.
-   **The Concept:** Materials are often incredibly strong when drawn into a microscopic fiber (because the small volume minimizes the chance of having a catastrophic structural defect like a crack). FRCs bundle these incredibly strong fibers and use a matrix to hold them together and transfer loads to them.
-   **Continuous Fibers:** Long fibers running the entire length of the part. They are highly **anisotropic**—the composite is incredibly strong and stiff in the direction parallel to the fibers, but very weak perpendicular to the fibers (where only the matrix is holding it together).
-   **Discontinuous (Chopped) Fibers:** Short fibers mixed randomly in the matrix. Lower strength, but isotropic (properties are roughly equal in all directions) and much cheaper to manufacture (e.g., fiberglass boat hulls).

## 3. Common Advanced Composites

**1. Polymer Matrix Composites (PMCs):**
-   **Fiberglass (GFRP):** Glass fibers in a polyester or epoxy resin. High strength, cheap, corrosion-resistant, but relatively low stiffness.
-   **Carbon Fiber (CFRP):** Carbon fibers in an epoxy matrix. The undisputed king of high-performance lightweight structures. Exceptional specific strength (strength-to-weight ratio) and specific stiffness. Used for the entire fuselage of the Boeing 787, Formula 1 monocoques, and high-end sporting goods.
-   **Kevlar (Aramid):** Aramid polymer fibers in an epoxy matrix. Known for incredible impact resistance and toughness. Used in ballistic armor, bulletproof vests, and rocket motor casings.

**2. Metal Matrix Composites (MMCs):**
-   A ductile metal matrix (like Aluminum or Titanium) reinforced with ceramic particulates or fibers (like Silicon Carbide).
-   Offers higher operating temperatures and better stiffness than typical metal alloys, without the extreme brittleness of a pure ceramic.

**3. Ceramic Matrix Composites (CMCs):**
-   An attempt to make ceramics "tough." A ceramic matrix reinforced with ceramic fibers (e.g., Silicon Carbide fibers in a Silicon Carbide matrix).
-   If a crack forms in the matrix, it hits the strong fibers. Instead of slicing clean through (shattering the part), the crack energy is absorbed as the fibers slightly pull out of the matrix or deflect the crack.
-   Allows components to operate at extreme temperatures (jet engine turbine blades, hypersonic vehicle leading edges) without catastrophically shattering.

## 4. Laminates and Sandwich Panels

To overcome the directional weakness of continuous fiber composites, engineers create **Laminates**.
-   Thin sheets (plies) of unidirectional carbon fiber/epoxy are stacked on top of each other.
-   Each ply is rotated to a different angle (e.g., 0°, 90°, +45°, -45°).
-   When glued together and cured under heat and pressure, the resulting laminate has tailored strength in multiple directions.

**Sandwich Panels:**
Used for ultimate bending stiffness with minimal weight (like an airplane floor panel).
-   Two thin, strong outer "face sheets" (e.g., CFRP laminates or aluminum).
-   Separated by a thick, ultra-lightweight "core" (e.g., aluminum honeycomb or structural foam).
-   It acts like an I-beam: the face sheets handle the bending stresses, while the lightweight core holds them apart to maximize the moment of inertia.
