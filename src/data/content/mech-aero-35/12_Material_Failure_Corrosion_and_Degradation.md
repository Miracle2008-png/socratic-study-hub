# Material Failure: Corrosion and Degradation

Materials do not just fail mechanically; they also fail chemically. **Corrosion** is the destructive and unintentional degradation of a material caused by its environment. For metals, it is an electrochemical process. For polymers, it is referred to as degradation.

Corrosion costs the global economy trillions of dollars annually in maintenance, replacement, and catastrophic failures (bridge collapses, pipeline explosions).

## 1. The Thermodynamics of Rust

Metals (except for noble metals like gold and platinum) do not want to be metals. In nature, iron exists as iron oxide (iron ore). We expend massive amounts of energy in blast furnaces to rip the oxygen away and create pure iron or steel.

Corrosion is simply the universe taking that energy back. The metal is returning to its lowest, most stable thermodynamic energy state—its natural oxide compound.

## 2. The Electrochemical Cell

For metallic corrosion (especially in aqueous environments) to occur, an electrochemical cell must be established. This requires four components:

1.  **The Anode:** The region of the metal that undergoes oxidation. Atoms of the metal give up electrons and dissolve into the electrolyte as positive ions. **This is where the actual physical destruction (pitting, rusting) occurs.**
    *   $Fe \to Fe^{2+} + 2e^-$
2.  **The Cathode:** The region that undergoes reduction. It consumes the electrons produced by the anode. It is protected from destruction. (Often, oxygen from the air reacts with water and these electrons to form hydroxide ions).
    *   $O_2 + 2H_2O + 4e^- \to 4OH^-$
3.  **The Electrolyte:** A liquid that can conduct ions (e.g., saltwater, humid air, soil) connecting the anode and cathode.
4.  **The Electrical Connection:** The metal itself serves as the wire, allowing electrons to flow from the anode to the cathode.

If you eliminate *any one* of these four components, corrosion stops completely.

## 3. Types of Corrosion

Corrosion manifests in various insidious ways:

*   **Uniform Attack:** The most common form (e.g., a rusty steel plate). It occurs evenly over the entire exposed surface. While destructive, it is predictable and leaves measurable thickness over time.
*   **Galvanic Corrosion:** Occurs when two *different* metals are in electrical contact within an electrolyte. One metal will act as the anode and corrode rapidly, while the other acts as the cathode and is protected. (e.g., using steel screws to hold copper flashing; the steel screws will rust away rapidly).
*   **Pitting:** A highly localized, extremely dangerous form of corrosion. A tiny pit forms on the surface and rapidly drills deep into the metal. It is dangerous because a pipe can look fine on the surface but be entirely perforated by a microscopic pit.
*   **Crevice Corrosion:** Occurs in narrow crevices (under a washer, or between two riveted plates) where stagnant fluid accumulates. The fluid becomes depleted of oxygen, changing the local chemistry and turning the crevice into a highly aggressive anode.

## 4. Corrosion Prevention Strategies

Engineers use several strategies, often in combination, to break the electrochemical circuit:

1.  **Material Selection:** Choose a metal appropriate for the environment. Stainless steel is "stainless" because it contains Chromium (>11%). The chromium instantly reacts with oxygen to form a microscopic, transparent, and totally impermeable layer of chromium oxide on the surface (passivation). This layer seals the iron away from the electrolyte.
2.  **Coatings:** Paint, enamel, or polymer coatings create a physical barrier between the metal and the electrolyte. (Breaks component #3).
3.  **Galvanic Protection (Sacrificial Anode):** Deliberately connect a more reactive (more anodic) metal to the structure you want to protect.
    *   Zinc blocks are bolted to the steel hulls of ships. The zinc acts as the anode and corrodes completely away over time, forcing the entire steel ship to act as the cathode, thereby protecting it from rusting.
4.  **Cathodic Protection (Impressed Current):** Used for large pipelines. An external DC power supply forces a constant stream of electrons into the pipeline. By flooding the pipe with electrons, it is forced to act as a cathode, preventing it from giving up electrons (oxidizing).

## 5. Polymer Degradation

Polymers do not corrode electrochemically, but they degrade.
*   **UV Degradation:** Ultraviolet photons from sunlight have enough energy to sever the covalent bonds in the polymer backbone, making plastics brittle and chalky over time (e.g., old patio furniture). Carbon black is often added to tires and pipes to absorb UV light and protect the polymer.
*   **Solvent Swelling/Dissolution:** Certain chemicals can seep between the polymer chains, pushing them apart (swelling) or completely dissolving the secondary bonds, turning the solid plastic into a gooey liquid.
