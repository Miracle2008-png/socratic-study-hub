# Internal Incompressible Flow and Piping

In Module 3, we defined the Mechanical Energy Balance, which featured a mysterious term called "Head Loss" ($h_L$). Head loss represents the mechanical energy permanently lost to fluid friction. Calculating this friction is the primary day-to-day task of a piping engineer.

## 1. Major Losses (Pipe Friction)

Friction generated by the fluid rubbing against the long, straight walls of a pipe is called **Major Head Loss**. It is calculated using the **Darcy-Weisbach Equation**:

$$ h_{L,\text{major}} = f \frac{L}{D} \frac{V^2}{2g} $$

Where:
- $L$: Length of the pipe
- $D$: Diameter of the pipe
- $V$: Average fluid velocity
- $f$: The Darcy Friction Factor

The entire difficulty of piping design lies in finding the value of $f$.

### Finding the Friction Factor ($f$)
The friction factor is a dimensionless number that depends entirely on the flow regime.

**For Laminar Flow ($Re < 2100$):**
Friction is caused purely by the sliding of fluid layers. It is mathematically exact and depends only on the Reynolds number. It is completely independent of how rough the pipe wall is.
$$ f = \frac{64}{Re} $$

**For Turbulent Flow ($Re > 4000$):**
Friction is caused by chaotic eddies smashing into the pipe walls. The rougher the wall, the more violent the collisions, and the higher the friction. Therefore, in turbulent flow, $f$ depends on both the Reynolds number ($Re$) and the Relative Roughness ($\varepsilon/D$) of the pipe material.
The value is calculated using the implicit **Colebrook Equation**:
$$ \frac{1}{\sqrt{f}} = -2.0 \log_{10} \left( \frac{\varepsilon/D}{3.7} + \frac{2.51}{Re \sqrt{f}} \right) $$
Because this equation cannot be solved algebraically for $f$, engineers historically used a graphical representation of this equation known as the **Moody Chart**. Today, it is solved iteratively by computers or using explicit approximations like the Haaland equation.

## 2. Minor Losses (Fittings and Valves)

Whenever fluid goes through a valve, turns a 90-degree elbow, or enters a tank, the flow streamlines are violently disrupted. The fluid separates from the walls, creating swirling zones of dead water that dissipate massive amounts of energy. 

These localized energy losses are called **Minor Losses**. Despite the name, in complex chemical plants with short, twisting pipes, "minor" losses can actually be much larger than the "major" friction losses of the straight pipes.

Minor losses are calculated using a loss coefficient ($K_L$):
$$ h_{L,\text{minor}} = K_L \frac{V^2}{2g} $$

Values for $K_L$ are determined entirely through experiments and are provided in engineering handbooks. A wide-open gate valve might have a $K_L$ of 0.2, while a half-closed globe valve might have a $K_L$ of 6.0.

## 3. Total Head Loss

To find the total energy required by a pump, the engineer sums all the major and minor losses in the system:

$$ h_L = \sum h_{L,\text{major}} + \sum h_{L,\text{minor}} $$
$$ h_L = \left( \sum f \frac{L}{D} + \sum K_L \right) \frac{V^2}{2g} $$

This total $h_L$ is then plugged back into the Mechanical Energy Balance to solve for the required pump head ($h_p$).
