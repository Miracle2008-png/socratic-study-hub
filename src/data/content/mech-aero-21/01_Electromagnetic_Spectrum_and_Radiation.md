# Electromagnetic Spectrum and Thermal Radiation

Unlike conduction and convection, which require a material medium (solid or fluid) to transfer heat, **thermal radiation** can propagate perfectly through a pure vacuum. In fact, it transfers heat *most* efficiently in a vacuum, as there is no matter to absorb or scatter the energy.

## 1. The Mechanism of Radiation

All matter at a temperature above absolute zero ($T > 0$ K) continuously emits thermal radiation.
- **Microscopic cause:** The thermal energy (temperature) of a substance causes its atoms and molecules to vibrate and rotate. Because these particles consist of charged protons and electrons, their acceleration generates electromagnetic waves.
- **Macroscopic result:** The body emits electromagnetic energy into its surroundings, lowering its own thermal energy unless replenished.

## 2. The Electromagnetic Spectrum

Electromagnetic waves are characterized by their frequency $\nu$ [Hz] and wavelength $\lambda$ [m], related by the speed of light $c$:
$$c = \lambda \nu$$
(In a vacuum, $c = 2.998 \times 10^8$ m/s).

The electromagnetic spectrum spans from massive radio waves ($\lambda \sim 10^3$ m) to minuscule gamma rays ($\lambda \sim 10^{-12}$ m).

**Thermal Radiation** is just one specific slice of this vast spectrum. It is defined as the electromagnetic radiation emitted by a body *solely due to its temperature*. 
- It encompasses wavelengths from approximately **0.1 $\mu$m to 100 $\mu$m**.
- This band includes a portion of the ultraviolet (UV), the entire **visible light** spectrum (0.4 to 0.76 $\mu$m), and the entire **infrared** (IR) region.

## 3. Light vs. Heat

It is a common misconception that "infrared is heat and visible is light." 
- *All* thermal radiation is "heat" in the sense that it transfers thermal energy.
- The sun (surface temp $\sim$5800 K) emits the majority of its thermal radiation in the visible spectrum. When sunlight hits your skin, the visible light is absorbed and converted into thermal energy, making you feel warm.
- A hot cup of coffee ($\sim$350 K) emits almost entirely in the infrared spectrum. You cannot see it glowing, but an infrared camera can, and your hand can feel the radiated heat if held close.

## 4. Directional and Spectral Nature of Radiation

Thermal radiation is highly complex because it is:
1. **Spectral (Wavelength-dependent):** A surface does not emit radiation at just one wavelength; it emits a continuous spectrum across many wavelengths. The intensity of emission varies wildly depending on the specific wavelength.
2. **Directional:** A rough surface may emit radiation equally in all directions, but polished or structured surfaces may emit preferentially in specific directions.

To fully characterize radiation, engineers must often analyze it on a *spectral* (per unit wavelength) and *directional* (per unit solid angle) basis before integrating to find total heat transfer.
