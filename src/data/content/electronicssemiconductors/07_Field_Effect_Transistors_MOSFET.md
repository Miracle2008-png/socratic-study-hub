# 7. Field-Effect Transistors (MOSFET)

## The Problem with BJTs

Bipolar Junction Transistors (BJTs) are incredible amplifiers, but they have one massive flaw when used as switches in digital computers: **They require constant Base current to stay "On".** 
If a BJT is holding a logic "1", it is constantly burning power. If you pack 10 billion BJTs into a microchip, the heat generated by these Base currents would instantly melt the silicon into slag.

To build modern computers, we need a transistor that can be turned ON without drawing *any* continuous current.
Welcome to the **Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor Field-Effect Transistor (MOSFET)**.

---

## The N-Channel MOSFET (NMOS)

The NMOS has three main terminals:
1.  **Drain (D):** The intake for current (similar to BJT Collector).
2.  **Source (S):** The exhaust for current (similar to BJT Emitter).
3.  **Gate (G):** The control valve (similar to BJT Base).

### The Insulated Gate
In a BJT, the Base is physically touching the silicon layers. 
In a MOSFET, the Gate is **completely insulated** from the silicon by a microscopic layer of Silicon Dioxide ($SiO_2$, basically glass). 
Because of this glass wall, **no current can ever flow into the Gate ($I_G = 0$)**. 

### How it Works (The Physics)
Below the Gate glass is a substrate of P-type silicon (full of holes). The Drain and Source are blocks of N-type silicon. Normally, no current can flow from Drain to Source because there is no N-type path between them.
When you apply a positive voltage to the Gate, the metal plate charges up like a capacitor. This creates a strong electrostatic **Field** that pushes the holes away and pulls stray electrons up to the surface.
These electrons form an "N-channel" right under the glass, creating a physical bridge between the Drain and Source! 
**Result:** By merely applying a *voltage* to the Gate, you create an electric field that turns the switch ON, drawing absolute zero control current!

---

## NMOS vs PMOS

1.  **NMOS:** Uses an N-channel. It turns ON when the Gate voltage is positive (e.g., $5\text{V}$ logic "1"). It is excellent at pulling outputs down to Ground.
2.  **PMOS:** Uses a P-channel. It turns ON when the Gate voltage is negative (e.g., $0\text{V}$ logic "0"). It is excellent at pulling outputs up to $V_{CC}$.

When you combine NMOS and PMOS together on the same chip, you get **CMOS** (Complementary MOS), the technology behind 99.9% of all digital chips in existence today.

---

## Engineering Applications

*   **Zero Static Power:** Because the Gate is insulated, a MOSFET holding a logic state draws $0.000000001\text{A}$ of current. A smartphone CPU can have 15 billion transistors and still run off a battery all day. The only time a MOSFET consumes power is during the nanosecond it is actively switching states (charging the Gate capacitance). This is why overclocking a CPU (making it switch faster) drastically increases its heat output!
*   **Power Electronics:** "Power MOSFETs" are designed with vertical silicon trenches to handle absurd amounts of current. A single Power MOSFET the size of a fingernail can switch $100\text{A}$ at $400\text{V}$, making them the heart of Electric Vehicle motor controllers and solar inverters.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Easy: Gate Current

**Problem:** A microcontroller applies $3.3\text{V}$ to the Gate of an NMOS transistor. The insulating oxide layer has a resistance of $10^{13} \, \Omega$. Calculate the steady-state Gate current ($I_G$).

**Solution:**
1.  **Apply Ohm's Law:**
    $$ I_G = \frac{V_G}{R_{oxide}} = \frac{3.3}{10^{13}} $$
    $$ I_G = \mathbf{3.3 \times 10^{-13} \text{ A (0.33 picoamps)}} $$
*(For all practical engineering purposes, $I_G = 0$. The microcontroller does no work to keep the transistor ON!)*

---

### Example 2 — Medium: The Threshold Voltage ($V_{th}$)

**Problem:** For an NMOS transistor to turn ON, the Gate-to-Source voltage ($V_{GS}$) must exceed a physical limit called the Threshold Voltage ($V_{th}$). Below $V_{th}$, the N-channel bridge hasn't fully formed.
You have an NMOS with $V_{th} = 2.0\text{V}$. The Source is connected to Ground ($0\text{V}$). The Drain is connected to a $12\text{V}$ motor.
Will the motor spin if you apply $1.5\text{V}$ to the Gate? What if you apply $5.0\text{V}$?

**Solution:**
1.  **Case 1: $V_{GS} = 1.5\text{V}$**
    Because $V_{GS} (1.5) < V_{th} (2.0)$, the channel does not form. The transistor remains in **Cutoff**. The switch is open. The motor **WILL NOT** spin.
2.  **Case 2: $V_{GS} = 5.0\text{V}$**
    Because $V_{GS} (5.0) > V_{th} (2.0)$, a deep N-channel forms. The transistor turns **ON**. The switch closes. The motor **WILL** spin.

---

### Example 3 — Hard: MOSFET Capacitance (Dynamic Power)

**Problem:** Even though $I_G = 0$ in steady state, the Gate acts like a tiny capacitor ($C_{gs}$). To turn the MOSFET on, the microcontroller must pump a burst of current to charge this capacitor up to $V_{GS}$.
An NMOS has a gate capacitance of $C_{gs} = 1000\text{pF}$ ($10^{-9}\text{F}$). A microcontroller switches the gate from $0\text{V}$ to $5\text{V}$.
(A) How much charge ($Q$) in Coulombs is required for one switch?
(B) If the microcontroller switches the transistor ON and OFF $1,000,000$ times a second ($f = 1\text{MHz}$), what is the average continuous current drawn from the microcontroller?

**Solution:**
**(A) Calculate Charge ($Q$):**
The physics formula for a capacitor is $Q = C \times V$.
$$ Q = (10^{-9} \text{ F}) \times (5\text{V}) = \mathbf{5 \times 10^{-9} \text{ Coulombs}} $$

**(B) Calculate Average Current ($I$):**
Current is defined as Charge per second ($I = Q / t$). 
Since it switches $f$ times per second, the total charge moved per second is $I = Q \times f$.
$$ I = (5 \times 10^{-9}) \times (1,000,000) = 5 \times 10^{-3}\text{A} = \mathbf{5\text{mA}} $$
*(Notice! Even though the MOSFET has "zero static power," driving it at high speeds requires significant dynamic power to constantly charge and discharge the gate capacitor. This "Dynamic Power Dissipation" is why modern CPUs hit thermal walls).*
