# Mathematical Modeling of Physical Systems

Before you can design a controller to command a physical machine, you must be able to describe how that machine behaves mathematically. We must translate gears, masses, and electrical circuits into differential equations.

## 1. Linear Time-Invariant (LTI) Systems

Control theory at the undergraduate level focuses almost exclusively on **Linear Time-Invariant (LTI)** systems.

*   **Linear:** The principle of superposition holds. If input A produces output X, and input B produces output Y, then input (A+B) produces output (X+Y). (Real systems are highly non-linear, but we usually linearize them around a specific operating point).
*   **Time-Invariant:** The physics of the system do not change over time. The mass of the car today is the same as the mass of the car tomorrow. (This ignores things like a rocket burning fuel and losing mass during flight).

## 2. Mechanical Systems

Mechanical modeling relies on Newton's Second Law ($\Sigma F = ma$) and its rotational equivalent ($\Sigma \tau = I\alpha$). We represent mechanical systems using three fundamental building blocks:

**1. Mass ($m$) / Inertia ($I$):**
Resists acceleration. It stores kinetic energy.
*   *Translational:* $F = m \ddot{x}$
*   *Rotational:* $\tau = I \ddot{\theta}$

**2. Spring ($k$):**
Resists displacement. It stores potential energy. It attempts to pull the system back to its equilibrium position.
*   *Translational:* $F = k x$
*   *Rotational:* $\tau = k \theta$

**3. Damper ($c$ or $b$):**
Resists velocity. It dissipates energy (usually as heat) through friction or viscous drag. A shock absorber is a damper.
*   *Translational:* $F = c \dot{x}$
*   *Rotational:* $\tau = c \dot{\theta}$

**Example: The Mass-Spring-Damper System**
Consider a car suspension (a mass $m$ attached to a spring $k$ and a shock absorber $c$, being pushed by an external force $F(t)$).
Summing the forces yields a Second-Order Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE):
$$ m\ddot{x}(t) + c\dot{x}(t) + kx(t) = F(t) $$

## 3. Electrical Systems

Electrical systems are modeled using Kirchhoff's Voltage and Current Laws (KVL/KCL), based on three fundamental components:

**1. Inductor ($L$):**
Resists changes in current. It is the electrical equivalent of **Mass**.
*   Voltage: $V = L \frac{di}{dt}$

**2. Capacitor ($C$):**
Resists changes in voltage. It is the electrical equivalent of a **Spring**.
*   Current: $i = C \frac{dv}{dt}$

**3. Resistor ($R$):**
Dissipates energy as heat. It is the electrical equivalent of a **Damper**.
*   Voltage: $V = i R$

**Electromechanical Analogies:**
Because the differential equations governing a mass-spring-damper are mathematically identical to those governing an RLC (Resistor-Inductor-Capacitor) circuit, control theory treats them exactly the same way. You can test a mechanical suspension design by building an electrical circuit on a breadboard.

## 4. Electromechanical Systems (DC Motors)

Most control systems use an electric motor to actuate a mechanical system. We must tie the electrical and mechanical domains together.

A DC motor involves two coupled equations:
1.  **Electrical (Armature Circuit):** The applied voltage ($V$) pushes current ($i$) through the coil resistance ($R$) and inductance ($L$), fighting against the back-EMF ($e_b$) generated by the spinning motor.
    $V - e_b = L\frac{di}{dt} + Ri$
2.  **Mechanical (Rotor Dynamics):** The motor generates torque ($\tau_m$) proportional to the current. This torque accelerates the rotor inertia ($I$) against viscous friction ($c$).
    $\tau_m = I\ddot{\theta} + c\dot{\theta}$

The crucial link is that motor torque is proportional to current ($\tau_m = K_t i$), and back-EMF is proportional to speed ($e_b = K_e \dot{\theta}$).
