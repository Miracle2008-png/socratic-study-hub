# Emulsions and Oil Treatment

The liquid oil that exits the bottom of the separator is rarely pure. To legally sell crude oil to a pipeline, it must contain less than 0.5% BS&W (Basic Sediment and Water). 

However, the fluid exiting the separator often contains 10% to 20% water. This water is not floating freely; it is chemically trapped inside the oil in a complex, microscopic state called an **Emulsion**. If you do not break the emulsion, the pipeline company will refuse to buy your oil.

## 1. What is an Emulsion?

An emulsion is a stable dispersion of one liquid completely trapped inside another liquid, where the two liquids normally refuse to mix.

*   **Water-in-Oil Emulsion:** (The most common in the oilfield). Microscopic droplets of water are perfectly suspended and trapped floating inside a continuous ocean of liquid crude oil. 
*   **Oil-in-Water Emulsion:** Microscopic droplets of oil are trapped floating inside a continuous ocean of water. 

If you take a bottle of pure oil and pure water and shake it violently, they will mix into a cloudy emulsion. But if you set the bottle down, gravity will instantly separate them back into two pure liquids in 60 seconds. This is an *unstable* emulsion.

Why do oilfield emulsions refuse to separate even after sitting in a tank for a week?
Because they are **stabilized by emulsifying agents**.

### The Emulsifying Agents
Crude oil is a complex chemical soup. It naturally contains heavy, sticky, polar molecules (like Asphaltenes and Resins) and microscopic solid particles (like clay dust and iron sulfide).
When the mixture is violently agitated (e.g., by blasting through the tiny wellhead choke at the speed of sound), these heavy molecules aggressively coat the outside of the microscopic water droplets.
They form a rigid, physical "skin" around the water droplet. When two water droplets bump into each other, this rigid skin physically prevents them from merging together (coalescing). Gravity is too weak to overcome this rigid skin, so the water droplets remain permanently suspended in the oil. 

## 2. Breaking the Emulsion: The Heater Treater

To separate the water and sell the oil, the production engineer must aggressively attack and destroy the rigid skin surrounding the water droplets. This is done using a **Heater Treater**.

A Heater Treater is a specialized, massive steel vessel that uses three forces simultaneously to break the emulsion: Chemicals, Heat, and Electricity.

### 1. Chemical Demulsifiers
Before the fluid even reaches the tank, engineers constantly inject specialized industrial chemicals (Demulsifiers) into the pipeline. These chemicals act like microscopic torpedoes. They are designed to aggressively attack the asphaltene "skin" surrounding the water droplets, neutralizing the chemical bonds and chemically stripping the skin away.

### 2. Heat (Thermal Coalescence)
Inside the Heater Treater is a massive steel fire-tube. A roaring natural gas flame burns inside the tube, heating the oil to temperatures of $120^\circ\text{F}$ to $160^\circ\text{F}$.
*   **Viscosity Reduction:** Heat drastically thins the crude oil. Because the oil is now thin like water, gravity can easily pull the heavy water droplets down through the oil.
*   **Kinetic Energy:** Heat makes the microscopic water droplets vibrate violently. They smash into each other with such intense kinetic force that they shatter the remaining chemical skin, allowing the droplets to merge (coalesce) into massive, heavy drops of water that instantly sink to the bottom of the tank.

### 3. Electrostatic Grids (The Final Polish)
If the crude oil is incredibly heavy and stubborn, heat and chemicals are not enough. 
The fluid is slowly pumped upward through a massive Electrostatic Grid inside the tank. The grid generates a violent, high-voltage electrical field (often 20,000 to 40,000 Volts). 
Because water is conductive and oil is an insulator, the massive electrical field violently rips the water droplets back and forth. The droplets slam into each other at high velocity, perfectly coalescing into massive drops and falling out of the pure oil.

## 3. The Wash Tank (Gun Barrel)

For older, low-volume oil fields where expensive Heater Treaters are not economical, engineers use a primitive but highly effective method called a **Wash Tank** (or "Gun Barrel").

1.  The massive tank is pre-filled halfway with hot, clean saltwater (the "Water Wash").
2.  The emulsion stream is piped into the absolute bottom of the tank, directly into the water.
3.  The emulsion slowly floats upward through the deep pool of hot water.
4.  As the microscopic water droplets in the emulsion physically rub against the massive pool of hot water, they are literally "washed" out of the oil. 
5.  Pure, clean oil floats to the very top of the tank and spills over into the sales line.
