# Oil and Gas Separation Equipment

The very first stop for the chaotic, turbulent mixture of oil, water, and gas exiting the well choke is the **Separator**. 

The separator is the absolute workhorse of the surface facility. Its job is simple in concept but incredibly difficult in practice: take a violently boiling, high-pressure foam and calmly split it into continuous, pure streams of Gas, Oil, and Water.

## 1. The Physics of Gravity Separation

Almost all oilfield separators rely on a single, fundamental law of physics: **Gravity (Density Difference)**.

*   Natural Gas is incredibly light. It wants to float to the top.
*   Liquid Oil is medium weight. It wants to sit in the middle.
*   Saltwater is heavy. It wants to sink to the bottom.

If you pour a mixture of the three into a glass and let it sit still for an hour, they will perfectly separate into three distinct layers. 

However, a separator cannot let the fluid sit still for an hour. It is constantly receiving 10,000 barrels of fluid a day. The fluid must flow continuously through the massive steel vessel. The separator must be engineered to artificially slow the fluid down just enough to allow gravity to do its job before the fluid exits the other side.

## 2. The Internal Anatomy of a Separator

A standard separator is a massive, high-pressure steel cylinder, either sitting horizontally on the ground or standing vertically.

To ensure the fluids separate efficiently within a 3-minute "retention time," the inside of the vessel is packed with mechanical obstacles.

1.  **The Inlet Diverter:** The fluid blasts into the vessel at extreme velocity. It violently smashes into a massive steel plate (the diverter). The sheer kinetic impact shatters the fluid momentum. The heavy liquids fall to the bottom pool, while the massive volume of bulk gas instantly breaks free and surges to the top of the vessel.
2.  **The Gravity Settling Section:** The massive empty space in the middle of the vessel. The liquid pool slowly flows horizontally from the inlet to the outlet. Because the vessel is massive, the fluid velocity slows to a crawl, giving gravity time to pull the heavy water drops down to the bottom and push the light oil drops to the top.
3.  **The Mist Extractor:** As the gas races along the ceiling of the vessel toward the gas exit valve, it carries millions of microscopic, airborne droplets of liquid oil with it (a mist). Before the gas exits, it is forced through a dense wire-mesh pad (the Mist Extractor). The tiny oil droplets smash into the wire, stick together, grow into large, heavy drops, and rain back down into the liquid pool.
4.  **The Vortex Breaker:** At the bottom liquid exit valves, as the heavy liquid drains out, it naturally wants to form a violent, spinning tornado (like a bathtub drain). This vortex will suck high-pressure gas directly down into the liquid pipeline, causing massive damage. A steel cross (the vortex breaker) is bolted over the drain to physically stop the fluid from spinning.

## 3. Two-Phase vs. Three-Phase Separation

*   **Two-Phase Separators:** Designed only to separate Liquid (a mixed stream of oil and water) from Gas. The liquid is dumped out the bottom, and the gas out the top. The oil and water are separated later in a specialized tank.
*   **Three-Phase Separators:** Designed to separate all three phases simultaneously. Inside the vessel, the liquid pool is split. The heavy water sinks to the bottom and is drained out a low valve. The lighter oil floats on top of the water, spills over a solid steel wall (a weir), and drains out a separate oil valve. 

## 4. Multi-Stage Separation

You cannot simply drop the pressure of a 5,000 psi fluid down to a 50 psi storage tank in one single step. 
If you drop the pressure violently in a single step, the thermodynamic shock causes massive amounts of the valuable, light liquid oil to instantly flash-boil into cheap, low-value gas. You literally vaporize your profits.

To maximize the volume of sellable liquid oil, engineers use **Multi-Stage Separation**.
The fluid is gently stepped down through a series of separators, each operating at a progressively lower pressure.

1.  **High-Pressure (HP) Separator:** Operates at 1,000 psi. Removes the massive bulk of the free gas gently.
2.  **Intermediate-Pressure (IP) Separator:** The remaining liquid is routed here and dropped to 300 psi. More gas gently bubbles out.
3.  **Low-Pressure (LP) Separator:** The liquid drops to 50 psi. The final bits of gas are removed.
4.  **Stock Tank:** The liquid finally rests at 0 psi (Atmospheric pressure).

By stepping the pressure down gently over three stages, the complex thermodynamics ensure that more of the intermediate hydrocarbon molecules stay trapped in the liquid phase, resulting in more barrels of highly valuable oil in the final storage tank.
