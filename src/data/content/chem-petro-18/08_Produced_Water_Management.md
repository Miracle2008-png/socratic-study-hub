# Produced Water Management

A young oil well produces 100% oil. A mature oil well might produce 5% oil and 95% saltwater. 

Globally, the oil and gas industry produces roughly **3 barrels of water for every 1 barrel of oil**. This water, originating from the deep underground aquifers, is called **Produced Water**.

Produced water is the single largest waste stream generated by the petroleum industry. It is highly toxic, hyper-saline, and incredibly expensive to manage. A production engineer often spends more time managing the waste water than they do managing the actual oil.

## 1. The Chemistry of Produced Water

Produced water is not fresh drinking water. It is a highly complex, hazardous chemical soup.

1.  **Extreme Salinity (TDS):** Ocean water contains roughly 35,000 parts per million (ppm) of Total Dissolved Solids (salt). Produced water can easily exceed **300,000 ppm**. It is a thick, syrupy brine so saturated with salt that if you spill it on the ground, it will permanently sterilize the soil, ensuring nothing will ever grow there again.
2.  **Heavy Metals and NORM:** The water has been sitting inside radioactive rock for 100 million years. It dissolves heavy metals (Lead, Arsenic, Barium) and Naturally Occurring Radioactive Materials (NORM), primarily Radium-226.
3.  **Dispersed Oil:** Even after passing through a massive separator, the water still contains microscopic, suspended droplets of crude oil (usually around 1,000 ppm of oil). 

## 2. Water Treating Equipment

Before you can dispose of the water, you must legally remove the dispersed oil droplets. Environmental regulations strictly dictate that water must contain less than 29 ppm of oil before it can be discharged into the ocean, and less than 50 ppm before it can be injected underground.

Because the microscopic oil droplets are too small for gravity to separate quickly, engineers use specialized machinery:

### 1. Skimmer Tanks
Massive, quiet settling tanks. The water is allowed to sit perfectly still for 24 hours. The microscopic oil droplets slowly float to the top and form a thin, rainbow sheen. A mechanical arm slowly skims the surface, scraping the oil off to be sold.

### 2. Hydrocyclones
Used heavily on offshore platforms where space is limited. A hydrocyclone has no moving parts. The oily water is pumped at extreme velocity into a cone-shaped tube. The fluid spins in a violent, high-speed tornado. The massive centrifugal forces push the heavy water to the outside wall of the cone, while the light oil droplets are squeezed perfectly into the dead-center of the tornado, where they are sucked out a small pipe at the top.

### 3. Induced Gas Flotation (IGF)
If the oil droplets are microscopic, engineers use an IGF unit. They violently pump millions of microscopic natural gas bubbles into the bottom of the water tank. As the tiny gas bubbles float upward, they collide with the microscopic oil droplets, stick to them, and act like tiny life-jackets, violently floating the oil to the surface as a thick, frothy foam, which is mechanically scraped away.

## 3. Water Disposal Methods

Once the water is stripped of oil, what do you do with 50,000 barrels of toxic brine every single day?

### 1. Re-Injection (Waterflooding)
The absolute best and most profitable use for produced water. The water is pumped through massive, high-pressure surface pumps and blasted straight back down into the edges of the oil reservoir. It maintains the reservoir pressure and physically sweeps more oil toward the producing wells. You solve the disposal problem and increase oil production simultaneously.

### 2. Saltwater Disposal Wells (SWD)
If the field does not need waterflooding, the company must drill a dedicated disposal well. They pump the toxic brine thousands of feet underground into a massive, useless, isolated rock formation (like a deep, porous limestone). 
*The Danger:* If you pump millions of barrels of water into a deep fault line at extreme pressures, you can literally lubricate the tectonic fault, causing it to slip and trigger man-made earthquakes (Induced Seismicity). This has become a massive regulatory issue in areas like Oklahoma and West Texas.

### 3. Surface Discharge
In onshore operations, surface discharge is almost universally illegal. However, on deep-water offshore platforms, once the water is polished to below 29 ppm of oil, it is simply dumped directly overboard into the open ocean, where the massive volume of the sea instantly dilutes the brine harmlessly.
