# Newtonian vs Non-Newtonian Fluids

Water, air, and gasoline behave in a predictable, intuitive way. They flow easily, and their "thickness" doesn't change unless you heat them up or cool them down.

However, ketchup, toothpaste, blood, mud, and paint behave very differently. Sometimes they act like solids, sometimes like liquids. To understand them, we must look at how fluids respond to shear stress.

## 1. Newton's Law of Viscosity

When you apply a shear stress ($\tau$) to a fluid—essentially dragging the top layer of fluid over the bottom layer—the fluid deforms at a certain rate. This is the **shear rate** ($\dot{\gamma}$, often written as $du/dy$).

Isaac Newton hypothesized a simple linear relationship between the applied stress and the resulting shear rate:
$$ \tau = \mu \dot{\gamma} $$

The constant of proportionality, $\mu$, is the **dynamic viscosity**.

If a fluid obeys this law, it is a **Newtonian fluid**.
*   The viscosity $\mu$ is a constant (at a given temperature and pressure).
*   If you push the fluid twice as hard, it flows twice as fast.
*   The viscosity does *not* depend on how hard you stir it or how fast it is flowing.

## 2. Non-Newtonian Fluids

If a fluid does *not* obey Newton's linear law, it is a **Non-Newtonian fluid**.

For these fluids, the "viscosity" is not a constant. It changes depending on how hard the fluid is being sheared. Because it is no longer a true constant, we call it the **Apparent Viscosity** ($\mu_{app}$).

$$ \tau = \mu_{app} \dot{\gamma} $$

Apparent viscosity can change based on two primary factors:
1.  **Shear Rate:** How hard you are pushing/stirring the fluid right now. (Time-Independent behavior).
2.  **Duration of Shear:** How long you have been pushing/stirring it. (Time-Dependent behavior).

## 3. Why do fluids behave this way?

Newtonian fluids (like water) are made of simple, small, perfectly spherical molecules. They roll over each other easily, regardless of how fast they are moving.

Non-Newtonian fluids are usually **complex mixtures or polymers**.
*   They might contain long, spaghetti-like polymer chains (like molten plastic).
*   They might be suspensions of solid particles in a liquid (like mud or blood).
*   They might be emulsions (like mayonnaise).

When these complex structures are subjected to shear stress, they can tangle, untangle, align with the flow, or clump together. This microscopic physical restructuring completely changes the macroscopic "thickness" of the fluid.
