# Atkinson and Miller Cycles

The standard Otto cycle (used in most petrol engines) inherently assumes that the compression ratio equals the expansion ratio. However, separating these two ratios allows for significant thermodynamic efficiency gains, which is the defining principle of the Atkinson and Miller cycles.

## 1. The Over-Expansion Principle

In a standard Otto engine, when the exhaust valve opens at the end of the power stroke, the gas pressure inside the cylinder is still significantly higher than atmospheric pressure. This pressure energy is wasted as it vents out the exhaust pipe (causing the loud exhaust noise).

If the power stroke could be longer than the compression stroke, the gas could continue to expand, doing more work on the piston until its pressure reached atmospheric. This "over-expansion" extracts more work from the same amount of fuel, increasing efficiency.

## 2. The Original Atkinson Cycle

James Atkinson invented a complex mechanical linkage in 1882 that allowed the piston to travel a shorter distance during the intake/compression strokes and a longer distance during the expansion/exhaust strokes.

- **Expansion Ratio > Compression Ratio.**
- **Thermodynamic Result:** More work extracted, higher thermal efficiency.
- **Drawback:** The mechanical linkage was heavy, complex, and prone to failure, limiting high-speed operation. The original mechanical Atkinson engine was a commercial failure.

## 3. The Modern "Atkinson Cycle" (via Valve Timing)

Modern hybrid vehicles (like the Toyota Prius) use a modified Otto engine that emulates the Atkinson thermodynamic cycle using clever valve timing, not complex linkages.

**How it works:**
1. The piston moves down, drawing in air.
2. The piston begins moving up for the compression stroke.
3. **Crucial step:** The intake valve is held open for the first portion of the compression stroke. Some of the air is pushed back out into the intake manifold.
4. The intake valve finally closes, and actual compression begins.

Because compression only occurs over a fraction of the piston's upward travel, the **effective compression ratio** is low. However, after combustion, the gas expands over the *entire* downward stroke, meaning the **expansion ratio is larger than the effective compression ratio**.

**Trade-offs:**
- **Pro:** Excellent thermal efficiency (up to 40-41% in modern hybrids).
- **Con:** Because some air is pushed back out, the engine breathes less air per cycle, resulting in a low specific power output (low horsepower for its size). This is why Atkinson engines are almost exclusively paired with electric motors in hybrid vehicles, where the electric motor provides the necessary low-end torque and acceleration.

## 4. The Miller Cycle

The Miller cycle (patented by Ralph Miller in the 1940s) is essentially the modern Atkinson cycle paired with a supercharger.

- The engine uses late intake valve closing to achieve a short compression stroke and long expansion stroke.
- To recover the power lost by pushing air back out, a supercharger forces pressurized air into the cylinder.
- **Result:** The efficiency benefits of over-expansion combined with high power output. This cycle is used in some modern turbocharged engines (e.g., specific Volkswagen/Audi and Mazda engines) to achieve excellent fuel economy under light loads while retaining high peak power.
