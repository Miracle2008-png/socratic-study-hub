# Internal Combustion Engine Enhancements

While the basic Otto and Diesel cycles define the theoretical limits, modern internal combustion engines employ advanced technologies to improve efficiency, increase specific power, and reduce emissions, pushing actual performance closer to those limits.

## 1. Turbocharging and Supercharging (Forced Induction)

The power output of an engine is directly limited by the mass of air it can ingest per cycle (since fuel can only be burned in proportion to available oxygen). Naturally aspirated engines rely on atmospheric pressure to push air into the cylinder during the intake stroke.

**Forced induction** uses a compressor to pack more air (at higher pressure and density) into the cylinder.

- **Supercharger:** The compressor is driven mechanically by a belt connected to the engine crankshaft. Provides immediate boost (no lag) but consumes engine power to operate, slightly reducing net efficiency.
- **Turbocharger:** The compressor is driven by a small turbine placed in the engine's exhaust stream. It recovers enthalpy and pressure energy from the exhaust gas that would otherwise be wasted. This significantly improves overall efficiency and specific power, though it suffers from "turbo lag" (delay while the exhaust spins up the turbine).

Because compressing air raises its temperature (which reduces density and promotes engine knock in petrol engines), forced induction is almost always paired with an **intercooler** (charge air cooler) to cool the compressed air before it enters the engine.

## 2. Variable Valve Timing (VVT) and Lift

In traditional engines, the camshaft is mechanically locked to the crankshaft, meaning the intake and exhaust valves open and close at fixed points in the cycle regardless of engine speed.

However, fluid dynamics dictate that the optimal valve timing depends heavily on engine RPM. At high speeds, valves need to open earlier and stay open longer to allow momentum to cram air into the cylinders. At low speeds, this same timing would cause air to flow backward out of the intake.

**VVT systems** dynamically alter the phasing of the camshaft relative to the crankshaft. Advanced systems also vary the valve **lift** (how far the valve opens). This optimizes volumetric efficiency across the entire RPM range, broadening the torque curve and improving fuel economy.

## 3. Direct Injection (GDI)

Traditional petrol engines use port fuel injection (PFI), where fuel is sprayed into the intake manifold before the intake valve.

**Gasoline Direct Injection (GDI)** injects highly pressurized fuel directly into the combustion chamber (similar to a diesel engine).
- **Advantages:** The evaporating fuel cools the air charge inside the cylinder, increasing density and suppressing auto-ignition (knock). This allows for a higher compression ratio, directly increasing thermal efficiency. It also enables highly precise fuel metering and stratified charge combustion (running a very lean mixture at part-throttle).

## 4. Cylinder Deactivation

At highway cruising speeds, a large V8 engine may only need 30% of its maximum power. Running 8 cylinders at low load is inefficient due to severe "pumping losses" (the engine expends energy pulling air past a nearly closed throttle plate).

**Cylinder deactivation** mechanically disables the valves and halts fuel injection to half the cylinders (e.g., turning a V8 into a V4). The active cylinders must operate at a higher load (wider throttle opening) to maintain vehicle speed, significantly reducing pumping losses and improving highway fuel economy.
