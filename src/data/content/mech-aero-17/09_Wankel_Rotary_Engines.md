# Wankel Rotary Engines

The Wankel rotary engine is a unique type of internal combustion engine that eliminates reciprocating pistons entirely. Instead of pistons moving up and down, a triangular rotor spins eccentrically within a specially shaped housing. Invented by Felix Wankel in the 1950s, it achieved fame primarily through Mazda's sports cars (RX-7, RX-8).

## 1. Operating Principle

The Wankel engine consists of a Reuleaux triangle-shaped rotor orbiting within an epitrochoid-shaped housing.

- As the rotor spins eccentrically (via a geared eccentric shaft), its three apexes remain in constant contact with the housing wall.
- This creates three isolated working chambers between the rotor and the housing.
- As the rotor turns, the volume of these chambers continuously expands and contracts.
- The housing has intake and exhaust ports cut into its sides (no valves are needed).

In one full revolution of the rotor, each of the three chambers undergoes the full four-cycle process:
1. **Intake:** The chamber expands as it passes the intake port, drawing in air-fuel.
2. **Compression:** The chamber volume contracts as it moves toward the spark plugs.
3. **Power:** The spark plugs fire, combustion expands the gas, pushing the rotor and spinning the eccentric shaft.
4. **Exhaust:** The chamber contracts as it passes the exhaust port, expelling the gas.

Because there are three chambers, there are **three power pulses for every one revolution of the rotor**. The output shaft spins three times faster than the rotor, resulting in one power pulse per output shaft revolution (equivalent to a two-cylinder two-stroke or a four-cylinder four-stroke).

## 2. Advantages

- **Incredible Smoothness:** Because it only has rotational motion (no heavy pistons changing direction thousands of times a minute), the Wankel is essentially vibration-free and can rev to very high RPMs effortlessly.
- **High Power-to-Weight/Volume:** The engine is exceptionally compact and lightweight for the amount of power it produces.
- **Simplicity:** Far fewer moving parts than a piston engine. No valves, camshafts, rocker arms, or timing belts.

## 3. Disadvantages

Despite its elegant kinematics, the Wankel suffers from severe thermodynamic and mechanical limitations that ultimately doomed its widespread adoption:

- **Poor Thermal Efficiency:** The combustion chamber is long, narrow, and flat. This provides a very high surface-area-to-volume ratio, causing massive heat loss to the chamber walls. The flame front also struggles to reach the far ends of the chamber, resulting in incomplete combustion. Consequently, rotary engines have notoriously poor fuel economy.
- **Emissions:** Incomplete combustion results in high unburned hydrocarbon emissions. Furthermore, the apex seals (the seals at the tips of the rotor) require direct oil injection for lubrication, meaning the engine is designed to burn oil, increasing particulate emissions.
- **Apex Seal Wear:** The seals at the tips of the rotor undergo immense stress as they sweep across varying angles of the epitrochoid housing, passing over intake/exhaust ports and undergoing massive temperature gradients. Apex seal failure is the most common death of a rotary engine.

While largely abandoned for mass-market automotive use due to strict emissions and fuel economy standards, its high power-to-weight ratio keeps it relevant in niche applications like experimental aviation, drones, and motorsports.
