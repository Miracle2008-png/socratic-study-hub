# Nuclear Power Cycles

Nuclear power plants provide a massive percentage of the world's baseload, carbon-free electricity. From a thermodynamic perspective, a nuclear power plant is simply a Rankine cycle. The fundamental difference lies in the heat source: instead of a chemical combustion boiler, heat is generated by the controlled nuclear fission of Uranium-235 or Plutonium-239.

## 1. The Pressurized Water Reactor (PWR)

The PWR is the most common reactor design globally. It employs a two-loop system, separating the radioactive primary coolant from the power generation cycle.

- **Primary Loop:** Water flows through the reactor core, absorbing the heat of fission. To prevent this water from boiling (which would alter its density and negatively affect its role as a neutron moderator), it is kept under immense pressure (approx. 15.5 MPa or 2250 psi). The water exits the core at around 315°C.
- **Steam Generator:** The hot, pressurized primary water flows through tubes inside a steam generator. Heat is transferred to the secondary loop.
- **Secondary Loop (Rankine Cycle):** Water in the secondary loop (at lower pressure, ~6 MPa) boils into saturated steam, drives a steam turbine, is condensed, and pumped back.
- **Advantage:** The secondary steam that drives the turbine is entirely non-radioactive, simplifying maintenance of the turbine and condenser.

## 2. The Boiling Water Reactor (BWR)

The BWR is simpler, utilizing a single-loop system.

- Water flows through the reactor core and is allowed to boil directly within the reactor vessel at about 7 MPa (1000 psi) and 285°C.
- The resulting steam goes through moisture separators and dryers within the top of the reactor vessel and is piped directly to the steam turbine.
- **Advantage:** Simpler design, eliminates the large and expensive steam generators, slightly better thermodynamic efficiency due to the elimination of the $\Delta T$ across the steam generator.
- **Disadvantage:** The steam expanding through the turbine is slightly radioactive (due to short-lived nitrogen-16 isotopes activated in the core). The turbine hall must be heavily shielded, and maintenance requires rigorous radiation protocols.

## 3. Thermodynamic Limitations

Because standard light-water reactors (PWRs and BWRs) use water as the coolant, their maximum temperature is limited by water's critical point and the extreme pressures required to prevent boiling at high temperatures.

Operating at a maximum cycle temperature of roughly 285°C to 315°C strictly limits the Carnot efficiency. Typical nuclear plants achieve thermal efficiencies of **33-34%** (compared to 40-45% for modern fossil fuel plants). They make up for this low thermal efficiency through the incredibly high energy density of nuclear fuel and very low fuel costs.

## 4. Advanced Reactor Cycles (Generation IV)

To achieve higher efficiencies and closed fuel cycles, Generation IV reactor designs shift away from light water to coolants that can operate at much higher temperatures without requiring extreme pressures.

- **Liquid Metal Fast Breeder Reactors (LMFBR):** Use liquid sodium or lead as coolant. Can operate at ~550°C at near-atmospheric pressure. Coupled to an intermediate heat exchanger and a high-temperature steam Rankine cycle.
- **High-Temperature Gas-Cooled Reactors (HTGR):** Use helium gas as coolant, operating at 750°C to 950°C. These can drive a Rankine steam cycle, or more efficiently, drive a **closed-cycle Brayton gas turbine** directly using the hot helium, achieving efficiencies near 50%.
